## Overview
Systematically audits every competitor's messaging across all public channels — website, ads, content, PR, events, social, G2 reviews — to identify white space in the narrative landscape, detect messaging pivots before they reach buyers, and engineer a category frame that structurally boxes competitors into unfavorable positions. Use this when you're losing deals because buyers parrot competitor language, when your differentiation sounds exactly like your top competitor's, when a rival just raised a round and started dominating share of voice, or when you're preparing to reposition, raise a round, or defend a Gartner/Forrester evaluation cycle.

**Section 1: Competitive Messaging Fingerprint — One Per Competitor**

For each competitor, extract and document:

*Primary Narrative Claim:* Their single core claim — the story they tell about why they exist and why buyers need them. Not their tagline, but the underlying narrative (e.g., "The old way of doing X is broken because of Y; we invented Z to fix it").

*Problem Frame:* How they define the problem. This is critical — whoever defines the problem owns the solution. Document their exact problem language: what they call the pain, who suffers from it, and what causes it.

*Category Name:* What they call the product category. Do they use an established analyst category name, a coined term, or an operational description? Map this: companies that coin category names are playing offense; companies that use analyst category names are playing defense.

*Hero Metric:* The single number or outcome they repeat most. This reveals their core value hypothesis (e.g., "40% reduction in time-to-hire," "$2.1M recovered in revenue leakage," "3x pipeline from existing accounts").

*Primary Buyer Persona Language:* The job titles, emotional language, and professional anxieties they speak to. Extract verbatim phrases from their homepage and G2 reviews that reveal how deeply they understand their buyer's psychology.

*Proof Mechanism:* How they back their claims. Customer logos? ROI studies? Industry analyst quotes? G2 badges? Case study metrics? The proof mechanism reveals their maturity and their buyer's risk tolerance.

*Emotional Hook:* The underlying emotional driver they're selling to. Fear (of being left behind, of compliance failure, of losing to competitors)? Aspiration (of becoming the person who transformed the company)? Belonging (of being part of a category of sophisticated buyers)? Status (of using what the Fortune 500 uses)?

*Narrative Maturity Score (1-5):* Rate how cohesive, specific, and defensible their narrative is. A score of 5 means every channel speaks the same story and it's impossible to confuse them with anyone else. A score of 1 means they sound like everyone else in the category.

**Section 2: Narrative White Space Analysis**

Using the messaging fingerprints as input, map the competitive narrative landscape across five dimensions:

*Dimension 1: Problem Ownership*
Create a 2x2 grid: X-axis = Specificity of problem definition (generic → highly specific); Y-axis = Emotional intensity (rational/functional → emotionally resonant). Plot each competitor. Identify which quadrant is unclaimed. Unclaimed quadrant = narrative white space.

*Dimension 2: Buyer Persona Coverage*
Map which buyer personas each competitor speaks to most. Identify personas that every competitor talks past — this is the beachhead persona your narrative can own without direct competition. B2B SaaS companies systematically over-invest in speaking to the economic buyer and under-invest in the practitioner persona (the person doing the daily work); identify if this gap exists in your category.

*Dimension 3: Outcome Claim Territory*
Categorize competitor outcome claims into: Revenue impact / Cost reduction / Risk reduction / Speed/efficiency / Compliance/governance / Team performance / Customer experience. Map claim density: categories with 3+ competitors making the same claim are noise; categories with 0-1 competitors making claims are narrative opportunities.

*Dimension 4: Time Horizon Framing*
Do competitors position around immediate ROI (quarter-over-quarter gains), medium-term transformation (12-18 month initiatives), or long-term competitive moat (multi-year strategic advantage)? Most B2B SaaS companies cluster around immediate ROI because it's easier to sell. The company that successfully owns the long-term strategic narrative is extremely difficult to displace from shortlists.

*Dimension 5: Proof Source Authority*
Map where competitors get their proof: customer quotes / case study data / analyst validation / independent research / community benchmarks / usage data. Identify the proof source type that no competitor owns — owning a distinctive proof type (e.g., "the only platform with 10,000+ anonymized benchmarks from your peer companies") creates narrative credibility that can't be replicated quickly.

**Section 3: Category Definition Intelligence**

Analyze the category definition wars being fought in your space:

*Step 1: Extract Category Definitions*
For each competitor, extract their implicit or explicit category definition. A category definition has three components: (a) the type of buyer it serves, (b) the type of problem it solves, and (c) the mechanism by which it solves it. Map all three for each competitor.

*Step 2: Identify the Category Definition Battle*
Are competitors converging on the same definition (commoditization signal) or diverging (category fragmentation signal)? Convergence means buyers are treating everyone as substitutes — dangerous for everyone but the market share leader. Divergence means there's an opportunity to define a sub-category that you can own exclusively.

*Step 3: Category Definition Options*
Propose three category definition options for your company:
- **The Aggressive Option:** A category definition that excludes your top 1-2 competitors by definition. Requires strong conviction and differentiated architecture.
- **The Expansion Option:** A broader category definition that captures new buyer personas and use cases your competitors haven't addressed, making your TAM narrative more compelling.
- **The Sub-Category Option:** A highly specific category definition that positions you as the only serious solution for a specific high-value use case, enabling dominance at the expense of total addressable market size.

For each option: the category name, the 2-sentence category definition you'd use in analyst and investor conversations, the competitors it neutralizes, and the risks.

Specifically watch for: new frameworks they're naming (frameworks become narrative anchors), new personas they're producing content for, new problem language in article titles. Monitoring cadence: bi-weekly. Tools: Feedly, Crayon/Klue, or manual RSS aggregation.

**Section 5: Counter-Messaging Playbook**

For each competitor's top 3 narrative claims, design a counter-messaging framework using the "Reframe Without Naming" technique:

*Reframe Without Naming Technique:*
Never mention competitors by name in market-facing messaging. Instead, use category language that makes their approach sound dated, limited, or misaligned with buyer sophistication. The goal is to make the buyer's own critical thinking eliminate the competitor — not your explicit argument.

*Framework for Each Counter-Message:*

**Step 1 — Competitor's Claim:** State exactly what they claim.

**Step 2 — Why It Resonates:** Identify why buyers find this claim compelling. Don't dismiss it — understand the legitimate buyer anxiety it addresses. Counter-messages that ignore why a claim works always fail.

**Step 3 — The Hidden Limitation:** Identify the real limitation of their approach that the claim obscures. This is usually a tradeoff they're hiding (e.g., "AI-powered" often means "AI outputs require significant human review and correction," which is hidden behind the efficiency claim).

**Step 4 — The Reframe:** Construct a positive statement that positions your approach as solving what their claim promises but doesn't deliver. Structure: "[The thing buyers want] requires [what we do], not just [a softened version of their claim]." Example: "Accurate revenue forecasting requires a model trained on your specific deal patterns, not just AI trained on generic sales data."

**Step 5 — Proof Requirement:** Identify what proof you need to make the reframe credible. Without specific, verifiable proof, reframes sound like defensive marketing.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Competitive messaging audit reveals our differentiation is the same as two competitors' — everything sounds identical.**
Solution: This is a symptom of over-indexing on feature differentiation and under-investing in narrative architecture. When features converge, narrative divergence is the only remaining lever. Restart from the Problem Frame dimension: competitors who have identical feature sets almost always describe the problem differently. Find the problem definition that makes your product's mechanism uniquely qualified to solve it, even if the outcomes are similar. Use the "Reframe Without Naming" technique at the problem layer, not the solution layer.

**Problem: We selected a narrative white space but internal stakeholders keep reverting to old messaging because "that's what buyers expect to hear."**
Solution: Narrative change requires proof before adoption. Run a 60-day paid social test with 3 ad variants: current messaging, white space messaging, and a hybrid. Share CTR and conversion data weekly with internal stakeholders. Data removes opinion. If the white space narrative outperforms by 15%+, executive buy-in accelerates; if it underperforms, you've learned something important before re-platforming your entire website.

**Problem: Primary competitor keeps copying our exact messaging within 30-60 days of us publishing it.**
Solution: This is actually a validation signal — you're winning the narrative war by setting the agenda, which means they're following you. Accelerate your publishing cadence and create proprietary frameworks, named methodologies, and original research that they can't replicate by paraphrasing. Trademarked frameworks (like your own "Revenue Credibility Model" or "Narrative Maturity Score") become defensible narrative assets that competitors can't legally copy verbatim. File trademark on your category name and key frameworks if your legal budget allows.
