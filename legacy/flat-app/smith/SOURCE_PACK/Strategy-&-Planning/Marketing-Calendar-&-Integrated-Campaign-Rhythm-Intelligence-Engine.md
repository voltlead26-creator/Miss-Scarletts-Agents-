**Difficulty:** Advanced | **Time:** 30 min | **Tags:** strategy, planning, campaign-orchestration, gtm, b2b, enterprise, annual-planning, campaign-calendar, demand-gen, leadership

## Overview
Builds a complete, revenue-synchronized annual marketing calendar that coordinates every campaign, content theme, event, product launch, and channel activation across your entire GTM motion. Use it when you're entering annual planning season, after a strategy refresh, or when your campaigns feel disconnected and reactive rather than intentional and compounding.

## MODULE 6: Q4 REVENUE ACCELERATION PLAYBOOK

Q4 is not like other quarters. Model it explicitly.

**BUDGET FLUSH STRATEGY (Oct-Nov):**
- Target: Buyers with unspent budget seeking to commit before year-end
- Signal: Companies 90%+ through their fiscal year; procurement conversations accelerating
- Campaigns: Urgency messaging focused on implementation timing ("start in Q4, see results in Q1"), ROI before year-end, "lock in current pricing" if applicable
- SDR priority: Stalled deals 60+ days with known budget — acceleration sequences

**HOLIDAY NAVIGATION (Nov-Dec):**
- Week of US Thanksgiving: Pause cold outreach; send value-add content to warm leads only
- Dec 15 - Jan 2: Dramatically reduce volume; prioritize pipeline that can actually close
- Exception: Decision-maker-only sequences for deal progression; executive outreach for deals in legal/procurement

**JANUARY PIPELINE SEEDING (runs in parallel with Q4):**
- Problem: Q1 is always weak because Q4 was heads-down closing
- Solution: Q4 content calendar includes "new year" positioning to warm 2026 pipeline
- Tactic: Publish the annual benchmark report/predictions piece in November so it ranks in January
- Tactic: Load SDR sequences for January drip in December — launch Jan 2 without delay

**Q4 PIPELINE COVERAGE CHECK:**
- Target pipeline needed to close $X new ARR in Q4: $[X] (factor in seasonal close rates, which are typically [X]% vs. annual average)
- Are you on track? Pipeline coverage ratio today vs. where you need to be

## Success Metrics

- **Calendar adoption rate:** % of planned campaigns launched within 1 week of scheduled date (target: >90%)
- **Pipeline coverage:** Quarterly pipeline sourced vs. target (target: within ±10%)
- **Campaign narrative coherence:** Internal marketing team can explain how each campaign connects to the annual theme without referencing a document (yes/no audit)
- **Audience fatigue score:** Email unsubscribe rate (target: <0.3% per send); paid ad frequency score (target: <4 impressions/week per person)
- **Cross-channel coordination:** % of campaigns where SDR sequences, paid, and email are synchronized within the same 48-hour launch window (target: >85%)
- **Q1 pipeline seeded in Q4:** Pipeline sourced in Q1 that was first touched in Q4 (target: >30% of Q1 pipeline has Q4 first touch — proves calendar continuity)
- **Quarterly review quality:** CMO can recite next quarter's priority campaign, target segment, hero asset, and pipeline target from memory (operational internalization test)

## Integration Tips

- **HubSpot:** Build the annual campaign calendar as HubSpot Campaigns — one parent campaign per quarter, child campaigns per activation. This auto-populates the campaign influence dashboard and ties every asset, email, and ad to revenue attribution without manual tagging.
- **Asana / Monday.com / Notion:** Import the calendar as a project with dependencies: "Paid campaign launch" cannot start until "Asset design complete" — dependency tracking prevents the most common execution failure (campaigns launch before assets are ready).
- **Google Sheets:** Build the pipeline math module as a connected spreadsheet — input your conversion rates by channel and it auto-calculates required MQLs, SALs, and opportunities per campaign. Link to HubSpot via a native connector so actuals update weekly.
- **Salesforce:** Map every campaign to a Salesforce Campaign with stage, budget, and expected pipeline values. This creates the bi-directional pipeline attribution that lets you prove which calendar investments generated revenue — critical for budget defense conversations.
- **Slack:** Create a #campaign-calendar Slack channel with weekly auto-posts from HubSpot or Asana showing what's launching that week, what's in progress, and what's behind schedule. The visibility alone prevents the most common failure: campaigns that miss their window because no one was tracking.
- **Gong:** Connect call recordings to campaign periods — when you review SDR calls, you can hear whether campaign messaging is landing and adjust the calendar in real-time rather than waiting for the quarterly review.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem:** The calendar output is full but feels like a list of tactics, not a coherent strategy — campaigns don't connect to each other.
**Solution:** Run Module 1 (Annual Narrative Architecture) first as a standalone prompt. Get the golden thread and seasonal themes approved before building any campaigns. Then re-run the full prompt with the narrative constraints filled in. The difference is profound: instead of "Q1 Email Campaign," you get "Q1: Position us as the company that transforms how legal teams work — benchmark report → webinar → customer roundtable — all laddering to the same theme."

**Problem:** The Q4 playbook conflicts with reality — the sales team says buyers go dark after November 15 in our industry.
**Solution:** Add this constraint to the prompt: "In our specific market, [describe your buyer's holiday blackout window]. Redesign the Q4 playbook with this constraint — specifically, what pipeline must be sourced and in late-stage by November 15, and what activities are actually worth running December 1–31 vs. when to redirect budget to Q1 seeding." The model will give you a Q4 calendar calibrated to your actual buyer behavior, not a generic SaaS template.

**Problem:** The cross-channel activation sequence creates conflicts — SDRs are already reaching out to the same accounts the paid campaign is targeting, creating a messy experience.
**Solution:** Add the following to Module 3: "Build a contact-level orchestration rule: define which motion takes priority (SDR-led vs. marketing-led) by account tier. Tier 1 accounts (top 100 named) are SDR-led — suppress from paid. Tier 2 (next 500) are marketing-led ABM — suppress from cold SDR outreach but allow warm follow-up. Tier 3 (all others) are fully programmatic." This creates the coordination framework that prevents the collision, which is the #1 calendar execution failure in B2B marketing.
