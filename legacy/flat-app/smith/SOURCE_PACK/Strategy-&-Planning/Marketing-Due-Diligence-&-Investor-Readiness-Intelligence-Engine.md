### MODULE 2: GROWTH REPEATABILITY & PREDICTABILITY ANALYSIS

Investors pay premiums for companies with PREDICTABLE, REPEATABLE growth engines. Assess along 5 dimensions:

**2A. Channel Concentration Risk**
- Current HHI (Herfindahl-Hirschman Index) of pipeline across channels — calculate and interpret
- Benchmark: Healthy = no single channel >40% of pipeline; Risk = >60% from one channel
- Specific risk: if [top channel] underperforms 30%, what happens to pipeline?
- Recommendation: Which 1-2 channels should be built out before close to reduce concentration risk?

**2B. Pipeline Coverage Consistency**
- Analyze 12-month pipeline coverage trend — is 3x coverage maintained, improving, or degrading?
- Flag any quarter where coverage dropped below 2.5x and explain the cause
- Investor question this answers: "Is your pipeline creation engine consistent or lumpy?"

**2C. Cohort-Based CAC Trends**
- Is CAC improving (efficiency gains) or increasing (scaling inefficiency) across the last 4 quarterly cohorts?
- If CAC is increasing: identify whether it's channel saturation, ICP expansion, or team scaling cost
- Provide investor explanation: "CAC is rising because X, and here's why that's strategically correct"

**2D. Lead Quality Consistency**
- Is MQL → SQL conversion stable or declining quarter over quarter?
- Declining conversion = marketing is growing volume at expense of quality = red flag
- Identify whether the issue is lead definition drift, channel mix shift, or ICP expansion

**2E. ICP Fit & Retention Correlation**
- Are customers acquired from the highest-CAC channels retaining better or worse than low-CAC channels?
- Ideal narrative: "Our highest-retention customers come from [channel], and we're increasing investment there"
- Risk flag: "We're optimizing for CAC but ignoring that [channel]'s customers churn at 2x the rate"

**Repeatability Score:** Rate the overall growth engine 1-10 with investor-facing justification.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem:** CAC data is inconsistent across finance, sales, and marketing — each team has a different number, and they can't agree before the investor meeting.
**Solution:** Create a single "CAC Methodology Card" (one page) that defines exactly what's included in CAC: fully-loaded marketing headcount cost + all marketing program spend + SDR compensation if marketing-owned + martech tools allocation, divided by new logos in the period. Have CEO, CFO, and CMO sign off on this single definition before any investor meeting. When asked, cite the methodology card: "Our CAC is $X using our documented methodology, which we're happy to walk through — it excludes professional services and one-time onboarding costs, which is consistent with how Vista Equity and Thoma Bravo define CAC in their SaaS benchmarks."

**Problem:** NRR of 118% looks weak when a competitor claims 135% — investors are asking why the gap.
**Solution:** Never accept a raw NRR comparison without segmentation. First, break your NRR by cohort vintage and ICP segment — if enterprise-only NRR is 131% and SMB drags the blended figure to 118%, the story changes dramatically. Second, ask investors whether the competitor's NRR is audited or self-reported — in many growth equity processes, NRR is calculated differently (some include resurrected churned customers, some don't). Request the competitor's methodology if investors use it as a benchmark. Third, model the "path to 125%+" by showing that the product roadmap's next 2 features are specifically designed to trigger expansion (usage-based billing, seat expansion triggers, or module attach rates) — shift the conversation from current NRR to NRR trajectory.

**Problem:** Investors are asking for a churn cohort table and the data in the CRM is not clean enough to build one accurately.
**Solution:** Stop trying to build it from scratch in the CRM under time pressure. Instead: (1) Pull raw subscription start/end dates from the billing system (Stripe, Chargebee, or Zuora) — this is cleaner than CRM stage history; (2) Build the cohort table in Excel/Google Sheets using the billing system as source of truth; (3) Document the methodology clearly ("Churn defined as subscription cancellation date in Chargebee, not CRM close date — methodology available in data room"); (4) If there are still data quality gaps, acknowledge them proactively: "Our cohort table covers Q1 2022–present with high confidence. Prior periods are reconstructed from billing records with ±2% margin of error — we've noted this in the methodology documentation." Investors respect honesty about data limitations far more than clean-looking numbers that don't reconcile to the P&L.
