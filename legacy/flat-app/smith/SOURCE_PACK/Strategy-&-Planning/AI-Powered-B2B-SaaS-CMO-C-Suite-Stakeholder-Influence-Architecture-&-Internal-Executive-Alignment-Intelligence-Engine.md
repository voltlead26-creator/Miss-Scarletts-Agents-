## Integration Tips

- **Salesforce/HubSpot**: Pull the stakeholder-specific metrics directly from your CRM for the CFO's Marketing P&L — pipeline sourced, CAC by channel, and win rate by lead source. Use saved reports that update automatically so you can send them monthly without manual work.
- **Gong/Chorus**: Mine call recordings from won and lost deals to find specific quotes from buyers about why they chose you. These are your most powerful proof points in CRO conversations because they come from actual customers, not marketing reports.
- **LinkedIn**: Track each C-suite stakeholder's LinkedIn activity — what they post about, what they engage with, what they share. This reveals their priorities in their own words and gives you language to use in 1:1 conversations that shows you understand what they care about.
- **Notion/Confluence**: Build a "Stakeholder Intelligence Hub" — a private doc with each executive's OKRs, their known opinions of marketing, your previous 1:1 notes, and your influence action log. Update it after every meaningful interaction. This becomes your institutional memory as a new CMO.
- **Google Slides / Pitch**: Create a "Marketing Metrics Library" — a bank of pre-built slides for each stakeholder type (CFO version, CRO version, CEO version) that you update monthly. When you need to present, pull from the library instead of building from scratch.
- **Slack**: Set up a private channel with your direct reports called #c-suite-signals. Share anything you learn about stakeholder perceptions — comments in all-hands, hallway conversations, second-hand reports from SDRs about what sales leadership is saying. Surface patterns early.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: The CFO still doesn't believe the Marketing P&L after 2 months of sharing it.**
Solution: Ask the CFO directly: "What would the data need to show for you to believe marketing is delivering measurable ROI?" Document their exact answer and build your next proof point to match that specific standard. Sometimes the issue is methodology disagreement, not data quality — ask if they'd like to co-design the attribution methodology with your marketing ops team. CFOs trust models they help build.

**Problem: The CRO is actively undermining marketing in all-hands meetings and you can't seem to win them over.**
Solution: Stop trying to convert them through data. Some CROs are territorial by nature and see marketing pipeline as a threat to their narrative (they want all pipeline to be sales-sourced). Switch tactics: instead of arguing about MQL quality, propose joint ownership — "Let's stop counting marketing-sourced vs. sales-sourced pipeline separately. Let's track total revenue and measure what combination of marketing and sales activity produces the best outcomes." This removes the adversarial dynamic by eliminating the competition.

**Problem: CEO gives positive signals in 1:1s but then describes marketing narrowly in board meetings.**
Solution: The CEO is using different vocabulary in different contexts — they may genuinely believe in what you're doing but haven't internalized your preferred framing. Ask for 15 minutes before the next board meeting to align on "how we talk about marketing" with the board. Bring your three-sentence board narrative and ask: "Does this feel accurate to you?" CEOs who are open to it will start using your language. Those who resist may signal a deeper misalignment worth addressing directly.
