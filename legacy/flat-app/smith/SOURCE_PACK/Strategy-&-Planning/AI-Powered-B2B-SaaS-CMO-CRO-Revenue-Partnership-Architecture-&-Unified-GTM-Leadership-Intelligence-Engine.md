## Overview
The CMO-CRO relationship is the most important—and most fragile—executive dyad in B2B SaaS. When it works, marketing and sales move as a unified revenue engine. When it breaks, every QBR becomes a blame session, pipeline suffers, and the board loses confidence in the revenue team. This engine helps you design a formal, durable CMO-CRO partnership with shared accountability structures, joint revenue governance, unified metrics, and a conflict resolution protocol that keeps you aligned even when the numbers miss. Use it when you're a new CMO or CRO joining a company, when marketing-sales tension has reached the board's attention, when you're building a RevOps function for the first time, or when you're preparing to present revenue leadership as a unified front to investors.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: The CRO agrees to the Charter, but sales behavior doesn't change — leads still go un-worked for 5+ days.**
Solution: The Charter without enforcement mechanisms is theater. Add automatic SLA tracking to the CRM with manager-level alerts at 24 hours and CMO-level alerts at 48 hours. Bring SLA compliance data to every weekly standup — not as an accusation, but as a shared ops metric. If SDR compliance is below 80%, it's a RevOps problem to solve together, not a blame conversation.

**Problem: Marketing keeps hitting MQL volume targets but the CRO still says quality is low — it feels like a moving target.**
Solution: This is a funnel definition failure, not a relationship failure. The binary acceptance criteria in Section 2 are doing the work here: "Sales must accept or reject every MQL within 24 hours with a specific reason code from the approved list." If the CRO can't point to specific, binary criteria you're failing, then "quality is low" is an opinion. Work backward from the most recent 20 rejected MQLs — what do they have in common? That's your ICP calibration session agenda.

**Problem: The CMO and CRO are aligned, but the RevOps team reports to the CRO and keeps building dashboards that favor the sales perspective.**
Solution: Acknowledge this openly — it's structural, not personal. The solution is a jointly-owned RevOps dashboard that CMO and CRO both review before it's published. Neither leader can edit it unilaterally. For high-stakes board presentations, have RevOps present the methodology to both CMO and CRO simultaneously before the numbers are finalized. If the bias persists, raise the RevOps reporting structure as an agenda item for the CEO — not as a power grab, but as a data governance issue.
