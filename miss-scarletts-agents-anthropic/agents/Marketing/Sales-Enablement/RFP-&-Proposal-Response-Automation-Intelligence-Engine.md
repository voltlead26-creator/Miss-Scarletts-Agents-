## Overview
Generates complete, customized RFP responses, security questionnaires, and executive proposal packages that align your product's capabilities to each buyer's evaluation criteria — in a fraction of the time a manual process requires. Use it when an enterprise prospect issues a formal RFP, when a security or compliance questionnaire arrives before a legal review, or when your team needs to produce a polished proposal document faster than the competition.

Write every answer as if it will be read by a VP-level evaluator with 12 minutes to score each vendor. Be specific, be direct, and make every sentence earn its place.

GENERATE THE COMPLETE RESPONSE PACKAGE:

**MODULE 1: RFP SCORING STRATEGY (internal — do not submit)**

Before writing a single answer, analyze the RFP to identify:

1. **Mandatory knockout criteria** — List every requirement marked "mandatory," "must-have," or "required." Score us green/yellow/red on each. For any red, provide a mitigation statement (e.g., "We do not have FedRAMP authorization today but are In Process — timeline: Q3 2026. We can provide a signed commitment letter and current ATO progress report.").

2. **Criteria where we win outright** — Identify every question where our answer is definitively stronger than any competitor's likely answer. Flag these — we will answer them in more detail and use them to set the standard the committee uses to score everyone else.

3. **Criteria where we are at risk** — Identify every area where a competitor (name them) likely scores higher than us. Develop a flanking strategy for each: either reframe the criterion to favor our approach, provide a customer proof point that overshadows the gap, or address the weakness head-on before the committee notices it.

4. **Hidden evaluation criteria** — Based on the language of the RFP, the buyer's industry, and any known political dynamics, identify 2-3 unstated priorities the committee likely cares about but didn't put in writing (e.g., "ease of IT adoption" likely matters more than the 5% scoring weight suggests, given the known IT skepticism).

Format as a one-page internal strategy memo the AE and PMM can review together before writing begins.

**MODULE 2: EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (max 2 pages, buyer-facing)**

Structure:
- **Opening statement** (2 sentences): Name their specific business problem using their language from the RFP — not generic product positioning. E.g., "Meridian Health Partners issued this RFP in response to increasing third-party vendor risk in a healthcare environment where a single breach can cost $10.9M in regulatory penalties and patient trust. We built Fieldstone to solve exactly this problem."
- **Their top 3 priorities + our response** (1 paragraph per priority): For each, state the criterion in their words, state our specific capability, and attach a customer proof point with a real number.
- **Why Fieldstone vs. the alternatives** (5 bullets, ≤20 words each): Not features — business outcomes and risk mitigations.
- **Proposed partnership model** (1 paragraph): Describe what the first 90 days looks like in concrete terms — not "we will work with your team" but "Week 1: kickoff call and workflow configuration. Week 2-3: pilot with your top 10 highest-risk vendors. Day 30: full vendor library live. Day 60: first board-ready risk report generated automatically."
- **Call to action** (1 sentence): Specific, confident, not tentative. "We recommend a 30-minute security architecture review with your CISO and our Head of Compliance as the logical next step — we've pre-answered every question in our Security Annex and can address follow-ups live."

---

**MODULE 3: QUESTION-BY-QUESTION RESPONSE (core RFP body)**

For each RFP question, provide:

**Answer structure per question:**
- **Direct answer** (first sentence): State the answer plainly. Never start with "Fieldstone believes" or "We feel." Start with "Yes," "Fieldstone provides," or "Our [specific capability] does X."
- **Evidence** (1-2 sentences): A specific customer outcome, a certification, a product capability with a metric, or a reference customer (named if permitted, anonymized if not — "a 3,500-employee regional health system" is still credible).
- **Differentiator signal** (1 sentence, optional but powerful): One sentence that subtly reframes the criterion to favor our approach. E.g., for a question about assessment frequency: "Unlike survey-based platforms that assess vendors annually, Fieldstone provides continuous monitoring — customers receive real-time alerts when a vendor's risk posture changes, not 11 months later."

**Special handling for common RFP question types:**

Security questionnaire questions (ISO/SOC/HIPAA/etc.):
- Answer with certification status + audit date + scope of certification
- Attach the relevant policy document reference by name (e.g., "See Appendix C: Information Security Policy, Section 4.2")
- Never say "we take security seriously" — describe the specific control

Implementation and timeline questions:
- Give the specific number of days for each phase — not "a few weeks"
- Name the roles involved on both sides
- Include a risk mitigation statement: "If go-live is delayed due to [buyer-side factor], here is our contingency process"

Pricing and commercial questions:
- Answer directly and completely — do not redirect to "let's discuss"
- Include total cost of ownership framing: "The all-in annual cost at your scale is $X, which includes [list everything]. There are no per-user overages, no professional services required, and no cost for security review support."
- Include a reference to the ROI model in the appendix

Reference and case study questions:
- Provide 3 named references minimum, all in the buyer's vertical or an adjacent one
- Include the reference's title, company, phone, and a one-sentence summary of their use case
- Never ask permission after the fact — confirm reference availability before submission

**MODULE 4: SECURITY & COMPLIANCE ANNEX**

Build a structured security annex that pre-answers every question a CISO evaluation team will ask, organized by category:

1. **Data handling and residency**: Where is data stored? Who has access? What is the data retention policy? How is data deleted at contract end?
2. **Access controls**: SSO/SAML support? MFA enforced? Role-based access controls? Audit log availability and retention period?
3. **Encryption**: At rest (AES-256 or equivalent)? In transit (TLS 1.2+ minimum)? Key management process?
4. **Incident response**: SLA for breach notification (e.g., "notification within 72 hours of confirmed breach, per GDPR Article 33")? Last penetration test date and findings summary? Bug bounty program?
5. **Certifications and audit availability**: Current certifications with expiry dates. Willingness to provide audit reports under NDA. Third-party sub-processors list.
6. **Business continuity**: RTO and RPO targets. Uptime SLA (with historical performance). Disaster recovery testing frequency.

Format each section as a table: Requirement | Our Posture | Evidence/Documentation Available.

---

**MODULE 5: ROI AND BUSINESS CASE APPENDIX**

Build a one-page ROI model customized to the buyer's stated context:

- **Current state cost** (quantify their pain): Staff hours spent on manual vendor assessments × hourly fully-loaded cost. Estimated regulatory fine exposure based on their vendor portfolio size and historical breach data in their industry.
- **Future state savings** (quantify our value): Hours eliminated. Breach risk reduction (use industry data — e.g., "Gartner: automated TPRM reduces breach-related incidents by 47% for companies with 200+ vendors"). Compliance preparation time reduction.
- **3-year TCO comparison**: Our total cost (Year 1, Year 2, Year 3 including renewal) vs. estimated cost of alternatives (competitor pricing if known, or internal build estimate: typically 1 FTE engineer × 12 months + annual maintenance).
- **Payback period**: Month by which cumulative savings exceed total contract cost.
- **Conservative / base / aggressive scenario**: Show three versions with different assumption sets — builds credibility by acknowledging uncertainty.

**MODULE 6: PROPOSAL DIFFERENTIATION STRATEGY (internal)**

Given the known competitive landscape, here is how to use this proposal to create unfair advantage:

1. **Anchor the evaluation criteria**: In the executive summary, define what "best-in-class vendor risk management" looks like in language that favors our real-time monitoring capability over OneTrust's survey-based model. Once the committee internalizes our framing, every competitor looks like they're playing catch-up.

2. **Inoculate against the internal build option**: Include a "Build vs. Buy" analysis in the appendix — not as a defensive document, but as a genuine decision framework. Show the 3 companies in their industry that tried to build internally, what they built, and why 2 of 3 eventually purchased anyway. Position us as the "smart build equivalent, ready today."

3. **Make the champion look smart**: Include a "Talking Points" one-pager formatted as if Jennifer Park wrote it herself — the points she can use to advocate for Fieldstone in internal meetings without needing to reference our proposal. Champions win deals in the rooms we're not in.

4. **De-risk the decision**: Every enterprise buyer fears being the person who chose the wrong vendor. Add a "What happens if this doesn't work?" section: our guarantee structure, our exit clause, our commitment to knowledge transfer if they ever leave. Removing fear of commitment is often more powerful than adding features.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: The AI produces generic, non-specific answers that could apply to any product in the category.**
Fix: The prompt requires specificity in the input — if you provide generic product descriptions, you get generic answers. Add at least 3 concrete customer proof points (real metrics, real company sizes, real outcomes) to the Product Intelligence section before running. The quality of the output is directly proportional to the specificity of the input. If you don't have case study data yet, use anonymized customer metrics from your CS team's QBR notes.

**Problem: The response doesn't address our known weaknesses and the evaluator will notice the gap.**
Fix: Run Module 1 (Scoring Strategy) explicitly before writing answers. For every red-scored mandatory requirement, write the mitigation statement directly in the response — evaluators respect transparency about limitations far more than they respect evasion. A response that says "We do not currently support X natively; here is our workaround and our roadmap commitment" will score better than one that ignores the question or buries the answer in feature jargon.

**Problem: The proposal is too long — evaluators are skipping to the pricing section.**
Fix: Apply the "17-minute rule" — the average procurement evaluator spends 17 minutes on an initial proposal pass. Every section must survive a 17-minute read. Run the executive summary and Module 3 outputs through this prompt: "Here is our draft proposal. Assume the reader has 17 minutes and will skim everything except the executive summary. Rewrite every section header as an outcome statement (not a topic label), and cut every paragraph that does not contain either a specific capability claim or a customer proof point."
