# Demo & Discovery Call Intelligence Engine - AI-Generated Discovery Frameworks and Demo Scripts That Close Deals

**Difficulty:** Advanced | **Time:** 25-30 min | **Tags:** sales-enablement, discovery, demo-script, b2b-saas, product-marketing, MEDDIC, SPICED, objection-handling, PMM, AE-enablement

## Overview
Generates persona-specific discovery call frameworks, customized demo scripts, and real-time objection handling playbooks that align your product's proof points to each buyer's stated priorities. Use it when launching a new sales motion, onboarding a new cohort of AEs, after win/loss analysis reveals demo inconsistency, or when a product update changes the core value narrative.

## Quick Copy-Paste Version

You are a senior sales engineer and product marketing expert who has run thousands of B2B SaaS demos. Your job is to build a complete discovery and demo playbook that any AE can pick up and use in a live customer call tomorrow.

Company and product context:
- Product: [e.g., "Meridian — AI-powered workforce scheduling for healthcare, $1,200/mo per location"]
- Core value delivered: [e.g., "Reduces scheduling time by 80% and cuts overtime spend by 35% in the first 90 days"]
- Buyer persona for this call: [e.g., "VP of Nursing Operations at a regional hospital network, 8-12 locations, 500+ nurses"]
- Deal stage: [e.g., "First discovery call — inbound, they downloaded our shift-swap ROI calculator"]

Build a complete playbook with:

1. **Pre-call research checklist** — 5 signals to find before joining the call (LinkedIn, job postings, recent news, tech stack, review sites) that reveal pain and urgency
2. **Call-opening power statement** — a 20-second framing that immediately positions you as a peer, not a vendor
3. **Discovery questions (SPICED framework)** — 12 questions organized by: Situation, Pain, Impact, Critical Event, Decision — with one follow-up probe for each
4. **Qualification knockout questions** — 3 questions that surface budget, authority, and timeline without sounding like a checklist
5. **Demo narrative arc** — a 3-act demo flow that opens with their stated pain, demonstrates the "aha moment" in act 2, and closes with a proof point from a similar customer
6. **Top 5 live objections + instant responses** — exact scripts for: "We built something internally," "The price is too high," "Our IT team will push back," "We need to see more ROI data," "We're not ready to move yet"
7. **Call-to-action close** — a specific next step script that assumes momentum rather than asking permission (e.g., "Based on what you've shared, the right next step is a technical deep-dive with your CNO and CIO — I can send a proposed agenda tonight. Does Thursday at 2pm work?")

Write everything as scripts, not frameworks. Every line should be ready to speak aloud.

GENERATE THE COMPLETE PLAYBOOK:

**SECTION 1: PRE-CALL INTELLIGENCE BRIEF (15 minutes of research, structured output)**

Signal 1 — Company hiring signals: What open roles reveal about their priorities and pain (e.g., 3 open RevOps roles = scaling mess; "Salesforce Admin" required = no automation today)
Signal 2 — Exec communications: Recent LinkedIn posts, press releases, earnings calls — what pressure is the exec under right now?
Signal 3 — Tech stack fingerprint: What tools show up in job postings, G2 reviews, or BuiltWith — what are they already bought into vs. likely to replace?
Signal 4 — Community signals: What are people at this company saying in Slack communities, Reddit, G2 reviews about their current stack?
Signal 5 — Recent trigger event: Any funding announcement, leadership change, product launch, or missed quarter that creates urgency NOW?

Format as a one-page brief an AE can read in 10 minutes before the call.

**SECTION 2: CALL OPENING (First 3 minutes)**

Pattern-interrupt opener (NOT "Thanks for joining, let me tell you about us"):
→ Reference a specific signal from pre-call research to demonstrate you've done homework
→ Frame the call as a peer conversation, not a pitch
→ Set the agenda in their language, not yours

Sample structure:
"[Name], I noticed [specific signal from research] — that tells me [implication for their business]. I want to make sure this 30 minutes is actually useful for you, so I'd like to spend the first 15 minutes understanding your situation, and then I'll show you exactly how two other [industry] companies solved [specific pain] — and you can tell me if it's relevant. Does that work?"

Write 3 opening variants: (a) inbound/warm, (b) outbound/cold, (c) competitive — prospect is already evaluating a competitor.

**SECTION 3: DISCOVERY FRAMEWORK (SPICED)**

For each SPICED stage, produce:
- The stage objective (what you're trying to learn)
- 3-4 primary questions
- 1 follow-up probe per question
- The "tell" — what answer reveals a highly qualified deal vs. a tire-kicker

**SITUATION** (understand their world, not your product)
Objective: Map their current process, team structure, and existing tools without making them feel interrogated.

Questions:
S1: "Walk me through how your team currently puts together the weekly forecast — who owns it, what data sources, how long does it take?"
→ Probe: "And when you say [their answer], what does that actually look like on a Thursday afternoon?"
→ Tell: Qualified if they describe manual Excel/Slides process with multiple owners. Tire-kicker if they say "it's pretty automated already."

S2: "When you look at your CRM data today, what percentage of it would you say is accurate enough to trust in a board meeting?"
→ Probe: "What's the last time bad data in the CRM caused a real problem — missed call, wrong resource allocation, CFO surprise?"
→ Tell: Qualified if they pause, laugh nervously, or give a number below 70%.

S3: "Who else on your team is feeling the pain here — is this primarily a RevOps problem, a VP Sales problem, or does it hit the CEO level?"
→ Probe: "How does [exec name] feel about the current forecasting process — have you ever heard them express frustration with it?"
→ Tell: Qualified if CEO/CFO is named as a stakeholder in forecast quality.

[Continue PAIN, IMPACT, CRITICAL EVENT, DECISION with same structure — produce 4 questions + probe + tell per stage]

**SECTION 4: DEMO NARRATIVE (StoryBrand Arc)**

Act 1 — The Character's World (Minutes 1-3):
Do NOT start by showing the product. Start by restating THEIR situation in THEIR words, pulled from discovery.
Script: "Based on what you shared, here's what I heard: [3-bullet summary of their pain in their language]. Before I show you anything, I want to confirm — is that a fair description of where you are today?"
[Pause. Get affirmation. This anchors the demo in their reality, not your feature set.]

Act 2 — The Guide Appears (Minutes 3-18):
Show only 3 features — the ones that directly solve the 3 pains they confirmed. For each:
- Name the pain: "You mentioned [specific thing they said]..."
- Show the feature: "Here's what that looks like in Meridian..."
- Prove the outcome: "When [Real Customer Name], a [similar company], turned this on, they went from [before] to [after] in [timeframe]"
- Invite their reaction: "Does this match what you were envisioning — or is there a different use case you're thinking about?"

Act 3 — Success State vs. Failure Avoided (Minutes 18-25):
Paint two pictures:
- Success: "In 90 days, Marcus, here's what your Sunday nights look like: [specific, vivid description — no spreadsheets, accurate forecast ready in the tool by Friday, no more CFO surprises]"
- Failure avoided: "The risk of staying with the current process isn't just time — it's a missed quarter because a deal that was rescueable at 45 days out slipped past you at day 10."

Use loss aversion psychology — the pain of staying the same must feel greater than the pain of changing.

**SECTION 5: OBJECTION HANDLING PLAYBOOK**

For each objection, provide:
- The surface objection (what they say)
- The real fear underneath (what they actually mean)
- The acknowledge-reframe-prove response
- A follow-up question that moves the conversation forward

OBJ 1: "We built something internally / we could probably just build this."
Real fear: "We might waste budget on something our team could do."
Response: "That's actually how [Customer Name] started — their engineering team built a Salesforce dashboard that took 4 months and 1 engineer full-time. The problem wasn't whether they could build it — it was that their engineers were then on the hook to maintain it every time Salesforce updated their API or their sales process changed. They came to us 8 months later when the tool broke mid-quarter. Can I ask — what's the opportunity cost of those engineering hours if they're pointed at your product roadmap instead?"
Follow-up: "What would have to be true for building internally to be the wrong decision a year from now?"

OBJ 2: "The price is too high / this is more than we expected to spend."
Real fear: "I can't justify this to my CFO."
Response: "I hear that. Let me reframe it for a second — at [your pricing], the question isn't whether it's expensive, it's whether it's more expensive than the status quo. Your team is spending [X hours/week] on manual forecast prep, and you mentioned [specific deal loss/slip they referenced]. If we help you recover one at-risk deal per quarter at your average ACV of [their number], what's the payback period on the annual contract?"
Follow-up: "If I could build you a one-page ROI case with your specific numbers, would that give your CFO what they need to approve it?"

OBJ 3: "Our IT/Security team will push back on a new tool."
Real fear: "I don't want to fight an internal battle for this."
Response: "Completely understand — and this is exactly why we built our security package the way we did. We're SOC 2 Type II certified, GDPR compliant, and we don't store deal data — we only read metadata. The typical IT review for us takes 5-7 business days because we've pre-answered every question your CISO will ask. I can send you our security one-pager right now — and I'll include three reference contacts at companies with similar security postures who've been through the process."
Follow-up: "Who on your IT team would own this review — would it help to get them on a 20-minute technical call with our security lead directly?"

OBJ 4: "We need to see more ROI data / more proof that this works."
Real fear: "I've been burned by vendor promises before."
Response: "Fair — and you should require that. Here's what I can do: I have three customers in [their industry/segment] who are on the phone every month with our CS team sharing results. I'd rather introduce you directly to their RevOps lead than send you a PDF that I wrote. Would a 20-minute peer reference call be more valuable than a case study?"
Follow-up: "What specific metric would you want them to validate — time savings, forecast accuracy, or pipeline visibility?"

OBJ 5: "We're not ready to move on this yet / timing isn't right."
Real fear: "I'm not the real decision maker, or there's no urgency."
Response: "I appreciate the honesty. Help me understand — when you say timing isn't right, is it a budget cycle thing, a team capacity thing, or is there another initiative taking priority? I ask because [Customer Name] said the same thing in Q2, waited until Q4, and in the meantime lost two deals they estimate they could have saved with better pipeline visibility. I don't want that to happen to you — but I also don't want to push you toward something before you're ready. What would have to change for this to be the right time?"
Follow-up: "If we could start with a 30-day pilot on one team at a lower commitment — does that change the calculus?"

**SECTION 6: MULTI-STAKEHOLDER NAVIGATION**

Champion management (your AE's ally):
- How to arm the champion to sell internally without you in the room
- Email template the champion can forward to their CFO/CIO
- How to identify when the champion is losing internal momentum and re-engage

Economic buyer play (CFO/CEO):
- What they care about: risk reduction, payback period, strategic fit
- What they don't care about: features, integrations, UX
- One-slide "business case" structure that speaks CFO language

Technical evaluator play (IT/Security/RevOps):
- Their job is to find reasons to say no — your job is to pre-answer every objection
- Security documentation checklist to send before they ask
- How to run a technical POC that produces a clear yes/no in 14 days

**SECTION 7: CALL CLOSE AND NEXT STEPS**

Momentum-assuming close (not "What do you think?" — never end on an open question):
"Based on what you've shared today, the clear next step is [specific action]. I'll send you [specific asset] tonight, and I'd like to propose [specific next meeting] with [specific attendees]. I have [day/time] open — does that work, or is [alternative] better?"

Three close variants:
(a) Hot deal — ready to move fast: "Let's get your RevOps lead and IT contact on a 45-minute technical deep-dive. I'll bring our solutions engineer. I can get you a pilot agreement by Friday."
(b) Warm deal — needs internal buy-in: "Let me send you three things: the ROI model with your numbers, our security one-pager, and an intro to [Reference Customer Name] at [Company]. Can we reconnect in 5 business days after you've had a chance to share this internally?"
(c) Cold deal — unclear urgency: "I don't want to waste your time if the timing isn't right. Let me send you one asset that's genuinely useful regardless — our [industry] benchmark report. If something changes on your end, use my calendar link to grab time whenever. And I'll check in lightly in 30 days."

## Success Metrics

A high-quality output from this prompt delivers:
- **Discovery completeness rate**: AE captures all 5 SPICED dimensions in a single 30-minute call
- **Demo-to-next-step conversion**: >70% of demos result in a scheduled follow-up meeting (industry benchmark: 45-55%)
- **Objection deflection rate**: Reps handle top 5 objections without escalating to PMM or SE
- **Time-to-qualified**: Deal moves from discovery to technical evaluation in ≤10 business days
- **Champion quality score**: Post-call, AE can name the economic buyer, technical evaluator, and critical event — all three required to advance

Validate output quality by having a veteran AE score each script section on: Would I actually say this in a live call? (1-5). Anything below 4 is rewritten.

## Integration Tips

**HubSpot / Salesforce CRM**: After each discovery call, have the AI populate a structured deal record: SPICED stage status, identified pain, critical event, economic buyer name, next step. Build a custom deal stage checklist that maps to SPICED completion — deals can't advance without all 5 fields populated.

**Gong / Chorus**: Feed call transcripts back into this prompt to identify which discovery questions land vs. which cause awkward silences. Run a monthly analysis: "Here are 10 call transcripts from deals we won and 10 from deals we lost — what discovery patterns predict wins?"

**Notion / Confluence**: Store the complete playbook as a living document. Connect to your CRM via Zapier so that when a new ICP account is created, a persona-specific discovery guide is auto-generated and added to the deal record.

**Slack**: Build a Slack workflow where AEs paste a prospect LinkedIn URL + company name and get an auto-generated pre-call research brief within 60 seconds using this prompt architecture + a web search API.

**Google Slides / Pitch**: Auto-populate demo slide templates with prospect-specific numbers from the SPICED discovery output — pull in their stated DSO, their team size, their current tool — so the demo deck shows their logo and their metrics, not generic placeholders.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Discovery feels like an interrogation — prospects give one-word answers.**
Fix: Open with fewer questions, more hypotheses. Instead of "What's your process?", say "I've talked to 40 companies like yours — most tell me their biggest problem is [X]. Is that true for you, or is it something different?" Hypotheses invite elaboration; open questions invite deflection.

**Problem: Demo runs long — AEs are showing too many features.**
Fix: Use the "3 features maximum" rule. Enforce it by building a pre-demo checklist: AE must write down the 3 specific pain points confirmed in discovery and map each to exactly one product screen. If they can't do this before the demo, they haven't done enough discovery.

**Problem: The prompt produces generic scripts that don't match our product's actual UI.**
Fix: Add a "product walkthrough transcript" section to the Advanced input — paste in a lightly edited Gong transcript of your best AE's best demo, and instruct the AI to adapt the framework to match your actual product flow and language.
