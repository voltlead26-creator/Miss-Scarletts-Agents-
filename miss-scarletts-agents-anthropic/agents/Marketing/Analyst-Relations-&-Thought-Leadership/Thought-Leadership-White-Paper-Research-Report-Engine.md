## Troubleshooting

**Problem: The report feels like a product brochure instead of genuine research.**
Fix: Remove all product mentions from the first 80% of the report. The company's solution should appear only in the conclusion and the "about" section. Ask yourself: "Would a competitor's customer find this valuable even if they never buy from us?" If yes, you have a real thought leadership asset. If no, rewrite the body sections to lead with the problem, the finding, and the framework — not the product.

**Problem: Research sample is too small or methodology is too weak to withstand scrutiny.**
Fix: Reframe directional data as "practitioner pulse" or "practitioner survey" rather than "research study." Use language like "among the [X] revenue leaders we surveyed" rather than "research shows." Add qualitative depth — 5–8 in-depth practitioner interviews can substitute for statistical power when quoted directly. Supplement with publicly available third-party data (Gartner, LinkedIn, industry associations) and cite properly.

**Problem: The report isn't generating downloads after launch.**
Fix: The landing page headline is almost always the culprit — most B2B reports fail because the title describes what the report is ("State of Sales Forecasting 2026") rather than what the reader gets ("Why 73% of Sales Leaders Are Flying Blind — And the 6% Who Aren't"). A/B test 3 headline variants immediately. Second most common issue: the report is gated too aggressively — consider ungating the executive summary (first 800 words) and gating only the full report to reduce friction while still capturing leads.
