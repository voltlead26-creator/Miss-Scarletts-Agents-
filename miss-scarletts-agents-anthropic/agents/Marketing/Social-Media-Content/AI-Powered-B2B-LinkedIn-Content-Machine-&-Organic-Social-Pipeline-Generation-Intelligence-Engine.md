## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Posts are getting impressions but zero ICP engagement — only peers and other marketers are commenting.**
Solution: Your content pillars are optimized for practitioners in your own function, not your buyer. Run a follower demographic audit in LinkedIn Analytics — if your top follower job titles don't match your ICP, you've built an audience of the wrong people. Shift 2 content pillars immediately to address pain points that your ICP experiences but that most of their vendors never write about. Example: If you sell to VP of Engineering, stop writing about "AI trends" (marketing content) and start writing about "the organizational politics of getting AI budget approved" (their actual daily frustration). Also audit your hashtags — remove broad ones like #AI or #Marketing that attract content consumers instead of buyers.

**Problem: High engagement rate on individual posts but zero pipeline movement after 60 days.**
Solution: Organic LinkedIn works on a 90-120 day trust-build cycle for complex B2B sales. Pipeline won't appear before the trust threshold is crossed. Check two things: (1) Are your CTAs too passive? Move 20% of posts to include a direct but soft offer — "DM me the word [X] and I'll send you our [resource]" — to create a trackable conversion event. (2) Is your SDR or sales team monitoring post engagement and proactively connecting with ICP commenters? The biggest missed opportunity in LinkedIn content is treating post engagement as passive brand awareness instead of a warm outbound signal. Every ICP who comments on your post is a better lead than any cold outbound target.

**Problem: The content machine is producing posts but the team can't maintain publishing consistency past week 3.**
Solution: The bottleneck is almost always in the approval process, not the content production. Implement a "draft library" approach: use this prompt to generate 60 posts at once (two months), have one approval session per month rather than per post, load all approved posts into your scheduler in one sitting. Consistency is a systems problem, not a motivation problem. Additionally, designate one person as the "LinkedIn lead" who owns the publishing calendar and has authority to publish without additional approval cycles. Teams that route every post through 3 approval layers fail at LinkedIn; teams with a single empowered owner succeed.
