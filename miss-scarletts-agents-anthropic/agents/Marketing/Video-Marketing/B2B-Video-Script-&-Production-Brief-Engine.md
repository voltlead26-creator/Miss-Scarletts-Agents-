# B2B Video Script & Production Brief Engine - Full-Funnel Video Content Automation for Any Format

**Difficulty:** Advanced | **Time:** 20-30 min | **Tags:** video-marketing, video-scripts, b2b-content, production-briefs, video-strategy

## Overview
Generates complete, ready-to-shoot video scripts and production briefs for every B2B video format — explainer videos, product demos, customer testimonials, LinkedIn thought leadership clips, YouTube tutorials, webinar openers, and executive talking-head pieces. Use it when your team needs camera-ready scripts that a videographer, an in-house presenter, or an AI avatar tool can execute without a creative director in the room.

### SECTION 1: STRATEGIC BRIEF (EXECUTIVE SUMMARY FOR STAKEHOLDERS)

Complete before any script work:

**Video Purpose Statement:** One sentence — what behavior change do we want in the viewer after watching? (e.g., "After watching, a VP of Sales at a 200-person SaaS company should believe that manual pipeline reporting is costing them 5+ hours per week and book a 15-minute demo to see how we fix it.")

**Positioning Anchor:** The single most important thing this video must communicate. Everything else is supporting evidence.

**Core Message (One-Liner):** The sentence the viewer should be able to repeat to their boss or team. Write it like a headline — specific, memorable, outcome-focused. Examples: "We cut deal cycle time by 40% without adding headcount." or "Most customer success teams are running blind — here's the data they're missing."

**Viewer Journey Map:**
- Where the viewer is before watching: [mental state, what they believe now]
- Where they are after watching: [what they now believe, what they're motivated to do]
- The single belief shift this video must create

**Format Rationale:** Why this video format (chosen above) is the right vehicle for this message and this distribution channel. If the format was requested, validate it — if not optimal, recommend an alternative and explain why.

### SECTION 2: HOOK ARCHITECTURE (0 TO [10] SECONDS)

The hook is the only part of the script that determines whether anything else gets watched. Apply one of these five proven B2B hook frameworks based on what will resonate most with the target persona:

**Hook Framework Options (choose the most relevant, or layer two):**

1. **The Pain Call-Out Hook:** Name the exact problem the viewer is experiencing right now, in the language they use internally — not the language you use in your marketing.
   > Example: "Your reps are updating Salesforce manually. Three hours a week. Every week. And you know the data is still wrong."

2. **The Contrarian Insight Hook:** Challenge a widely-held belief in the viewer's industry. Specific, provocative, defensible.
   > Example: "The best-performing enterprise sales teams have stopped doing discovery calls. Here's what they do instead."

3. **The Curiosity Gap Hook:** Promise a specific revelation that the viewer doesn't know yet but immediately wants to.
   > Example: "There's a reason your highest-intent prospects aren't converting — and it's not your pricing."

4. **The Result-First Hook:** Lead with the outcome, then prove it. Works best for product demos and testimonials.
   > Example: "This is how DataSync helped Meridian Financial reduce onboarding time from 11 days to 2 — without changing a single internal process."

5. **The Pattern Interrupt Hook:** Open with something visually or verbally unexpected for the format and channel — breaks the autopilot scroll without being gimmicky.
   > Example (for a B2B LinkedIn video): Open in silence, presenter looks directly at camera for 3 seconds before speaking. Text on screen: "Most CFOs don't know this is happening."

**Deliver:**
- Selected hook framework with rationale
- Hook script, word-for-word (0 to 10 seconds)
- Opening visual direction (what does the viewer see in the first frame?)
- On-screen text overlay copy if applicable (keep to 5-7 words maximum)
- What the hook is promising the viewer they'll get if they keep watching (the implicit contract)

### SECTION 3: FULL VIDEO SCRIPT

Format: Two-column screenplay style.

| VISUAL / ACTION | AUDIO / SCRIPT |
|---|---|
| [Camera angle, subject, on-screen text, B-roll description] | [Exact words the speaker says, OR voiceover narration, OR on-screen text-only sequence] |

**Script Architecture by Video Type:**

**For Explainer Videos (60 sec – 3 min):**
Structure: Hook → Problem (the world as it is) → Solution Reveal (the world as it could be) → How It Works (3 key mechanisms, no more) → Proof (one specific result) → CTA
- Maximum one idea per scene — if a scene is trying to say two things, split it
- Every transition must advance the argument — no "and another thing is..."
- Avoid feature lists; translate every feature into a viewer outcome

**For Product Demo Videos (narrated screen recording or live walkthrough):**
Structure: Hook (what problem this demo solves) → Setup (here's the situation we're starting from) → The Workflow (step-by-step, narrated with "here's why this matters at each step") → The Payoff (show the end result, not just the process) → CTA
- Narrate the "why" of each click, not just "now I click here"
- Highlight 3 key "wow moments" — the features that make viewers say "we need this"
- Include a "pause point" callout where viewers should screenshot or take notes

**For Customer Testimonial Videos:**
Structure: Identity Hook (who the customer is — make the viewer see themselves) → Before State (the problem in the customer's own words) → Turning Point (why they chose you, specifically) → After State (specific result with numbers) → Recommendation (what they'd say to a skeptical peer)
- Write the interview questions the customer needs to answer to deliver this arc — do not script the customer's words, script the questions
- Include directing notes: how to get authentic, non-rehearsed-sounding responses
- Note which answers to use as pull quotes for social clips

**For LinkedIn Short-Form (< 60 sec):**
Structure: Hook (first 3 sec, text on screen) → Insight or Story (single, specific point) → Takeaway (one actionable thing the viewer can do today) → Soft CTA (optional — comment-bait or profile link)
- Write for mute-first: all key points captioned on-screen
- No intro ("Hi, I'm [name] and today I'm going to talk about...") — these kill view completion on LinkedIn
- End on a question that invites comments — algorithmic amplification depends on early comment velocity

**For YouTube Tutorial (5-15 min):**
Structure: Hook (what you'll learn and why it matters in 30 sec) → Agenda (optional for long tutorials, builds commitment) → Step-by-Step Instruction (each step named with a timestamp-friendly title) → Common Mistakes to Avoid → Recap (3-bullet summary) → Subscribe + Next Video CTA
- Insert "retention hooks" at minute 2 and every 2-3 minutes: a forward reference to what's coming ("in the next step, I'll show you the one setting most people miss")
- Write chapter titles formatted for YouTube chapter markers (0:00 - Intro, 1:22 - Step 1: ...)
- Include a "pattern interrupt" at minute 3-4 to recover viewers who are losing attention

**For Webinar Intro / Opening (2-4 min):**
Structure: Audience Call-Out (who this is for — who should stay and who it's not for) → Stakes (why this topic matters right now — urgency frame) → Credibility (why the presenter is the right person to teach this — specific, not a bio recitation) → Agenda Preview (3-4 bullets, outcome-framed) → Logistics (housekeeping — brevity is a kindness) → Transition to content
- Write the exact slide text for each section alongside the spoken script
- Include audience engagement prompt at the 90-second mark (poll question, chat prompt)

**For Executive Thought Leadership (LinkedIn Video, Conference Talk Opener, YouTube):**
Structure: Provocative Premise (the thing everyone in the industry believes that you disagree with) → Evidence (3 specific data points or observations that support your contrarian view) → The Reframe (what the right mental model actually is) → Implication (what this means for the viewer's strategy or decisions) → Invitation (a question that opens discussion, not a product CTA)
- Executive TL videos live or die on specificity — vague "thought leadership" is invisible
- Every claim must be sourced or grounded in firsthand experience — never generic industry advice
- Coaching note: write speaker notes on delivery (pause here, slow down for emphasis, look directly at camera for this line)

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Presenter reads the script stiff and unnatural on camera**
Fix: The script is too formal — rewrite all sentences under 15 words. Read every line aloud before finalizing: if you can't say it naturally in one breath, break it into two sentences. Add a parenthetical delivery note in brackets after any line that has a specific rhythm or emphasis. For nervous presenters, have them record a casual 2-minute video explaining the topic on their phone before any scripted takes — use that version as the delivery energy benchmark, then film the scripted version immediately after.

**Problem: Video gets views but no CTA clicks or demo requests**
Fix: The CTA is failing at specificity or trust. Audit three things: (1) Does the CTA appear before the viewer's attention expires — for 90-second videos, the CTA must appear by the 75-second mark, not saved for the last 5 seconds; (2) Is the landing page load time under 2 seconds — mobile viewers who waited through your video will not wait for a slow page; (3) Does the CTA promise match the landing page delivery — "Start free trial" must land on a signup page, not a "request a demo" form. Add a friction-reducing qualifier to the CTA: "Start free trial — no credit card required, setup in 8 minutes" outperforms "Start free trial" by 20-35% in B2B.

**Problem: Short-form LinkedIn videos plateau at low reach despite good content**
Fix: LinkedIn's algorithm prioritizes content that generates comments in the first 30 minutes. The script's CTA must ask a specific, answerable question — not "what do you think?" (too broad) but "What's the biggest time sink your reps complain about in your CRM — drop it in the comments" (specific, low effort to answer). Film a follow-up reply to the top comment within 2 hours of posting; LinkedIn rewards creator responsiveness with extended distribution. Also audit the first-frame thumbnail — LinkedIn displays a static frame from the first 2 seconds before the viewer presses play; ensure the opening frame contains a face (human faces in thumbnails increase play rate by 30%+) or high-contrast text.
