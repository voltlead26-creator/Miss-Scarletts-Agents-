## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Sean Ellis survey response rate is below 15%, making results statistically unreliable.**
Solution: The most common cause is sending to the full customer list rather than the "activated + engaged" cohort. Filter to accounts that have logged at least 2 sessions in the last 30 days and have been customers for 30–90 days (the "honeymoon window" where opinions are freshest and strongest). Personalize the send from the CEO or CPO — a 3-line plain-text email outperforms an HTML survey invite by 2–3x. If you still can't hit n=50 minimum, run the survey as a 2-minute Zoom call instead of a form; offer a $25 Amazon gift card for completion.

**Problem: Retention data shows highly variable cohort performance — some months show 90% retention, others show 55%, making it hard to read PMF signals.**
Solution: Cohort variability above ±15 points usually indicates ICP drift in acquisition — certain months you acquired off-ICP customers who churned faster. Segment your cohorts by ICP tier (use firmographic data from Clearbit, 6sense, or your CRM) and graph retention separately for ICP-perfect vs. off-ICP. You'll likely find that ICP-perfect cohorts show consistent retention while off-ICP cohorts drag the aggregate down. This is actually a strong PMF signal in the correct segment — the problem is in targeting, not the product.

**Problem: The model returns a high PMF score, but the sales team can't close deals consistently and growth has stalled.**
Solution: High retention + low new logo growth is the "strong PMF, weak GTM" pattern. The product delivers value to existing customers but the go-to-market motion isn't efficiently reaching new buyers. Run the JTBD interview program specifically focused on Questions 4–6 (consideration and selection) to understand what triggers the purchase decision, what makes prospects hesitate, and how buyers found you. Typically, this reveals that the sales motion is targeting the wrong persona (e.g., selling to IT when Legal Ops is the actual buyer) or the awareness channels don't reach the trigger moment (e.g., the buyer's trigger event is signing a new enterprise vendor but marketing is running brand awareness ads to people not in an active procurement cycle).
