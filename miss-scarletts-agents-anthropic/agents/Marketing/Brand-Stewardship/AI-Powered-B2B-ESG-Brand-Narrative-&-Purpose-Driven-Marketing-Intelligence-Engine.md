## Troubleshooting

**"Our ESG commitments are too thin to build a credible narrative"**
Start with governance — every company has a data ethics policy, security certifications, and some form of board governance. These are the ESG pillars enterprise buyers care most about in SaaS procurement. Build the narrative around data governance first, then add social (DE&I metrics you have, even if imperfect) and environmental (even "we're measuring our footprint for the first time" is honest and credible). The ESG narrative doesn't need to be perfect to be procurement-ready — it needs to be honest, specific, and verifiable.

**"A prospect sent an ESG questionnaire we don't know how to answer — deal is stalling"**
Use Section 4 (Procurement ESG Response Toolkit) immediately as a gap assessment. For questions you cannot answer, respond with: "We do not currently have a formal program for [topic], however [describe any relevant practice or commitment, even informal]. We are implementing [specific next step] by [specific date]." Honest acknowledgment of gaps with a concrete plan is significantly better — and less legally risky — than vague or fabricated responses.

**"A competitor is making ESG claims that seem exaggerated — how do we respond without looking defensive?"**
Never directly attack competitor ESG claims. Instead, execute the "proof asymmetry" strategy: publish highly specific, third-party verified ESG claims alongside links to the verification source. The contrast in credibility becomes apparent without a direct challenge. If a competitor's claim appears to violate FTC Green Guides or EU Greenwashing Directive, report it to the appropriate regulator — this is a legitimate competitive action, not an attack.
