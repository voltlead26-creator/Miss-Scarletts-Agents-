## Overview
Diagnoses whether a brand needs repositioning, maps competitive whitespace, and produces a complete repositioning strategy — including new positioning statements, internal narrative, messaging architecture, and a phased rollout plan. Use it when the market has shifted around you, a new category is forming, a competitor has stolen your narrative, or your growth has plateaued despite a strong product.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: The board or CEO agrees on the new position conceptually but will not approve the budget or timeline to execute it, citing risk to current pipeline.**
Solution: Reframe the budget request as pipeline insurance, not brand investment. Pull the last 90 days of lost deals where the positioning was a contributing factor (CRM notes: "evaluated as a point solution," "compared us on price to Jira"). Quantify the pipeline dollars that were lost or discounted because of positioning failure. Present the repositioning budget against that number — not against the company's total ARR. A $300K repositioning investment is a different conversation when positioned against $2M in attributable pipeline loss. Then propose a phased approach: $75K for Phase 1-2 (internal, owned channels, analyst briefings) before committing to Phase 3 (PR, events, full demand gen retool). This de-risks the ask and creates a natural go/no-go gate after 60 days.

**Problem: The sales team resists the new positioning because they have quota on deals using the old narrative and do not want to destabilize active opportunities.**
Solution: Never launch repositioning over the sales team — launch it with them. Identify 3 sales reps who are early adopters and have deals that fit the new ICP best. Give them the new pitch first, in a 1:1 with the CMO and VP Sales, before anyone else hears it. Ask for their honest pushback — fix whatever is not working in the room. Then let those 3 reps pilot the new pitch for 30 days while the rest of the team uses the old narrative. At 30 days, compare the 3 pilot reps' pipeline quality and early-stage conversion to the control group. If the new pitch is working, you have peer evidence to bring to the full team — sales reps trust other sales reps more than CMO slide decks.

**Problem: The new positioning is live on the website and in content, but analysts continue to categorize the company under the old category definition.**
Solution: Analyst repositioning is a separate workstream that requires proactive management, not a website update. Request a dedicated "repositioning briefing" from your Gartner and Forrester coverage teams — a 60-minute session specifically to present the new category thesis, the supporting market data, and the customer evidence. Come with original research: a 1-page data brief showing the size of the problem the new category solves, with numbers sourced from customer interviews or third-party research. Analysts respond to market data and customer evidence, not marketing positioning statements. If you do not have analyst relationships, engage a B2B analyst relations firm for a 90-day sprint — their warm introductions and briefing expertise will compress the timeline significantly.
