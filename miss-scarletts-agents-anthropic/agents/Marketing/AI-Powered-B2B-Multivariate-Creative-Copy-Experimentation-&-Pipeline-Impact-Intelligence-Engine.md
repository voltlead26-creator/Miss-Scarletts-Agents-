## Overview
This engine designs, executes, and analyzes systematic creative and copy experiments across B2B paid media, email, and landing pages — connecting test outcomes directly to pipeline and revenue rather than vanity metrics. Built for B2B SaaS marketing teams who need statistically valid creative learnings at the small sample sizes inherent to enterprise demand generation.

## 4. STATISTICAL VALIDITY PLAN (BAYESIAN APPROACH)
B2B teams rarely have enough traffic for traditional frequentist A/B testing. Use Bayesian analysis:

**Sample Size Requirements:**
- Baseline conversion rate: [X%]
- Minimum detectable effect (MDE): [X% relative lift — e.g., 15% relative lift = going from 2.0% to 2.3% conversion rate]
- Number of variants: [X]
- Bayesian prior: [uniform/weak — assume no prior knowledge unless specified]
- Required sample size per variant to reach 95% Probability of Superiority: [calculate using Bayesian formula or reference table]
- Available sample per week: [X impressions / sends / visitors]
- **Estimated test duration to reach validity:** [X weeks]
- Early stopping rule: [Pause if any variant reaches ≥95% Probability of Superiority before planned end date; do NOT stop at 80% — commit to the plan]

**Bayesian vs. Frequentist rationale for B2B:**
- Frequentist p<0.05 testing requires large sample sizes (often 10,000+ per variant) that B2B teams can't achieve in reasonable time
- Bayesian Probability of Superiority (P(B>A)) reaches actionable conclusions with 1,000-3,000 samples per variant
- Recommended tool: [Optimizely Bayesian calculator / AB Testguide.com / Custom Python/R implementation using Beta-Binomial model]
- Report weekly: track P(B>A), expected loss (cost of choosing the wrong variant), and 95% credible interval for lift

**Sequential Testing Safety:**
- Check results weekly (not daily — peeking inflates false positive risk even in Bayesian models)
- Implement a Bayesian sequential testing procedure (SPRT or mSPRT) if continuous monitoring is required
- Define a stopping boundary before the test begins: [e.g., "Stop and declare winner if P(B>A) ≥ 95% AND expected loss < 0.1%"]

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Test runs for 6 weeks but no variant reaches statistical significance — results are inconclusive**
Solution: This is the most common B2B experimentation failure mode and almost always indicates one of three root causes: (1) **Insufficient traffic volume** — if you have <200 conversions per variant after 6 weeks, you are mathematically unable to detect a 15-20% relative lift. Solution: consolidate test variants to 2 (not 3-5), expand the audience or budget to increase conversion volume, or shift to a higher-traffic touchpoint like email subject lines where you can generate 5,000+ opens per variant per week; (2) **Testing the wrong variable** — if the variable you're testing isn't the primary constraint on conversion (e.g., testing headline when the real friction is the form length or pricing page clarity), no amount of testing will move the needle. Run a conversion funnel diagnostic first: where does your funnel lose the most people between channel click and demo request? Test the variable at the biggest drop-off point; (3) **The variants are too similar** — if Variant B and Variant A differ by one word in the headline, the effect size will be too small to detect. Create dramatically different variants (different psychological lever, different value proposition angle, different audience identity framing) to maximize detectable effect size.

**Problem: Winning creative variant drives more volume but lead quality drops — sales is complaining about MQL quality**
Solution: This is a classic creative optimization trap where short-term volume metrics mask long-term revenue damage. Implement a "Lead Quality Gate" before scaling any winning creative: (1) Wait until the winning variant has generated ≥30 SQLs (not just leads) before declaring victory — evaluate SQL conversion rate, not just form fill rate; (2) Pull Gong recordings from SQLs sourced through the winning variant — are prospects entering with correct expectations about the product? Are they the right ICP? (3) Add a qualification layer: if the winning variant drove volume via a "broad promise" (e.g., "Get SOC 2 fast" attracts SMBs who can't afford enterprise pricing), add a budget or company size qualifier to the landing page form. The correct metric for creative success in B2B is never raw CPL — it is Cost per Qualified Opportunity (CPQO) and ultimately cost per closed ARR dollar.

**Problem: AI-generated creative variants read as "too obviously AI" — formulaic, lacking authentic voice**
Solution: AI-generated first drafts are starting points, not final assets — they require human voice injection before testing. Three specific fixes: (1) **Input raw material, not briefs**: Instead of prompting Claude/GPT with "write me a B2B LinkedIn ad about compliance automation," paste 5 verbatim customer quotes from Gong calls and instruct: "Rewrite this LinkedIn ad headline using ONLY the language these customers used — no marketing jargon." The output will be authentic because it's built from real customer language; (2) **Apply the Specificity Test**: Replace every generic phrase ("faster," "easier," "better") with a specific number or name from your customer data ("18 days," "$340K ARR unlocked," "3 engineering hours vs. 3 months"). Specific copy is always harder to identify as AI-generated because it's anchored to reality; (3) **Add friction and texture**: Real customer language has rough edges — slightly imperfect grammar, informal contractions, and specific situational references. Instruct the AI: "Make this sound like a VP Engineering wrote it in a Slack message to a peer, not like a marketing team wrote it for a landing page."
