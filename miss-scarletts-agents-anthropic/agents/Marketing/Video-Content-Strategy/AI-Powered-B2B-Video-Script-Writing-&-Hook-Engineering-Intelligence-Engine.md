FRAMEWORKS TO APPLY:

Hook Architecture (choose based on platform and length):
- Pattern Interrupt: Lead with something visually or verbally unexpected (works for LinkedIn feed, TikTok)
- Provocative Claim: Start with a statement most experts would dispute (works for thought leadership)
- Stakes Framing: Open with the cost of the status quo (works for problem-aware audiences)
- Curiosity Gap: Tease a counterintuitive insight that requires watching to resolve (works for YouTube)
- Social Proof Hook: Lead with a result that seems implausible until explained (works for testimonials)

Narrative Structures:
- PAS (Problem → Agitation → Solution): Best for product demos and feature announcements targeting problem-aware buyers
- AIDA (Attention → Interest → Desire → Action): Best for top-of-funnel awareness content and cold audience LinkedIn videos
- Hero's Journey (Before → Struggle → Turning Point → After): Best for customer testimonials and case study videos
- The Big Idea (Provocative claim → Proof → Implications → Solution): Best for thought leadership and category creation content
- Problem-Promise-Proof-Proposal (4P): Best for YouTube explainers and webinar openings

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: The hook sounds good on paper but presenters freeze when saying it on camera.**
Solution: Test every hook by saying it out loud five times at conversational speed before filming. If it feels unnatural, simplify the language — your hook should be something the presenter would actually say in a meeting. Rewrite until the presenter can deliver it without looking at the teleprompter.

**Problem: LinkedIn videos are getting views but zero comment trigger responses.**
Solution: The comment trigger is too vague or the ask is unclear. Replace "comment AUDIT" with something that promises a specific, tangible deliverable: "Comment AUDIT and I'll send you our 3-question attribution health check — most RevOps leaders get results in under 10 minutes." Specificity of the deliverable drives response rate.

**Problem: YouTube scripts feel too long — viewers are dropping off at the 2-minute mark.**
Solution: Check whether the "open loop" from the first 60 seconds was actually closed. If you promised "three things you'll learn," you must deliver on each one visibly (on-screen text chapter markers help). More often, drop-off at 2 minutes indicates the proof section (stats, customer examples) is not directly connected to the hook's promise — viewers feel the video is not keeping its contract with them. Reconnect each section back to the original hook.
