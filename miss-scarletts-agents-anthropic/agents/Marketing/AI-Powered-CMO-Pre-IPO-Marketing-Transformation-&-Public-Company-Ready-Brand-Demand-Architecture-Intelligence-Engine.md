## Troubleshooting

**Problem:** Legal counsel wants to strip all performance claims from marketing, leaving us with generic messaging that won't build credibility.
**Solution:** Engage legal on the "approved claims library" framework — proactively document and verify 20–30 specific, auditable claims (customer ROI stats, survey data, G2 ratings) that can be used without re-review. This shifts the conversation from "review every piece" to "use from the approved library." Work with legal to understand the difference between historical facts (allowed), forward-looking statements (restricted), and comparative claims (allowed with citation).

**Problem:** Our attribution model shows wildly inconsistent marketing contribution numbers quarter to quarter — CFO won't use them.
**Solution:** This is a data quality problem, not an attribution methodology problem. Run a 90-day data audit: identify where leads are being created without source attribution, fix HubSpot/Salesforce integration gaps, implement mandatory source fields, and backfill historical data. Present CFO with before/after data quality report. Consider bringing in a marketing analytics consultant (RevOps) to independently validate the model — the CFO will trust a third-party validation.

**Problem:** We're in the quiet period and a competitor just made a major announcement. We want to respond but can't.
**Solution:** Pre-build a competitive response content library before the quiet period that covers 5–7 likely competitor moves. Get legal pre-approval on each response template. When a competitor announcement happens during quiet period, publish from the pre-approved library without needing legal re-review. For truly unexpected moves that fall outside the pre-approved templates, coordinate with legal and IR before publishing anything — the safe option is always "say nothing" during quiet period.
