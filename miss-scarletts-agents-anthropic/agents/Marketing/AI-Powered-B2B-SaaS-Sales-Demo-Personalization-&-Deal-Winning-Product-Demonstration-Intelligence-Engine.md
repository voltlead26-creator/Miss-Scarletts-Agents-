Automates the full lifecycle of a high-converting B2B SaaS sales demo — from AI-powered pre-demo prospect research and persona-specific story arc design, through live objection handling scripts and "wow moment" choreography, to post-demo follow-up automation and deal-velocity scoring. Use it when you need to convert more discovery-qualified prospects into POC/trials and closed-won deals by delivering demos that feel custom-built for each buyer, not generic product walkthroughs.

#### 2. DEMO CALL SCRIPT (45-60 minutes)

**Pre-Demo Setup (AE internal checklist, 15 minutes before call):**
- [ ] CRM updated with all attendees — no surprises on who is joining
- [ ] Demo environment loaded with prospect's industry data, not generic placeholder data. If you sell to fintech companies, your demo account should show fintech-relevant metrics, not "Acme Corp"
- [ ] Screen resolution and font size set for screen share legibility (1080p, font size ≥14pt in any UI)
- [ ] Backup plan ready: if the live demo environment fails, have a 3-minute recorded walkthrough of the two most critical features ready to play immediately
- [ ] Champion pre-briefed 30 minutes before: "Who's attending that we haven't talked to? Any agenda changes? Any political context I should know going in?"
- [ ] Personalized agenda slide prepared — their company logo, their names, and the 3 pain points they shared in discovery listed as the session agenda items (not generic "overview → demo → Q&A")

**Opening Hook (0–5 minutes) — Do Not Open With "Let Me Walk You Through Our Platform":**

*Option A — Industry Insight Hook (for economic buyers):*
> "[Economic Buyer name], before I share my screen — I want to share one data point that I think will frame the next 45 minutes. [Industry-specific statistic about their pain — e.g., 'According to a recent analysis of 500 SaaS CROs, the average enterprise sales team updates CRM records accurately for only 34% of deals — which means the average pipeline forecast is built on two-thirds guesswork. That's not a people problem; it's a systems problem.'] Based on what [Champion name] told me about your Q3 experience, I suspect that number sounds familiar. Is that a fair read?"

*Option B — Problem Statement Hook (for technical evaluators or end users):*
> "[Technical Lead name], [Champion name] gave me a specific scenario to show you today that I want to validate before I dive in. She described [specific workflow problem in their words — e.g., 'AEs are spending 3+ hours every Friday manually updating Salesforce opportunity fields so you can run an accurate forecast, and by Monday those fields are already stale again']. Is that the right problem to solve today, or has something changed since we last talked?"

*Option C — Mutual Agenda Setting (for mixed audiences):*
> "Before we start — I want to make sure we cover the right things in the time we have. Based on what we discussed with [Champion name], I've structured this around three questions your team is trying to answer: [Pain Point 1 as a question], [Pain Point 2 as a question], [Pain Point 3 as a question]. By the end of the next 45 minutes, you should have a clear answer to all three — and a concrete sense of whether we're the right tool to solve them. Does that agenda work for everyone?"

**Discovery Refresh Questions (5–10 minutes, before screen share):**

*Purpose:* Surface any priority shifts since discovery. A demo built on last week's context and delivered against today's reality misses. These three questions take 4 minutes and save you from 40 minutes of presenting to the wrong pain.

| Question | What It Uncovers | Follow-Up If Answer Surprises You |
|----------|-----------------|----------------------------------|
| "Since [discovery call / your SDR call], has anything changed in terms of what's most urgent for your team right now?" | Priority shifts, new stakeholders, budget changes, competitive timeline acceleration | "That's really helpful context — should I adjust the order of what I show you today to make sure we hit that first?" |
| "When you picture [product outcome — e.g., 'a forecast you're confident presenting to your CFO'], what does that look like in practice for your team?" | Specific success definition that you can anchor your demo to | "Perfect — I'm going to come back to exactly that scenario in about 10 minutes so you can see whether we deliver on it." |
| "Who else, besides the people on this call, would need to say yes before you'd move forward — and what do they need to see?" | Hidden stakeholders, security reviews, procurement process, legal — surface these NOW, not after the demo | "Great — I'll make sure to build a one-pager specifically for [that stakeholder] that we can send to them after today." |

PAIN → FEATURE → PROOF → IMPACT

| Step | Script Pattern | Example |
|------|---------------|---------|
| PAIN | "One of the things [Champion name] mentioned was [specific pain in their words]..." | "One of the things Sarah mentioned was that AEs spend 3 hours every Friday updating Salesforce — and the data is stale by Monday." |
| FEATURE | "Let me show you exactly how [product] solves that. [Navigate to feature.]" | "Let me show you what happens when an AE closes their laptop on Friday. [Navigate to activity capture view.]" |
| PROOF | "What you're seeing here is [explain what's happening on screen in plain language, not product jargon]." | "What you're seeing here is every email, call, and meeting this AE had this week — automatically captured and written to their Salesforce opportunity record. Zero manual entry." |
| IMPACT | "What this means for your team is [business outcome in their language]. Does that solve the [specific pain] scenario you described?" | "What this means is your AEs get their Fridays back — and your forecast is built on actual data, not what an AE remembered to type in on Sunday night. Is that the problem you're solving?" |

Before I do — [Champion name], this is the capability you mentioned was the reason you took this meeting. Can you remind [Economic Buyer name] what you saw that made you want to show them this?"

[Let champion re-sell the feature in their own words — this is worth 10 times more than anything you say.]

Delivery Script (during the feature reveal):
> [Navigate to the specific screen. Pause for 3-5 seconds before speaking. Let them see it before explaining it.]
> "What you're looking at is [plain-language description]. The thing I want you to notice specifically is [point to the most differentiated element — the one thing no competitor shows in a demo]. This is built on [brief technical credibility note — one sentence, no jargon]. [Pause.] [Champion name] — tell them what this means for the workflow you showed me last week."

Expected Reaction Handling:
- If they ask "can you show me that again?" → This is a green flag. Replay it, then say: "What specifically would you want to test in a trial environment with your own data?"
- If they go quiet and take notes → Do not fill the silence. Let them write. Then: "What are you writing down?"
- If the economic buyer leans in or asks a detailed question → This is a buying signal. Note the exact question and address it first in the follow-up email.

**Live Objection Handling During the Demo:**

*Objection 1: "Can you show me [feature that is on your roadmap but not yet built]?"*

*What it actually means:* They've been briefed by a competitor or have a specific use case you haven't addressed. This is a buying signal dressed as an objection.

*Response:*
> "Yes — and I want to give you an honest answer. [Feature X] is [on our roadmap for Q[N] / available in a limited release / available via API today but not surfaced in the UI yet]. Here's what that means for you: if [Feature X] is a hard requirement for Day 1, we should talk about that explicitly before you invest further evaluation time. If it's a nice-to-have, I'd suggest we assess whether [existing capability Y] solves the underlying need. Which scenario are we in? Tell me more about why [Feature X] matters for your workflow."

*Objection 2: "We already have [competing tool / homegrown solution] that does something similar."*

*What it actually means:* They are evaluating you against a sunk cost (existing investment). The real question is: what is the cost of staying with the existing solution?

*Response:*
> "That's exactly why I want to show you this side by side — not to win a feature comparison, but to answer a more important question: what is [existing tool] costing you that you're not counting? The [specific differentiated capability] I'm about to show you — [set up Wow Moment 2 here, since this is the perfect entry point]. If your current tool does this natively, then the switching cost math doesn't favor us. If it doesn't, then the question becomes: what is the cost of the workaround your team currently uses? Let's look at that together."

*Objection 3: "How does this integrate with [specific tool in their stack]?"*

*What it actually means:* Technical evaluator is assessing implementation risk. This question, asked during a demo, is a strong buying signal — they are mentally calculating implementation feasibility, not exit criteria.

*Response:*
> "[Integration name] is [native integration / via API / via Zapier / on our roadmap — be precise]. Let me show you exactly what that looks like. [Navigate to integration documentation or live integration view.] The reason this matters for your team is [specific workflow outcome — e.g., 'every deal update that happens in [Product] automatically syncs to your Salesforce opportunity without your AEs touching it']. For your environment specifically — [Champion name], do you know if [technical requirement — e.g., 'your Salesforce instance uses custom objects or standard objects for pipeline?']. That changes the implementation path slightly and I want to be specific."

*Objection 4: "What does implementation look like? We don't have engineering resources."*

*What it actually means:* They have been burned by a previous SaaS implementation that required months of developer time. This is a fear-based objection that blocks deals in the demo, not the POC stage.

*Response:*
> "The single most important thing I want to show you before we talk implementation is the customer who said the exact same thing. [Navigate to a case study or onboarding timeline for a similar-sized company with no dedicated IT team.] [Company X] went from signed contract to first live dashboard in [N days], with no engineering resources — just a Salesforce admin and one of our onboarding engineers. The reason I'm showing you this is not to pitch you — it's so you can ask me exactly where their experience would differ from yours, based on [their specific tech stack complexity]. What's the part of implementation that concerns you most?"

*Objection 5: "This looks great, but I need to get [other stakeholder] involved before we move forward."*

*What it actually means:* Either a legitimate multi-stakeholder process (positive — they are real), or a deflection to avoid a direct next step (negative — they are not yet sold or not empowered to decide).

*Response:*
> "Completely expected — this decision affects [relevant stakeholders]. Before we wrap today, I want to make sure we give [other stakeholder] everything they need to get up to speed quickly. Two things: First, I'll put together a one-page brief customized specifically for [their title and concerns] — different from what you've seen today, framed for their priorities. Second — [Champion name], what's the best way to get 20 minutes with [other stakeholder] in the next week? I don't need them on a full demo; I just want to answer their top three questions live rather than via email."

#### 3. CLOSING THE DEMO

**Trial Close Question (use 5 minutes before the formal close):**

Do not wait until the end to gauge momentum. Insert this question while showing the last major feature:

> "Based on what you've seen so far — and I know we have a few more things to cover — on a scale of 1 to 10, how relevant is this to the problem you're trying to solve? I'm not asking if you're ready to buy — I'm asking if we're solving the right problem. What's your honest read?"

*Interpretation guide:*
- 8-10: High interest. Move directly to next-step close. Ask: "What would need to happen for you to move from interested to confident in the next two weeks?"
- 5-7: Partial interest. Ask: "What's creating the gap? Is it a use case we haven't shown, a stakeholder you need to involve, or something about the solution itself?" Address the gap before closing.
- 1-4: Low interest. Ask: "That's helpful context — what specifically is missing? Is it that we haven't solved the right problem, or that you have a higher priority problem right now?" Qualify out if appropriate rather than waste a POC slot.

**Next-Step Framework — Two-Path Close:**

Never end a demo with "so what do you think?" or "feel free to reach out if you have questions." Always offer two concrete paths that both advance the deal.

*Path A — High Confidence:*
> "If what you've seen today confirms this is the right direction — here's what I'd propose as a next step: a structured [2-week trial / 3-week POC] with your actual data. We'd spend 30 minutes with your [technical lead / RevOps team] this week to scope the exact workflows you want to test, then you'd have hands-on access within 48 hours. [Champion name] has already been through our onboarding — she can tell you exactly what that looks like from the inside. Does a 30-minute setup call later this week or early next week work for the right people on your end?"

*Path B — Additional Information Needed:*
> "If there are specific questions you want answered before moving to a hands-on evaluation — totally reasonable — here's what I suggest: let me identify the top three scenarios you're still uncertain about and send you a 15-minute walkthrough video of each, customized to your environment. You can share it with [other stakeholders] on your own time. Then we get back together in [3-5 business days] to talk through what you saw. Does that give you what you need to move forward confidently?"

*Commitment-Securing Language (avoid the indefinite close):*

AVOID: "Sounds great — I'll follow up next week."
USE: "Let's lock in something specific. [Champion name], what does your calendar look like [day] at [time] for a 30-minute setup scoping call? I'll send a calendar invite right now so we don't lose the momentum from today."

**Champion Internal Selling Brief (one-page asset, attached to follow-up email):**

### Constraints
- All demo scripts must be conversational — AEs should sound like they're having a conversation, not reading a script. Write for spoken delivery, not written formality.
- Every feature narrative must connect to a specific pain point stated in discovery — no feature orphans
- The wow moments must be sequenced in order of impact on the economic buyer (not the technical evaluator), because the economic buyer's body language determines whether the deal advances
- Follow-up emails must be sent from the AE's email, not a marketing automation system — authenticity matters more than scale at this stage
- The champion internal selling brief must be shareable without edits — it should not contain internal sales language, pricing specifics, or anything the champion would be embarrassed to forward
- All objection handling must be non-defensive and curiosity-led — never argue, always investigate

## Troubleshooting
- **Problem:** The prospect shows up to the demo without the economic buyer they promised would attend, and you're demoing only to technical evaluators.
  **Solution:** Do not run the full economic-buyer demo to a technical-only audience — it will bore them and waste your best material. Reframe in real time: "Perfect — I want to make sure we use this time specifically to pressure-test the technical integration and security requirements, so that when [Economic Buyer name] joins our next session, they're hearing only the business case and not technical setup questions. Let's go deep on [integration / security / data model] today." Then reschedule the executive-facing demo for when the EB can attend. A demo designed for a CRO delivered to a RevOps analyst creates misalignment that kills deals.

- **Problem:** The prospect sits silently through the entire demo, offers no reactions, asks minimal questions, and gives a 5/10 on the trial close.
  **Solution:** Silence in a demo almost always means the presenter is showing features, not solving problems. Stop the demo immediately: "I want to pause here — I'm going to be direct. I notice we're moving through the product but I'm not hearing any reactions that tell me we're solving the right problem. Help me understand: what's the biggest gap between what you've seen and what you were hoping to see?" If they name a specific gap — address it. If they can't name it — you may have qualified the wrong person into the demo. Ask: "Is there a more urgent problem on your team's plate right now than [stated pain]? I'd rather talk about what's actually keeping you up than show you a solution to a problem that isn't top-of-mind."

- **Problem:** The champion loved the demo but the economic buyer rejected the tool before the AE could get them on a separate call.
  **Solution:** The champion did not have the right language to sell internally. This is a champion enablement failure, not a product failure. Request an emergency call with the champion: "Before this is officially off the table — help me understand exactly what [EB name] said when they passed. If it was about pricing, implementation risk, timing, or a specific feature gap, I want to address it directly. Can we get 20 minutes this week? I want to make sure [EB name] made their decision with complete information." Then offer to send the EB a direct 5-minute video addressing their specific concern — the same content the champion tried to explain verbally, now in a format the EB can consume on their schedule.
