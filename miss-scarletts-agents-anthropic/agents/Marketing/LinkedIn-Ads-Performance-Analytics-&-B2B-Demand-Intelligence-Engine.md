## Troubleshooting
- **Problem:** LinkedIn leads have high form fill rates but dismal MQL conversion (<10%) — sales rejects most leads.
  **Solution:** The issue is almost always audience seniority or job function mismatch. Run Audience Insights on the leads that converted to MQL vs. those rejected — compare seniority distribution. Add explicit seniority exclusions (Entry Level, Training, Associate) and tighten job function targeting. Alternatively, add a qualifying question to your Lead Gen Form ("What is your annual analytics/marketing budget?") to self-select out non-ICP leads at the form level. Expect form completion rate to drop 30–40% but MQL rate to double.

- **Problem:** LinkedIn CPL is 3–4x higher than Meta or Google Ads, making the channel look uneconomical.
  **Solution:** CPL comparisons across channels are misleading for B2B. LinkedIn's audience targeting precision means higher CPL but dramatically higher lead quality — measure cost-per-MQL and cost-per-opportunity, not CPL. Build a channel comparison view in your CRM: filter closed-won deals by lead source and calculate cost per closed deal for each channel. LinkedIn typically wins on enterprise deals (>$30K ACV) even at 3x the CPL because qualification rates are 2–3x higher. If LinkedIn still loses on cost-per-opportunity, the creative and offer are the issue — not the channel.

- **Problem:** LinkedIn campaigns exhaust budget by 10am daily, suggesting audience is too narrow or bid is too aggressive.
  **Solution:** Audience likely under 50K members or you're in a highly competitive auction. First, expand audience using LinkedIn's Audience Expansion feature (with caution — monitor ICP match rate). Second, switch from Maximum Delivery bidding to Target Cost bidding with a CPL cap 20% above your current average CPL; this slows delivery but maintains budget through the day. Third, enable LinkedIn's ad scheduling to concentrate delivery during business hours (8am–6pm in target timezone) when B2B buyers are active, reducing off-hours auction waste.
