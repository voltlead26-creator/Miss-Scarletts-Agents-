# AI-Powered B2B SaaS Demand Generation Capacity Planning & Revenue Math Intelligence Engine

**Difficulty:** Advanced | **Time:** 30 min | **Tags:** b2b, demand-generation, capacity-planning, pipeline, revenue-math, budget-allocation, forecasting, headcount

## Overview
Works backward from a B2B SaaS revenue target to calculate exactly how much demand generation activity is required, how to distribute that load across channels, and whether the current team and budget can actually deliver it — producing a defensible capacity plan that finance, the CEO, and the board can scrutinize.

## Example Input/Output

**Input Example:**
- Company: Archway Analytics (Series B, enterprise data integration SaaS)
- New ARR target: $12M
- Marketing-sourced pipeline target: 40% of new ARR
- ACV: $85,000
- Sales cycle: 7 months
- Win rate: 22%
- Pipeline coverage ratio: 3.5x
- Current marketing team: 4 FTEs (1 demand gen manager, 1 content writer, 1 marketing ops, 1 SDR manager)
- Current budget: $1.4M/year
- Top challenge: "We've been hitting MQL targets but missing pipeline targets — sales says our MQLs are garbage"

**Output Example (Revenue Waterfall):**

**The Math Archway Actually Needs to Hit $12M ARR:**

New ARR Target: $12,000,000
Less expected expansion ARR (20% of base): -$2,400,000 (from existing customer upsell — not marketing's job to source)
New logo ARR required: $9,600,000
New logos required: 113 logos = $9,600,000 ÷ $85,000 ACV

Marketing-sourced logos (40%): 45 logos
Pipeline required to close 45 logos: $17,400,000 = (45 × $85,000) ÷ 22% win rate
Pipeline with 3.5x coverage buffer: $60,900,000

**This is the real number: Marketing must generate $60.9M in pipeline to hit their share of $12M ARR.**

Opportunities required: 717 = $60,900,000 ÷ $85,000
Per-month opportunity creation: 60 opportunities/month
Current marketing-sourced opportunities: 22/month (based on stated conversion rates)
Gap: 38 additional opportunities per month — a 2.7x increase from current output

Leads required: 400/month = 60 opportunities ÷ 15% MQL-to-Opp rate
Current leads: 180/month
Lead gap: 220 additional MQLs per month

**Critical diagnosis before building the plan:**
The stated problem ("MQLs aren't converting to opportunities") points to a 15% MQL-to-Opp rate — which is actually the B2B SaaS benchmark. The real problem is the VOLUME: Archway needs 400 MQLs/month, not 180. The MQL quality complaint from sales may be a red herring — the actual issue is that 180 MQLs simply cannot produce enough pipeline volume to hit $60.9M, regardless of quality. Double the volume, keep the quality, and the pipeline gap closes.

**Channel Allocation for Archway:**

Given $85K ACV and 7-month sales cycle, recommended channel mix:
1. ABM (Primary): 35% of budget — accounts with $100K+ ACV potential respond better to coordinated ABM than inbound
2. Paid Search (Secondary): 25% — strong demand capture for data integration category queries
3. Content/SEO (Secondary): 20% — 6-12 month lag, but compounding asset for year 3+
4. Events (Tertiary): 15% — 2 owned executive dinners/quarter + 1 major sponsorship
5. PLG/Self-Serve: Not applicable at this ACV — self-serve CAC math doesn't work above $50K ACV

**Headcount plan:**
Current 4 FTEs can execute $25-30M pipeline/year at maximum efficiency with the right tools. To reach $60.9M:
- Add 1 senior demand gen manager (paid media + ABM): Month 2 hire, full capacity Month 5
- Add 2 SDRs (inbound qualification): Month 1 hire, full capacity Month 4
- Add 1 content writer: Month 3 hire, SEO pipeline contribution begins Month 9
- Add 1 ABM/field marketer: Month 2 hire, full capacity Month 5

**Q1 honest expectation with ramp:**
At current team of 4 FTEs + new hires ramping, Q1 pipeline output will be approximately $9-11M (not $15M per model). Board must be set on realistic Q1 expectations before plan is approved, with Q3-Q4 weighted heavily. This is a 12-month compound growth model, not a linear one.

## Success Metrics

- Capacity plan accepted by CFO and CEO without material revision (measures quality of the math and communication)
- Pipeline actuals within ±15% of model in each quarter (measures model accuracy)
- No mid-year budget emergency due to unforecasted channel underperformance (measures risk planning quality)

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Marketing hits the MQL target every month but pipeline targets are consistently missed, creating ongoing tension with sales and CFO**
Solution: This is almost always a math problem masquerading as a quality problem. Run the full revenue waterfall from Deliverable 1 to check whether the MQL volume target was ever sufficient to produce the required pipeline, given the actual MQL-to-Opp conversion rate. In most cases, the MQL target was set by working forward from a budget constraint ("we can afford to generate 200 MQLs/month") rather than backward from a pipeline requirement ("we need 400 MQLs/month to generate $15M in pipeline"). The fix is not to improve MQL quality — it's to recalibrate the MQL target against the pipeline math and either increase volume or explicitly reduce the marketing-sourced pipeline commitment to match what 200 MQLs can actually deliver. Bringing the math to the conversation replaces opinion-based debates with arithmetic.

**Problem: The capacity plan gets approved in Q4 planning, but by Q2 it's clear the plan was too optimistic — pipeline is tracking 30-40% below model and there's no clear recovery path**
Solution: The most common cause is failing to model ramp time and pipeline lag. New SDRs hired in January contribute minimal pipeline in Q1 and Q2. Content published in Q1 produces SEO traffic in Q3 at the earliest. Events in Q1 generate pipeline in Q2 at best. If these lags weren't built into the quarterly model, Q1-Q2 pipeline will always underperform while Q3-Q4 overperforms — and boards will panic mid-year before the payoff arrives. Prevention: Build a month-by-month pipeline projection in Deliverable 5, not an annual total. Recovery when already off-track: Activate the highest-velocity demand gen activities available (paid search, LinkedIn, SDR outbound) since these produce pipeline in weeks not months, and buy time for the longer-horizon investments to compound.

**Problem: Finance is asking marketing to justify headcount additions, but the pipeline math shows you need 3 more FTEs and they're only approving 1**
Solution: Reframe the headcount conversation as a pipeline shortfall, not a resource request. Show finance the capacity model from Deliverable 3: with current headcount, the team can sustainably generate $[X]M in pipeline per year. The revenue target requires $[X]M. The gap is $[X]M in pipeline — which requires either [X] FTEs, a $[X]M budget increase in agency/contractor spend, or a reduction of $[X]M in the marketing-sourced pipeline commitment. Give them three options, not one ask. Finance almost always prefers to understand the trade-off rather than approve or deny a number without context. If only one headcount is approved, use the model to show explicitly which channel will be underfunded and how much pipeline risk that creates — then revisit at mid-year review with the data to support the remaining asks.
