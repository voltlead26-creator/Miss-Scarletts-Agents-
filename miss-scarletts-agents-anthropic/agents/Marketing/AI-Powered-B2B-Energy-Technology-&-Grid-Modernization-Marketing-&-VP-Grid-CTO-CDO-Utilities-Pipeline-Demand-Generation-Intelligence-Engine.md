## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Meetings are consistently with IT integration teams but Grid Operations directors won't engage — deals stall when IT can't build the grid reliability business case for capital approval**
Solution: This is the most common failure mode in utility technology sales — IT sponsors are technically capable of evaluating integration complexity but fundamentally cannot build a regulatory economics business case for a capital investment. Immediately deploy a parallel Grid Operations engagement track alongside IT relationship. First, equip your IT champion with Grid Operations-specific content: a "Grid Operations Executive Brief" summarizing the feeder-level reliability analysis your platform produces in plain operations language (SAIDI impact, DER dispatch latency reduction, NERC CIP control coverage) — give the IT champion the tools to brief upward credibly. Second, request a dedicated "Grid Operations Architecture Review" meeting — positioned as a technical peer discussion between your grid engineering team and their Grid Operations engineers, not a sales call — at the Technical Qualification stage. Third, introduce your VP Grid Technology directly to their VP Grid Operations or Director of Grid Modernization via a brief warm LinkedIn message referencing a specific operational challenge in their territory (DER penetration on specific feeders, recent reliability events visible in public NERC filings). Track Grid Operations engagement separately in CRM and make "Grid Operations Executive Briefing Completed" a required gate before advancing to Pilot.

**Problem: Rate case justification is blocking capital approval — Regulatory Affairs team is skeptical of vendor-provided ROI projections and is requiring third-party validation before recommending rate case inclusion**
Solution: Regulatory Affairs teams are expert economists and engineers who have seen hundreds of optimistic vendor ROI projections fail to materialize — their skepticism is professionally warranted. Do not argue with or try to overcome this skepticism. Instead, make your ROI model transparent and auditable: (1) Build your pilot program to generate utility-owned grid performance data as the evidence base — every pilot deliverable should produce data that belongs to the utility, not data locked in your platform; (2) Offer to have your SAIDI/SAIFI methodology reviewed by an independent consultant the utility selects (Navigant/Guidehouse, KEMA, Black & Veatch) at your expense — nothing signals confidence in your ROI model like offering independent validation; (3) Connect Regulatory Affairs teams with your existing utility reference customers' Regulatory Affairs peers directly for unscripted peer-to-peer discussion of actual rate case outcomes; (4) Provide your complete statistical methodology documentation including confidence intervals and sensitivity analysis — Regulatory Affairs teams specifically look for honest uncertainty quantification as a signal of analytical credibility. Utilities that have included your technology in rate cases and received PUC approval are your most powerful sales asset — build a "Rate Case Approval" reference library and make it available to prospects in active regulatory evaluation.

**Problem: Long 18–24 month sales cycles are making it difficult to maintain pipeline momentum — deals go dark after initial briefings and resurface only when the procurement process formally advances**
Solution: Utility procurement cycles are structurally long because they are driven by regulatory timelines (IRP cycles, rate case filing dates, compliance deadlines) that have no relationship to your quarterly targets. Trying to compress them through sales pressure creates immediate credibility damage with utility procurement teams who understand their own regulatory constraints. Instead, design your engagement model around regulatory milestone-based touchpoints: (1) Map every active opportunity to its specific regulatory trigger timeline (next IRP filing, rate case filing date, NERC audit window, state mandate deadline) — these dates are publicly available in PUC dockets; (2) Provide continuous value delivery between formal procurement stages — monthly technical intelligence brief relevant to their specific operational situation, alerts when FERC/NERC regulatory developments affect their compliance posture; (3) Formalize a "Grid Intelligence Subscription" — offer prospects access to your regulatory monitoring platform or research reports as a no-cost relationship maintainer while their procurement process proceeds at its regulatory pace; (4) Establish quarterly "Grid Advisory Check-in" calls with VP Grid Operations contacts — not sales calls, but 30-minute technical discussions on their current operational challenges — these relationships convert when the procurement window opens; (5) Track "Regulatory Milestone" dates in Salesforce for every active opportunity and set automated reminders to reach out with relevant content 60 days before each milestone — be present with value exactly when their attention is focused on the relevant decision.
