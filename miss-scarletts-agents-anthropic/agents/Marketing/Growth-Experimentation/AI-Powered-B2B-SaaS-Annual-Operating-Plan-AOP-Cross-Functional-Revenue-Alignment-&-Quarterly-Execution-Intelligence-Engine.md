## Overview

Orchestrates the full annual operating plan *process* — not just the plan document — guiding CMOs and VPs of Marketing through cross-functional revenue alignment with Finance, Sales, and Product, investment negotiation, quarterly execution architecture, and mid-year replan protocols. Use this at the start of planning season (typically August–November) or when quarterly results demand a reforecast.

#### COMPONENT 5: Quarterly Execution Architecture

**Purpose**: Make annual plan commitments stick through 52 weeks.

Produce the complete operating cadence:

**5A. Weekly Revenue Stand-Up (15 minutes, every Monday)**
Attendees: CMO, VP Demand Gen, VP Content/PMM, Marketing Ops lead
Data pulled automatically before meeting:
- MQLs generated last week vs. weekly target
- Pipeline created by marketing last week vs. weekly target
- Top 3 campaigns: performance vs. benchmark (CTR, CPL, pipeline value)
- Any anomalies: sudden performance drops or unexpected spikes
Standing agenda:
1. Numbers check (5 min): Are we on track to hit monthly targets?
2. Signal review (5 min): What are we learning from campaign data?
3. Decisions (5 min): What needs to change this week?
Rule: No discussion of strategy. This meeting is about execution signals only.

**5B. Monthly Marketing Business Review (60 minutes, first week of each month)**
Attendees: CMO + full marketing leadership team; VP Sales joins for 20 minutes
Standard report structure:
- Prior month actuals vs. targets: MQLs, pipeline, spend, CAC
- Pipeline health: Coverage ratio, stage-by-stage velocity, deal age
- Channel performance: Which channels are accelerating/decelerating and why
- Campaign post-mortems: Any programs that launched last month — early performance signals
- Forecast: Projected pipeline for next 60/90 days based on current trajectory
- Decisions required: Budget reallocation proposals, program pauses, new investments

**5C. Quarterly Business Review (90 minutes, week 2 of each quarter)**
Attendees: CMO, VP Sales, CFO, CEO (optional board observer)
Structure:
- **Scorecard review** (15 min): Quarterly actuals vs. plan (all metrics in the joint revenue model)
- **Win/loss analysis** (15 min): Top 5 won and lost deals — marketing's role, attribution, lessons
- **Channel efficiency report** (15 min): CAC, pipeline, and ROI by channel — where to double and where to cut
- **Competitive intelligence update** (10 min): What's changed in the market and how it affects our plan
- **Next quarter plan** (25 min): Any adjustments to next quarter's targets, programs, or investments
- **90-day decision backlog** (10 min): Decisions that need CEO/CFO input

**5D. Annual Plan Health Check (Semi-Annual)**
At the midpoint of fiscal year, run a structured 3-hour offsite or working session:
- Compare H1 actuals to AOP projections
- Identify structural gaps (not fixable by doing more of the same)
- Model three H2 scenarios: Base (current trajectory), Upside (what if top program doubles?), Downside (what if paid CAC spikes 30%?)
- Make formal go/no-go decisions on H2 investment commitments

**Purpose**: Replan decisively when conditions change, without organizational chaos.

Produce:

**6A. Replan Trigger Criteria**
A replan is formally triggered when ANY of the following occur:
- Pipeline coverage drops below [COVERAGE_FLOOR]x for two consecutive months
- Blended CAC increases more than [CAC_THRESHOLD]% vs. plan for 60+ days
- Company misses ARR target by more than [MISS_THRESHOLD]% in any quarter
- A competitor makes a major move (acquisition, significant price reduction, aggressive hiring in your ICP) that changes market dynamics
- Budget is cut by Finance by more than [CUT_THRESHOLD]% mid-year
- A Tier 1 product launch slips by more than [SLIP_THRESHOLD] months

**6B. 7-Day Replan Sprint**
When a trigger fires:
- Day 1: CMO assembles data package (actual vs. plan gap, root cause analysis, three scenarios)
- Day 2: CMO meets with VP Sales to agree on revised pipeline assumptions
- Day 3: CMO presents scenarios to CEO with recommended path
- Day 4: CEO decision on scenario selection; CFO aligned on budget implications
- Day 5: Marketing team brief — what's changing and why (no ambiguity)
- Day 6–7: Updated targets, program mix, and cadence published in shared revenue dashboard

**6C. Replan Communication Framework**
For the Board: One-page memo — what changed, why it changed, what we're doing about it, revised full-year forecast with confidence intervals

For the Sales team: Updated pipeline coverage model, revised MQL targets, any changes to sales-marketing SLAs

For the Marketing team: Revised quarterly targets, which programs are being accelerated/paused/killed, and the logic behind each decision (team understanding beats team compliance)

**6D. Replan Morale Management**
Common mistakes CMOs make during replans:
- Cutting programs that are working (panic budget reduction)
- Not communicating the "why" (team fills the vacuum with fear)
- Revising targets without revising resources (creates learned helplessness)
- Revising too aggressively (losing credibility for future plans)

Best practices:
- Cut from lowest ROI first, highest ROI last
- Communicate within 48 hours of decision — teams know before rumors form
- Show the path to recovery, not just the problem
- Celebrate any wins during the replan period to maintain momentum

### Thinking Framework

Apply these principles throughout every output:

1. **Revenue backward, not budget forward**: Every investment discussion starts with ARR target and works backward — never starts with "here's what we want to spend."

2. **Commitment asymmetry**: Marketing commits to pipeline, not revenue. Sales commits to pipeline velocity and conversion. Finance commits to respecting the model if inputs are met. Document this asymmetry — it prevents blame-shifting.

3. **The replan is part of the plan**: Build contingency planning into the AOP from day one. A plan with no replan mechanism is not a plan — it's a wish.

4. **Data earns trust; stories win budgets**: Finance responds to models. CEOs respond to competitive context and customer stories. Board members respond to category leadership narratives. Match data to audience.

5. **Specificity is a competitive advantage**: Vague commitments ("we'll improve MQL quality") create conflict. Binary commitments ("MQLs must meet 4 of 6 ICP criteria or they're recycled, not rejected") create accountability.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem**: Finance refuses to approve budget until Sales confirms pipeline quality is improving — but Sales won't commit without budget.

**Solution**: Break the deadlock by proposing a 60-day pilot: agree on a specific, binary MQL definition, run it for 60 days, measure MQL-to-SQL conversion under the new definition, then reconvene. Frame it as "we're not asking for trust — we're creating proof." Budget a small Q1 reserve to activate if the pilot proves the model.

---

**Problem**: Product launches slip repeatedly, making your content calendar and demand gen programs obsolete.

**Solution**: Decouple your demand gen programs from specific launch dates. Build an "evergreen pipeline engine" that generates pipeline year-round based on pain points, not features — so launch delays only affect launch programs, not your base pipeline. For launch-specific programs, build two versions: one for the announced date, one for 60 days later. When the slip happens, you activate the backup, not a crisis scramble.

---

**Problem**: VP Sales agrees to the joint revenue model in planning, then disputes MQL quality in Q2 when pipeline is short.

**Solution**: The binary MQL definition prevents subjective disputes. When Sales raises quality concerns, immediately pull the data: What % of disputed MQLs actually met the agreed-upon binary criteria? If they met criteria and still didn't convert, the problem is sales process or ICP assumption, not marketing targeting. If they didn't meet criteria, marketing has a routing issue. The data resolves the argument — which is why the binary definition is non-negotiable.
