# Research Report to Demand Generation Engine - Original Research Atomization & Pipeline System

**Difficulty:** Advanced | **Time:** 25 min | **Tags:** original-research, demand-gen, content-repurposing, earned-media, b2b, thought-leadership, lead-gen, data-driven, pr, sales-enablement

## Overview
Takes a completed B2B original research report or survey dataset and generates a complete demand generation ecosystem: a gated landing page brief, an executive summary for press distribution, a data-driven press release, five LinkedIn data revelation posts, a three-issue email nurture sequence, three SEO blog articles (each exploring a distinct finding), a sales enablement data talk track, LinkedIn and Meta ad copy, an infographic brief, a media pitch template, and a 90-day earned media distribution calendar — all output as immediately deployable assets ready to load into your CMS, CRM, email platform, PR wire service, and sales enablement tool.

OUTPUT SPECIFICATIONS:

**Module 1 — Gated Landing Page Brief**

Format: Full creative brief for the landing page team (or for direct CMS implementation)

Requirements:
- Page goal: Maximum form fills from ICP visitors — every element serves conversion
- Above-the-fold structure:
  - H1 headline: Lead with the most alarming/provocative finding — not the report title (e.g., "72% of Buyers Have Already Decided Before Your Sales Rep Gets Involved" beats "The 2026 State of B2B Buyer Behavior Report")
  - H2 subheadline: Who this report is for + what they'll walk away knowing (1 sentence, ICP-specific)
  - Form placement: Above the fold, right column — name, work email, company, job title (maximum 4 fields — every additional field reduces conversion 11%)
  - Form CTA button: Specific, benefit-forward (e.g., "Get the 2026 Buyer Behavior Data" — not "Submit" or "Download")
  - Trust signals below the form: Respondent count, methodology credibility statement, logos of notable companies represented in the data
- Benefit section (below the fold):
  - 3-4 "What you'll discover" bullets — each a complete finding stated as a direct benefit (e.g., "Why 3 out of 4 deals are now lost before your first discovery call — and the one signal that predicts which accounts are still winnable")
  - Preview data visualization: One chart or data callout that shows enough value to make the gate feel worth it — but leaves the most valuable findings behind the form
- Social proof section: 2 customer quotes that validate the research methodology and/or the relevance of the findings to the ICP's real experience
- FAQ section (optional but recommended): 3 questions addressing "Is this relevant to my industry/company size?" "What's the methodology?" "How is this different from [competitor research]?"
- Meta title + description (for SEO and social sharing): Primary keyword front-loaded; the meta description should read like a compelling tweet — not a document description

---

**Module 2 — Executive Summary (500 words, press-ready)**

Format: Standalone document, formatted for distribution to press, analysts, and customers

Requirements:
- Opening paragraph: State the research program, methodology credibility, and the headline finding in 3 sentences — lead with the most alarming number, not with "we are pleased to present"
- Background section (50-75 words): Why this research matters now — what market shift or business problem makes this data timely and urgent for the reader
- Key findings (5-7 bullet points): Each finding stated as a complete, data-anchored insight — not just a percentage, but the implication (e.g., "72% of B2B buyers complete their vendor evaluation before engaging sales — meaning the conventional outbound playbook is now reaching buyers after the decision is effectively made")
- Methodology summary (75-100 words): Enough detail to establish credibility without requiring a statistics degree — sample size, respondent profile, fielding dates, confidence interval
- Company context (50 words): One paragraph on the sponsoring company — what they do, why this research is relevant to their product, and where to go for more information
- Contact information + report URL

---

**Module 3 — Data-Driven Press Release (400-500 words, AP style)**

Format: Full press release, ready to send to PR wire services (PR Newswire, BusinessWire, GlobeNewswire) and to individual reporters as a pitch attachment

Requirements:
- Headline: Lead with the most newsworthy number — not the report title. "New Study: 72% of B2B Buyers Finalize Vendor Choice Before Speaking to Sales, Exposing Fatal Flaw in Traditional Outbound Strategy" beats "Gong Releases 2026 State of B2B Buyer Behavior Report"
- Dateline: [City, Date] — format exactly as AP style requires
- Lead paragraph: 5W structure — who, what, when, where, why — in under 50 words. The most important information first.
- Body paragraph 1: The headline finding with full context and year-over-year comparison (if available)
- Body paragraph 2: A second, supporting finding that builds the narrative case
- Quote 1 (company executive): CMO or CRO — speaks to the business implication, not the data itself. The best executive quotes interpret data, not restate it. (e.g., "This data confirms what we've been hearing from our customers for 18 months — the playbooks that worked in 2022 are actively hurting conversion rates in 2026. The implications for how teams should be spending their pipeline budget are significant.")
- Body paragraph 3: Third finding + its implication for the ICP's daily reality
- Quote 2 (customer or industry voice, if available): A practitioner who validates the findings from their own experience — this is the quote journalists are most likely to use
- Boilerplate: Company description (3 sentences — who you are, what you do, who you serve) + website URL + contact information
- ### (standard press release end marker)

---

**Module 4 — LinkedIn Data Revelation Posts (5 posts)**

Format: 5 complete LinkedIn posts, each anchored to a different finding, ready to schedule across a 30-day window

Post structure for each:
- Opening line: The data point stated as a standalone provocation — one number, one stark claim, in under 15 words. No "I'm excited to share our report" — open with the finding as if it's the most important thing you've said this week.
- Context (2-3 short paragraphs): Why this number is alarming, what conventional wisdom it overturns, what it means for someone in the ICP's role reading this right now
- Implication (1-2 paragraphs): What a smart leader should DO with this information — make the reader feel slightly behind if they haven't already acted
- Call to action: Soft — "Full data and methodology in the report — link in comments" (never in the first paragraph of the post; LinkedIn suppresses link-in-post reach)
- Engagement hook: End with a question that invites responses from people who recognize the dynamic being described
- Format discipline: Short sentences. Single-sentence paragraphs. Line breaks after every 2 sentences maximum. 1,200-1,500 characters total. No bullet lists in the first 5 lines.

Post angles to cover across the 5:
1. The headline finding (most provocative — use for launch day)
2. The counterintuitive finding (most surprising — deploy on day 7-10 when launch hype fades)
3. The year-over-year trend (urgency angle — "this has gotten dramatically worse in 12 months")
4. The segment-specific finding (targeted angle — speak directly to enterprise or mid-market readers)
5. The "what good looks like" finding (prescriptive angle — what the top 25% of respondents do differently)

**Module 5 — 3-Email Nurture Sequence**

Format: Full email copy for all 3 emails, ready to load into your MAP (HubSpot, Marketo, Pardot, etc.)

*Email 1 — The Teaser (send 5-7 days before launch, to existing list)*
- Subject line: A/B pair — Version A: The headline finding as a statement of alarm. Version B: "We surveyed 1,247 B2B buyers. What they told us about [topic] is going to make you rethink [current assumption]."
- Preview text: Complements but doesn't repeat the subject line — add a second alarming data point
- Body (150-200 words): One paragraph on what the research is, one paragraph on the single most provocative finding (stated as a cliffhanger — give them 60% of the insight), one CTA to register for the launch webinar or join the waitlist for the report
- PS: The secondary finding — brief, and also left unresolved to drive curiosity

*Email 2 — Report Delivery (send on launch day, to all form-fills)*
- Subject line: Deliver what was promised + surface the report's highest-value section ("Your 2026 Buyer Behavior Report — and the one chart that changes how I think about outbound")
- Preview text: Personal, warm — as if sent from a human, not a brand
- Body (200-250 words): Personal intro from the CMO or research lead (2 sentences), the 3 most important findings in bullet form (each with a direct download link to the specific page of the PDF), a call out to the "action section" of the report (where you turn findings into specific recommendations), a primary CTA (the download link) + secondary CTA (book a 30-min data debrief call with a sales rep — most researched offer in launch sequences)
- P.S.: An invitation to forward the report to a colleague who "is wrestling with [the exact problem the research describes]"

*Email 3 — The Follow-Up Insight (send 14 days after delivery, to all who opened Email 2)*
- Subject line: Surface a finding they likely skimmed — "The finding on page 14 of the report that most people missed"
- Preview text: The finding, stated plainly — reward the open with information, not a tease
- Body (150-200 words): One finding that gets overlooked in the headline finding's shadow, explained with enough context to make it immediately actionable, a reference to a blog post or sales enablement asset that takes the finding further, and a soft CTA to the secondary offer (newsletter subscribe or product demo, depending on the list segment)

**Module 6 — 3 SEO-Targeted Blog Articles (900-1,200 words each)**

Format: Full articles, ready to publish on the company blog, each targeting a distinct search intent

Requirements for each article:
- This is NOT a report summary — each article should be independently valuable to someone who never downloads the report. The research findings are the backbone, not the substance.
- Headline: Target a specific search query the ICP would use when experiencing the problem the finding describes (e.g., "Why B2B Sales Cycles Are Getting Longer in 2026 (And What the Data Says About Fixing It)")
- Article structure:
  - Intro (100-150 words): Open with the real-world scenario the reader is experiencing — put them in the problem before presenting the data
  - Section 1 (200-250 words): The finding + its full context. What the data says, what methodology supports it, why it's statistically significant.
  - Section 2 (200-250 words): Why this is happening — the causal mechanism behind the finding. This is where expert analysis (your CMO, CRO, or a customer quote) adds depth beyond the data.
  - Section 3 (200-250 words): What high performers do differently — findings from the top quartile of respondents, or actionable recommendations drawn from the data
  - Conclusion (100-150 words): Summary + a forward-looking challenge question + CTA to download the full report (with a specific mention of what additional data they'll find beyond what this article covered)
  - Internal link suggestions: 2-3 content categories this article should link to
  - External link suggestions: 2-3 authoritative sources that corroborate the finding (for SEO credibility)

Article angles to cover across the 3:
1. The headline finding (broadest audience, highest search volume — targets "state of [industry]" type queries)
2. The most actionable finding (decision-maker angle — targets "[problem] solutions" or "how to [fix specific issue]" queries)
3. The year-over-year trend finding (urgency angle — targets "2026 [topic]" queries and establishes the research as current and authoritative)

**Module 7 — Sales Enablement Data Talk Track**

Format: A structured guide for SDRs and AEs, ready to distribute via Highspot, Seismic, Guru, or Notion

Structure:
- One-page overview (for Slack/email distribution to the sales team): Report title, the 3 most powerful data points for sales conversations, and instructions for how to use them
- Cold email data hooks (5 variants, each using a different finding): Each hook is 2-3 sentences that can be inserted into any outbound email sequence. The hook must: (a) reference the data point without sounding like an unsolicited research paper, (b) connect the finding directly to a problem the prospect is likely experiencing, (c) position the data as a reason to have a conversation — not as a reason to download a report.
  - Example format: "New research we just published found that [finding]. For [company name], given [what you know about their context from your research], that likely means [specific implication]. Worth a 15-minute call to share what the top 25% of [ICP title] are doing differently?"
- Discovery call question extensions (3 questions, each grounded in a different finding): Questions an AE can ask in a discovery call that (a) demonstrate they've done research, (b) help qualify the account against the problem the report describes, (c) open the door to a conversation about how the company solves that problem
  - Example: "Our research found that 72% of buyers complete their vendor evaluation before their first sales call. When your team last lost a deal at [prospect company], where in the process did you find out the prospect had already made up their mind? How are you currently trying to get in earlier?"
- Objection data responses (2-3 common objections, each answered using data from the report): If a prospect says "our sales cycle isn't that long" or "we don't have that problem" — here's how to use the data to productively challenge the assumption without being confrontational
- Executive-level data talking points (for AE + executive sponsor conversations): 3 board-ready statistics that a VP or CRO can use internally to justify a change in approach — these are the data points that get forwarded to a CEO or CFO as justification for a purchase

**Module 8 — Paid Social Ad Copy (LinkedIn + Meta)**

Format: Full ad copy for both platforms, 3 variants each, ready to upload to LinkedIn Campaign Manager and Meta Ads Manager

*LinkedIn Ad Copy (3 variants)*

For each variant:
- Introductory text (150 characters): The data hook — state the finding in a single, alarming sentence that stops a scroll in a professional context

- CTA button: "Download Now" or "Get the Report" (not "Learn More")

Variant angles:
- Variant A: The headline finding (broadest reach — for awareness campaigns targeting the full ICP)
- Variant B: The counterintuitive finding (highest CTR potential — for retargeting campaigns targeting website visitors who haven't converted)
- Variant C: The year-over-year trend (urgency angle — for bottom-of-funnel campaigns targeting known prospects)

*Meta Ad Copy (3 variants)*

For each variant:
- Primary text (125 characters visible before "See More"): The data hook stated as a provocative question or bold assertion — social media context requires more directness and less assumed credibility than LinkedIn
- Headline (40 characters): Benefit-forward, action-oriented
- Description (30 characters): Report credibility signal (e.g., "1,200+ B2B leaders surveyed")
- CTA button: "Download" or "Get Report"

**Module 9 — Infographic Brief**

Format: A complete design brief for your in-house designer or agency, covering all content and structure decisions so the designer can execute without a brief revision cycle

Structure:
- Title treatment: The headline finding as the infographic's title — not the report name
- Hierarchy of information:
  - Hero data point (largest visual element): The headline finding, displayed as a large number or percentage with a one-line interpretation
  - Supporting data points (4-6 elements, medium size): Each finding displayed as a data visualization type recommendation (bar chart / donut chart / comparison numbers / timeline progression) with a brief explanatory label
  - Trend section (if applicable): Year-over-year comparison displayed as a directional arrow or side-by-side bar chart
  - "What top performers do differently" section: 2-3 prescriptive data points from the top quartile — this gives the infographic a forward-looking conclusion rather than ending on a problem statement
- Brand elements: Color palette guidelines (primary finding in brand primary color, supporting findings in secondary palette), logo placement, source attribution at the bottom
- Sharing specs: Dimensions for LinkedIn (1200×627px horizontal), Instagram (1080×1080px square), and the full vertical version for website/blog embedding (800×2400px)
- Caption templates (pre-written for each platform): Ready-to-use captions for the infographic post on each platform, so the social team doesn't have to write copy when the design is done

**Module 10 — 90-Day Earned Media & Distribution Calendar**

Format: Week-by-week content calendar covering all 9 asset types, with specific distribution actions for each

Structure:
- Pre-launch (Days -14 to -1):
  - Analyst briefings: Which analysts to brief pre-embargo, talking points for the briefing, embargo date
  - Journalist outreach: 5 target publications, each with a personalized pitch angle drawn from a specific finding that aligns with their editorial beat
  - Customer validation: Which referenceable customers to brief pre-launch to secure attribution quotes for the press release
  - Sales team briefing: The internal launch — how to brief the sales team on the data and the talk track before the public launch
- Launch week (Days 1-7):
  - Day 1: Press release distribution + LinkedIn headline finding post (CEO or CMO account) + email teaser to full list + report live on gated landing page
  - Day 2-3: Follow-up journalist outreach + LinkedIn post 2 + paid social campaign goes live
  - Day 5: Blog Article 1 goes live + LinkedIn promotion of article
  - Day 7: Review press coverage and engagement data — identify which findings are getting traction and prioritize those in week 2 content
- Week 2-4:
  - LinkedIn posts 3, 4, 5 (spaced 3-5 days apart to avoid fatigue)
  - Blog Articles 2 and 3 (days 14 and 21)
  - Email nurture sequence: Report delivery (day 1) and follow-up insight (day 14)
  - Infographic distribution: LinkedIn + Instagram + Pinterest (day 10)
  - Sales team check-in: Are the talk tracks being used? Collect early feedback.
- Days 30-60:
  - Repackage for dark social: Specific findings for relevant Slack communities (RevOps Co-op, Pavilion, G2 community), Reddit threads, and Discord servers where the ICP congregates — frame as sharing data, not promoting a report
  - Analyst relations follow-up: Provide findings to Gartner/Forrester contacts as supporting data for their research
  - Conference speaking abstract: Use findings to pitch a speaking slot at 2-3 industry conferences where the ICP attends (lead with the data, not the company story)
  - Customer newsletter version: A version of the executive summary formatted for distribution to existing customers — positions you as a thought leader, not just a vendor
- Days 60-90:
  - "What we learned 60 days later" refresh content: A LinkedIn post or blog article reflecting on how the market has responded to the findings and what new questions the data has raised
  - SDR sequence refresh: New email touches using findings that weren't in the original launch sequence — re-engage prospects who downloaded the report but didn't convert
  - Partner co-promotion: Share findings with technology partners and request co-distribution (they get content value, you get audience reach)
  - Planning note for next edition: Document which findings drove the highest engagement and which research gaps were surfaced in comments, replies, and sales conversations — this becomes the brief for the next research cycle

---

CONSTRAINTS:
- The gated landing page must never summarize all key findings above the fold — give enough to compel the download, hold back enough to justify the gate
- The press release must be distributed under embargo to top-tier journalists at least 48 hours before public launch — never release to press wire simultaneously with public launch if you want coverage
- LinkedIn posts must never paste a link in the post body — always instruct to add in the first comment, as LinkedIn's algorithm suppresses reach for posts with external links in the body
- All email copy must be written in first-person singular from a named human sender — "I found something in the data that I think will change how you're thinking about your Q2 pipeline" outperforms "We are pleased to share our annual research report" by 4-6x in open and click rates
- The sales talk track must never instruct a rep to "share the report" as an outreach tactic — data hooks must be used to start conversations, not to send PDFs to prospects who didn't ask for them
- Every distribution action in the 90-day calendar must include a named owner role (Marketing, PR, Sales, Executive) so the calendar functions as an operational project plan, not just a content schedule

## Success Metrics

- **Gated report downloads (first 30 days):** Target 400-1,200 downloads depending on list size and paid promotion budget; use 1-3% of your email list as a benchmark for what's achievable through owned channels alone — paid social should add 30-60% on top
- **MQL conversion rate:** Industry benchmark for gated research reports is 12-18% MQL rate from downloads; reports with a direct connection to a product pain point consistently hit the high end; calculate MQL cost against content investment to establish research ROI
- **Press coverage:** Target 3-5 articles in tier-1 B2B media within 30 days; measure by total earned impressions from press coverage; a single HBR or Forbes placement can deliver 500K-2M impressions; track using Google Alerts + Mention
- **LinkedIn post performance:** Data revelation posts on in-demand B2B topics typically achieve 15,000-50,000 impressions per post from a company page with 20,000+ followers; executive personal posts with the same data can achieve 2-5x more; measure engagement rate (comments + reposts ÷ impressions), targeting 3-5%

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: The research findings feel generic or already well-known — the data confirms what the market already believes rather than challenging it, and journalists and LinkedIn audiences are not engaging with the launch content.**
Solution: Before launching, run a "provocation test" on your headline finding: share the statistic with 5 people in your ICP and ask "Is this surprising to you?" If 4 out of 5 say "no, that's about what I expected," the finding is not newsworthy — it's validating. Go back into your data and look for subgroup findings that tell a different story: findings that are true for a specific company size, industry, or job title that contradict the overall trend. A finding that's surprising for a specific segment ("Mid-market companies with 50-200 AEs have seen their average deal cycle DECREASE by 12% in 2026 — the opposite of the overall trend") is more newsworthy than a broad finding everyone already suspected. If your data is genuinely confirmatory, reframe the launch angle around urgency rather than surprise: "We all knew this was happening. We didn't know how fast. Here's the number."

**Problem: The gated landing page is generating traffic but conversion rates are below 15% — people are reading the page and leaving without downloading.**
Solution: The most common cause of this pattern is a mismatch between the audience the traffic is coming from and the ICP the report was written for (wrong audience) or a headline finding that's visible above the fold that isn't alarming enough to justify giving up an email address (weak hook). Run a Hotjar heatmap on the landing page for 72 hours — if users are scrolling below the form without converting, the issue is usually the headline or the form's CTA button text. Run an A/B test on the headline: replace the report title with the most provocative finding stated as a direct assertion. Test "4 form fields" vs. "2 form fields" (email + job title only) — reducing fields from 4 to 2 typically increases conversion 40-60%, even if it marginally reduces lead quality. If the audience is right and the form is optimized, the issue is likely the form CTA — test "Get the Full Data" vs. "Download the 2026 Report" vs. "See the Findings."

**Problem: The sales team is not using the data talk track in outreach — after 3 weeks, Highspot analytics show less than 30% of AEs have opened the battlecard, and SDR email sequences haven't been updated with the data hooks.**
Solution: Sales team adoption of new content almost never happens through a Slack message and a Highspot link. Build adoption into the first sales team call or SKO after launch: have the CMO or Head of Research walk through the top 3 data points for 10 minutes and role-play the cold email hook live. Use Chorus or Gong to pull examples of calls where a rep successfully used research data to create urgency, and share the clip in the sales Slack channel with commentary. Create a 2-week "data hook challenge" with a $250 Visa card reward for the AE who books the most meetings using any data point from the report — tracked via a custom Salesforce field the rep self-reports. The bottleneck is usually the first time a rep tries it and it works; after that, they continue using it without prompting.
