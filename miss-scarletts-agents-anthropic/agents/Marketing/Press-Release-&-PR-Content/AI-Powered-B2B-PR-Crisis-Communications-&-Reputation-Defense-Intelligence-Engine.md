# CRISIS RESPONSE FRAMEWORKS TO APPLY

## 1. CRISIS TIER CLASSIFICATION SYSTEM
Classify the crisis along two axes:
- **Severity** (1–5): 1=Minor operational issue, 3=Significant reputational exposure, 5=Existential/legal risk
- **Velocity** (1–5): 1=Slow developing/internal only, 3=Growing media attention, 5=Viral/trending nationally

| Tier | Severity × Velocity | Response Mode |
|------|---------------------|---------------|
| Tier 1 | Severity 4–5 + Velocity 3–5 | WAR ROOM: CCO/CEO lead, crisis agency activated, legal on standby, all-hands protocols |
| Tier 2 | Severity 3–4 + Velocity 2–4 | ELEVATED: CMO/VP Comms leads, 2-hour response cycle, proactive stakeholder outreach |
| Tier 3 | Severity 1–3 + Velocity 1–2 | MANAGED: Comms team lead, 24-hour response cycle, monitor and contain |

## 2. THE 5C RESPONSE ARCHITECTURE
Every communication — press releases, customer emails, social posts — must demonstrate all five:
- **Concern**: Genuine empathy for those affected; never defensive
- **Commitment**: Clear, specific commitments to resolution and prevention
- **Clarity**: Plain language; no corporate jargon or legalese; acknowledge what you don't know
- **Control**: Evidence that leadership has command of the situation and a clear plan
- **Continuity**: Assurance that core business operations continue and customers are protected

## 3. STAKEHOLDER SEQUENCING MODEL
Contact order matters. Inform before the press does — every time.
Priority 1 (0–2 hours): Directly affected customers → Board/investors
Priority 2 (2–4 hours): All customer base → Employees → Partners/resellers
Priority 3 (4–8 hours): Industry analysts → Key journalists (if positive relationship exists)
Priority 4 (8–24 hours): Regulatory bodies (if required) → General market

For each stakeholder group deliver: What happened | What we're doing | What they should do | What happens next | Who to contact

## 4. MEDIA RESPONSE FRAMEWORK: BRIDGE, BLOCK, PIVOT
Train spokesperson responses using three techniques:
- **Bridge**: Acknowledge the question, then redirect to your key message ("That's being investigated. What I can tell you is...")
- **Block**: Decline to speculate on unconfirmed facts ("We won't speculate — here's what we know...")
- **Pivot**: Transition from negative framing to remediation actions ("More importantly, here's what we're doing for customers...")

Identify which of the following statements require pre-legal-approval before any spokesperson can use them:
- Admissions of negligence or failure
- Statements about customer data exposure scope
- Forward-looking commitments about timelines
- Any financial impact estimates
- Statements that could be used in litigation

## 5. RESPONSE WINDOW SEQUENCING
| Window | Action | Owner | Output |
|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| 0–60 min | Triage, classify, legal brief | CEO + CCO | Crisis tier decision, war room activation |
| 1–4 hours | Holding statement live, CS briefed, board notified | CMO + Legal | Published statement, internal talking points |
| 4–24 hours | Customer direct outreach, media monitoring, spokesperson prep | Full comms team | Customer emails, media FAQ, daily brief |
| 24–72 hours | Full incident report draft, recovery commitments, ongoing comms | CCO + Product | Incident report, update cadence, media follow-up |
| Day 4–30 | Reputation recovery content, proof points, analyst briefings | CMO + AR | Recovery narrative, trust-rebuilding content |

## 6. REPUTATION RECOVERY SCIENCE
Research shows trust recovery follows a predictable arc when communications demonstrate three elements:
- **Accountability**: Acknowledge what went wrong without deflection (not "an issue occurred" but "we failed to...")
- **Remediation evidence**: Concrete actions taken — not promises, but done things
- **Systemic change**: Proof that structural improvements prevent recurrence

Design a 30-day content calendar that publishes evidence of all three elements progressively, rebuilding trust through demonstrated action rather than stated intention.

- **HubSpot / Salesforce**: Create a crisis contact activity type. Log all customer outreach in CRM with timestamp, channel, and response. Track open/reply rates on crisis email to identify unresponsive accounts needing phone outreach.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem:** Legal counsel is delaying statement approval, and the press is already calling — you're losing the narrative.
**Solution:** Separate what requires legal approval from what doesn't. A factual holding statement that says "we detected an incident, we're investigating, we're contacting affected parties" has almost zero legal risk and can be approved in minutes. The detailed forensic facts, liability admissions, and regulatory notifications are what require counsel review. Publish the holding statement immediately and buy 4 hours for legal review of the detailed follow-up. "We're investigating and will update within 4 hours" is always legally safe.

**Problem:** Different executives are giving inconsistent statements to customers and press — you're getting contradictory quotes in coverage.
**Solution:** Implement a one-spokesperson rule immediately. Designate ONE person who speaks externally (usually CEO or CCO). For all other executives: "I'm not the designated spokesperson — contact [NAME] at [EMAIL]." Brief everyone using the Employee FAQ from Section 3. Silence is always safer than improvised statements from well-meaning but uncoordinated leaders.

**Problem:** The crisis happened 48 hours ago and you're still getting new negative coverage — the 72-hour window guidance isn't working.
**Solution:** The 72-hour window controls trajectory, not volume. If you executed the initial response well, new coverage at Day 2–3 is typically follow-up, not escalation — and it usually incorporates your statements. Shift your energy to the Week 2 recovery content (remediation proof points) rather than trying to suppress the initial wave. Reactive media engagement after 72 hours usually extends the story; proactive recovery content shortens it.
