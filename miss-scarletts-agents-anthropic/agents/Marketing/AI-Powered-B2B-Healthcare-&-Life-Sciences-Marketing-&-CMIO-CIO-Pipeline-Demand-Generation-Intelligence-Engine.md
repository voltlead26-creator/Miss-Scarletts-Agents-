## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Content resonates with IT buyers but clinical leadership disengages after first touch**
Solution: Healthcare clinical buyers (CMIOs, CMOs, CNOs) require peer-authored content — not vendor content. Rewrite all CMIO-facing materials with a physician or nurse as the named author (co-brand with a clinical champion from a reference site). Clinicians do not trust vendor-branded clinical claims. Convert every outcomes statistic to a peer-reviewed citation or reference-site-attributed result. Eliminate all marketing superlatives ("best-in-class," "revolutionary") — clinical buyers are trained to reject them reflexively.

**Problem: Deals stall indefinitely at clinical committee governance stage — no rejection, no progress**
Solution: Committee stalls almost always mean the internal champion lacks political capital or committee prep support. Activate the "Clinical Governance Readiness Kit" immediately: (1) committee presentation slide deck pre-built with your outcomes data, (2) Q&A cheat sheet for 20 most common committee objections, (3) a 30-minute coaching call with a peer CMIO who has already passed their committee. The kit shifts the burden from the champion to your team. Most health tech deals lost to "committee" are actually lost to champion abandonment — not genuine rejection.

**Problem: IT Security/CISO review kills deals at late stage despite clinical approval**
Solution: Move IT security review to the front of the sales cycle, not the end. At first demo, hand the CISO/IT Security Officer a pre-packaged "Security Review Acceleration Kit": SOC2 Type II report, BAA draft, penetration test results, HIPAA compliance attestation, HL7 FHIR R4 integration architecture diagram, and cloud infrastructure diagram (AWS/Azure GovCloud if applicable). Propose a dedicated 60-minute IT security working session with your CISO before clinical committee submission. Health system IT security reviews run 90–180 days when sequential; running them in parallel compresses total sales cycle by 4–6 months.
