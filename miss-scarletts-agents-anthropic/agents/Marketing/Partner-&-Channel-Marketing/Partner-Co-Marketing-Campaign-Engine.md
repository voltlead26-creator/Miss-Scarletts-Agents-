# Partner Co-Marketing Campaign Engine - Joint Campaign Brief, Asset Production & MDF Allocation System

**Difficulty:** Advanced | **Time:** 30-45 min | **Tags:** partner-marketing, co-marketing, channel-marketing, mdf, joint-campaigns, b2b, partner-ecosystem, go-to-market, demand-generation, revenue-operations

## Overview
Transforms a partner relationship into a fully-built co-marketing campaign: generates a joint value proposition, co-branded campaign brief, asset production plan, MDF (Market Development Fund) allocation request, and launch timeline. Use it when you have an active technology, reseller, or integration partner and need to convert the partnership into pipeline — without spending weeks in planning meetings.

OBJECTIVE:
Build a complete, launch-ready co-marketing campaign package across six layers:

LAYER 1 — JOINT VALUE PROPOSITION & MESSAGING ARCHITECTURE
Develop the messaging foundation that makes both marketing teams say "yes, that's exactly what we've been trying to say":

a) Joint headline (10 words max): The "together we make X possible" statement that anchors all co-marketing assets. Must be specific to the shared outcome, not generic partnership language.

b) Joint value proposition (2-3 sentences): Written to the shared ICP from the perspective of "what you get when you use both." Include one concrete outcome metric (use the joint customer data provided, or construct a plausible benchmark from the partnership type).

c) Messaging hierarchy (3 tiers):
   - Tier 1 (Hero message): The primary campaign theme — one bold claim about what the integration/partnership enables
   - Tier 2 (Supporting proof points, 3 bullets): Specific capabilities or outcomes that only exist because both products work together
   - Tier 3 (Individual product bridges): 1-sentence descriptions of each company's contribution to the combined value, written so neither partner feels subordinate

d) Objection pre-emptions (3): The most common objections prospects raise about integrated/combined solutions and how to address each in co-marketing copy without triggering "sounds like a sales pitch"

e) Messaging dos and don'ts: 5 things to include and 5 things to avoid in all joint assets (to prevent the campaign from feeling like two separate vendor pitches stapled together)

LAYER 2 — CAMPAIGN BRIEF
A single document both teams align on before any asset is built:

a) Campaign name and theme: Something both teams will actually use in internal planning, not just a campaign code. Should be memorable and descriptive.

b) Target segment definition: Exact ICP with firmographic and technographic filters — who receives this campaign, who does not, and how to identify them in each company's CRM or marketing platform.

c) Campaign narrative (3 paragraphs):
   - Paragraph 1 — The problem: Describe the specific business pain the shared ICP experiences that neither company solves alone. No product mention.
   - Paragraph 2 — The joint solution: How the combined products address the problem. Reference the integration or workflow specifically.
   - Paragraph 3 — The outcome: What success looks like for a customer who uses both. Include a proof point or plausible benchmark.

d) Primary CTA and conversion path: The single action the campaign drives toward (demo request / joint trial / webinar registration / ebook download / co-sell meeting). Describe the full conversion flow from click to CRM entry.

e) Success metrics and measurement plan:
   - Primary metric: [# leads / # opportunities / $ pipeline] with specific target
   - Secondary metrics: email open rate, webinar attendance, landing page conversion rate
   - How to attribute leads to the co-marketing campaign in both companies' CRMs
   - Which company "owns" a lead when both databases generate a match (resolution rules)

LAYER 3 — 6-WEEK CAMPAIGN TIMELINE
A week-by-week execution plan with explicit ownership (My Company / Partner / Both / Contractor):

Week 1 — Foundation:
- Joint kick-off call agenda (30 min): 6 agenda items that produce alignment, not conversation
- Asset assignments with deadlines
- Shared campaign tracking setup (Google Sheet, Notion, or Asana template structure)
- Landing page brief and domain decision (joint subdomain vs. each company's own)

Week 2 — Asset production:
- Which assets are drafted this week, by whom, and what "good" looks like
- First review checkpoint: what gets reviewed and who approves on each side

Week 3 — Asset finalization + launch prep:
- Final approval gates before anything goes live
- List segmentation and suppression logic (do not email existing customers of both products with a "try it" CTA — they should receive a different track)
- Email send schedule with send times and timezone logic

Week 4 — Launch week:
- Day-by-day launch sequence with exact send order and timing
- Webinar promotion cadence (if applicable)
- Social amplification plan: LinkedIn posts by both companies' official accounts + key employees

Week 5 — Nurture and follow-up:
- Lead routing: how new leads flow into each company's CRM within 24 hours of conversion
- SDR/BDR activation: the one-paragraph brief both sales teams use to personalize outreach to campaign respondents
- Mid-campaign performance check: the 3 metrics that determine whether to accelerate, pivot, or pause

Week 6 — Close and measure:
- Final campaign performance report template (5 metrics, both companies' perspectives)
- MDF reconciliation: how to document spend against the approved MDF request
- Retro questions: 4 questions to answer together before deciding whether to run a second campaign

LAYER 4 — ASSET PRODUCTION PLAN
Every content piece required for the campaign, specified precisely enough that a contractor could execute without a briefing call:

For each asset, provide:
- Asset name and format
- Word count or length
- Primary audience (prospect / partner's customer / both companies' database)
- Key message to lead with
- CTA
- Who drafts (My Company / Partner / Shared contractor)
- Review deadline
- Notes on tone, brand considerations, or data to include

Required assets (adapt based on approved channels):
1. Joint landing page (hero copy, form, social proof section, FAQ)
2. Campaign launch email — My Company list (subject line A/B test + full email body)
3. Campaign launch email — Partner list (adapted for partner's voice and audience context)
4. 3-touch nurture sequence (see Layer 5 for full copy)
5. LinkedIn ad copy (3 variants: awareness / consideration / conversion)
6. LinkedIn organic posts: 2 per company for launch week
7. Webinar landing page + registration email sequence (if webinar is in scope)
8. One-pager PDF: the joint solution explained in 400 words + visual flow diagram description
9. Partner newsletter feature blurb (150 words, written for partner's editor to drop in as-is)
10. Internal sales brief: 1-page "here's what this campaign is and how to use it in a call" for both sales teams

LAYER 5 — 3-TOUCH EMAIL NURTURE SEQUENCE
Full, ready-to-send email copy for both the My Company list and the Partner list versions. The sequences should feel like they come from a trusted source, not a vendor coalition.

For each email, provide:
- Subject line (A and B variant)
- Preview text
- From name recommendation (personal name vs. company name rationale)
- Full email body (structured: opening hook → problem → solution → social proof → CTA → P.S.)
- Recommended send day and time
- Segment logic: who receives this email and who should be excluded

Email 1 (Day 1 — Problem-first):
Lead with the pain, not the partnership. Make the reader feel understood before introducing any solution. The integration or partnership should appear as a natural resolution, not the point of the email.

Email 2 (Day 5 — Proof-first):
Lead with a specific customer outcome, benchmark, or use case that makes the combined value concrete. Include one data point. Drive toward the primary CTA.

Email 3 (Day 12 — Urgency + CTA):
Create legitimate urgency (event deadline, limited spots, quarter-end offer — only include if genuinely applicable). The CTA should be lower-friction than a demo request if open rates on Email 1 and 2 were below 22%. Include an opt-down option: "Not the right time? Here's a 2-minute read instead" with a link to the one-pager.

LAYER 6 — MDF REQUEST & PARTNER GOVERNANCE
Everything needed to get partner budget approved and the relationship structured for repeatable success:

a) MDF request document (ready to submit to partner's channel team):
   - Campaign name and objective (1 sentence)
   - Target segment and list size
   - Planned activities and budget breakdown (line-item table: activity / estimated cost / my company contribution / MDF requested)
   - Expected ROI: projected leads, opportunities, and influenced pipeline with methodology shown
   - Measurement commitment: how you will report results back to the partner within 30 days of campaign close
   - Reimbursement documentation plan: what receipts/reports you will provide

b) Partner governance checklist (5 items to confirm before launch to prevent the most common co-marketing failures):
   - Brand approval: both companies have reviewed and approved all co-branded assets
   - Data sharing agreement: clear rules on who owns lead data, how it's used, and GDPR/CCPA compliance
   - Lead routing SLA: both SDR teams know the 24-hour response rule for campaign leads
   - Attribution agreement: how both companies will count the same lead/opportunity without double-counting in board reports
   - Escalation path: one named contact at each company who can unblock campaign issues within 4 hours

c) Co-marketing performance scorecard (for quarterly partner reviews):
   Metrics to track for each joint campaign and roll up quarterly:
   - Leads generated (sourced by my company / sourced by partner / joint)
   - Partner-sourced pipeline: $ value of opportunities where partner was involved in sourcing
   - Co-sell velocity: average days from partner-referred lead to closed-won vs. non-partner deals
   - MDF ROI: pipeline influenced per dollar of MDF spent (benchmark: $8-12 pipeline per $1 MDF for healthy programs)
   - Relationship health score: a 1-5 rating on responsiveness, content quality, and sales team activation from both sides

OUTPUT FORMAT:
1. Joint value proposition and messaging hierarchy (copy-ready)
2. Campaign brief (one-page summary + full narrative)
3. 6-week campaign timeline (table format: Week / Activity / Owner / Deadline)
4. Asset production plan (table format: Asset / Format / Owner / Deadline / Notes)
5. 3-touch email nurture sequence (both list versions, full copy)
6. MDF request document + partner governance checklist + performance scorecard

CONSTRAINTS:
- Every campaign asset must be executable by a team of 2 people without agency support — if it requires a creative team of 5, simplify it
- The joint value proposition must pass the "so what?" test: if a prospect's first reaction is "so what?", rewrite until the outcome is specific and compelling
- Do not recommend campaign activities that require the partner to share their raw contact list — instead design campaigns that run on each company's own list with coordinated messaging
- The MDF request must show projected pipeline ROI using the formula: (projected leads) x (industry average MQL-to-SQL rate for campaign type) x (average deal size) = influenced pipeline. Show the math.
- Lead routing rules must be agreed in writing before launch — do not launch without this or leads will fall through the gap between two CRMs and two SDR teams

## Success Metrics
- Joint leads generated: total registrations or form submissions from shared ICP, both list sources combined (target: 60)

- Post-campaign NPS: ask both partner contacts "how likely are you to run another campaign with us?" — if below 7/10, fix the process before the next campaign

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: The partner's legal or brand team is slowing down asset approvals and threatening the launch date.**
Build the review timeline into Week 3 with a 5-day buffer before your planned launch. If approvals stall, escalate to your named partner contact (established in the governance checklist) rather than waiting in the review queue. Have a "lite launch" contingency: identify which 2 assets (typically the email + landing page) can go live independently if the full asset suite isn't approved, so the campaign ships on schedule while remaining assets clear review. Never delay a webinar promotion by more than 5 days — registrations drop sharply when promotion windows compress below 2 weeks.

**Problem: The campaign generates registrations but the partner's sales team isn't following up on co-sell opportunities.**
This is the most common co-marketing failure mode and it's almost always caused by the sales brief being too vague. Replace the one-paragraph campaign summary with a 5-bullet "here's how to use this in a discovery call" document: (1) the problem the campaign addresses, (2) the specific question to ask to qualify the lead, (3) the product demo path for a joint presentation, (4) the success story to reference, (5) who to tag at your partner company when a co-sell opportunity is identified. Deliver this as a Slack message to the partner's sales channel on launch day — not a PDF that gets filed and forgotten.

**Problem: The joint value proposition sounds generic and both companies' marketing teams are unhappy with it.**
The symptom is usually that the draft VP leads with "powerful," "seamless," or "end-to-end" — words that signal the writers couldn't agree on a specific outcome. Force specificity by starting with the customer result and working backward to what made it possible. If you don't have a specific joint customer result yet, run a 1-question survey to your existing joint customers before the campaign: "What's the one measurable change you've seen from using both products together?" The answers will write the campaign for you.
