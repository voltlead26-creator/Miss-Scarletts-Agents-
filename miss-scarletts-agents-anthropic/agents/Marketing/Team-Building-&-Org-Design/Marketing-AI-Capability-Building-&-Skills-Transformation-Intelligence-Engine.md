**SECTION 5: ADOPTION RESISTANCE PLAYBOOK**

**Resistance #1: "AI will replace my job"**

Context: Most common in content writers, copywriters, designers. Driven by media narratives and genuine uncertainty.

Manager Response Script:
"I hear that concern, and I want to address it directly. Our plan is to use AI to handle the parts of your job that are most repetitive and draining — first drafts, formatting, research compilation — so you can spend more time on strategy, creativity, and the judgment calls that actually require a human. The marketing teams most at risk aren't the ones using AI — they're the ones refusing to learn it while their competitors automate circles around them. I want you to be the person on this team who knows this better than anyone. That's job security."

Follow-up action: Show them a specific example where AI made a colleague's job more interesting, not less.

**Resistance #2: "The output quality isn't good enough"**

Context: Usually from perfectionists and senior contributors who tried AI once, got mediocre output, and dismissed it entirely.

Manager Response Script:
"You're right that AI output needs editing — always. The question isn't whether it's perfect, it's whether it's faster and good enough as a starting point. Think of it like Google Docs autocomplete on steroids. The first draft it produces in 5 minutes would have taken you 2 hours — and you'd still have to edit both versions. We're not replacing your judgment. We're eliminating the blank page problem. Let me show you the prompts that are working for [colleague name] right now — the output quality depends almost entirely on how you write the prompt."

Follow-up action: Do a live 20-minute prompt engineering session where you show the difference between a weak prompt and a well-engineered prompt using their own work as the example.

**Resistance #3: "I don't have time to learn new tools"**

Context: Common among marketing ops, demand gen managers, and people who are already at 100% capacity.

Manager Response Script:
"I completely understand — and that's exactly why I'm not asking you to learn a new tool. I'm asking you to try one specific workflow this week: [name the specific workflow]. I've already built the prompt. You paste in your data, hit enter, and tell me if it saved you time or not. That's it. No course, no certification, no setup. Thirty minutes this week. If it doesn't save you time, I won't ask again. If it does, we figure out what to do with that saved time."

Follow-up action: Remove every barrier — pre-build the prompt, set up the tool access, and schedule a 30-minute hands-on session with them specifically.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Team completes Month 1 training but reverts to old habits by Day 45.**
Fix: Reversion happens when AI isn't embedded in the workflow — it's treated as optional. Audit: Are managers asking for AI-assisted outputs specifically? If not, make it explicit. For the content team's weekly pieces, add "AI draft version" as a required deliverable before editing begins. What gets required gets done; what stays optional gets skipped.

**Problem: AI output quality is inconsistent — some team members get great results, others get garbage.**
Fix: Quality variance almost always comes down to prompt quality, not the AI tool. Run a prompt engineering workshop where high performers share their exact prompts side-by-side with low-performers' prompts. The difference is usually specificity of role instruction, context provided, and output format specification. Create a minimum prompt quality standard: every prompt must include a role, objective, context, constraints, and output format.

**Problem: CMO or leadership loses confidence in the program because a high-profile AI output was poor quality.**
Fix: Prevention first — establish a "no AI without human review" rule for any customer-facing or executive-visible output during the first 60 days. If an incident occurs anyway: acknowledge it directly, show the QA process that was skipped, implement a mandatory review step for that content type going forward, and present the 90-day data showing the overall quality trajectory. One miss doesn't invalidate the program — it validates the need for the QA process you're now implementing.
