DELIVERABLES:

**PART 1: STRUCTURED INTERVIEW PROCESS**

Design a 4-stage interview process with:
- Stage name and format (phone screen / video / panel / on-site)
- Duration and interviewer(s) with their roles
- Primary evaluation goal for each stage
- Pass/fail criteria to advance to next stage

**PART 2: COMPREHENSIVE QUESTION BANK**

For each interview stage, provide a curated question set. For every question, include:
- The competency being assessed
- The question itself (written to be genuinely probing, not easily rehearsed)
- Follow-up probes (2-3 deeper questions if the answer is strong but surface-level)
- What a STRONG answer sounds like (specific behavioral signals, not just "good communication")
- What a WEAK answer sounds like (red flags, avoidance patterns, vague generalities)
- Bias risk note (where interviewers commonly let halo effect or affinity bias distort judgment)

QUESTION CATEGORIES:

*Behavioral / Competency Questions (15 questions):*
Use STAR anchoring: "Tell me about a time when..." or "Walk me through a specific example of..."
Cover: pipeline ownership, prioritization under pressure, managing up, cross-functional conflict, failure and recovery, team leadership, building from zero vs. scaling what exists, influencing without authority.

*Technical / Craft Questions (10 questions):*
Role-specific questions that require demonstrated knowledge, not just vocabulary. For a demand gen hire: attribution modeling, CAC calculation, campaign ROI analysis, ABM program design. For PMM: positioning methodology, launch planning, competitive narrative, sales training design. For content: SEO strategy, editorial calendar governance, distribution amplification, content ROI measurement.

*Strategic Thinking Questions (8 questions):*
Present real scenarios from your business (anonymized if needed). Ask candidates to diagnose, prioritize, and plan. Include at least one "here's a dataset, what do you see?" exercise relevant to their function.

*Culture and Values Questions (5 questions):*
Probe for: how they handle ambiguity, how they treat junior teammates, their relationship with data vs. instinct, how they define great work, what they need from a manager to thrive.

*Anti-Pattern Probes (5 questions):*
Designed to surface common failure modes for this role. For demand gen directors: probes for whether they actually understand pipeline vs. MQL theater. For PMMs: probes for whether they write for sales or for customers. For content leads: probes for whether they optimize for output or outcomes.

**PART 3: TAKE-HOME ASSIGNMENT**

Design one realistic, time-boxed assignment that:
- Takes no more than 2-3 hours to complete honestly
- Mirrors an actual challenge this hire will face in their first 60 days
- Has a clear, unambiguous brief with all context provided
- Can be evaluated objectively against a rubric

Assignment brief: [specific scenario, data provided, deliverable required, format]

Grading rubric (evaluate on 5 dimensions, score 1-5 each):
1. Strategic thinking: Does their approach demonstrate understanding of business context, not just marketing tactics?
2. Analytical depth: Do they use data to drive decisions, or do they make up numbers without acknowledging uncertainty?
3. Communication quality: Is the output clear, structured, and persuasive — something you'd actually use?
4. Prioritization: Given constraints (time, budget, team size implied), do they make smart tradeoffs?
5. Differentiation: Does their answer reveal a distinctive perspective, or does it look like a ChatGPT template?

Include: what a 5/5 assignment looks like vs. a 2/5 assignment (concrete examples, not vague descriptors).

**PART 4: CANDIDATE SCORECARD**

Build a weighted competency scorecard:
- 6-8 evaluation dimensions specific to this role
- Weighting per dimension (must sum to 100%)
- 1-5 behavioral anchor scale for each dimension (describe what 1, 3, and 5 look like in observable terms)
- "Must have" vs. "nice to have" column
- Dealbreaker flags checklist
- Overall recommendation options: Strong Hire / Hire / Neutral / No Hire / Strong No Hire
- Space for interviewer conviction score (1-10) separate from scorecard total — because sometimes the scorecard says hire but conviction is low, and that matters

**PART 5: REFERENCE CHECK SCRIPT**

10 questions for former managers and senior colleagues:
- At least 3 questions specific to the competencies that are hardest to assess in interviews
- 2 questions designed to uncover how they perform under pressure or in conflict
- 1 question about how they handle feedback and development
- 1 question about what management style they need to thrive
- The "magic question" that almost always surfaces something useful: [include this]
- Instructions: how to create psychological safety so references give candid feedback, not cheerleading
- Note on reference inflation: what to listen for when a reference is being diplomatically negative

**PART 6: HIRING DECISION FRAMEWORK**

Structured debrief agenda (45-60 min):
- Pre-debrief: all interviewers submit their scorecards independently before seeing others' feedback (prevents anchoring)
- Round 1: each interviewer shares their score and top 3 signal moments — no crosstalk until all have shared
- Round 2: open discussion, focus on disagreements (where scores diverged >1.5 points)
- Round 3: assess overall candidate against the role success profile (30/60/90 days) — could this person execute it?
- Hiring manager final recommendation
- If divided: escalation criteria and who has final say

Offer decision rubric:
- Green light: proceed to offer — criteria
- Yellow light: proceed with conditions — what conditions, how to address in offer or onboarding
- Red light: decline — how to deliver decline with speed and dignity

Compensation calibration: how to use market data, internal equity, and candidate signals to construct an offer that closes, not just one that's "fair."

## Example Input/Output

**Input:**
- Role: Senior Demand Generation Manager (IC, not managing anyone yet — may build a team in 12 months)
- Company: Prismatic Data (B2B data integration SaaS, Series B, $12M ARR, 70% YoY growth)
- Stack: HubSpot, Salesforce, LinkedIn Ads, Google Ads, 6sense, Clearbit
- Must-haves: Owned pipeline sourcing, experience with ABM platforms (6sense/Bombora/Demandbase), strong analytical skills
- Challenge: Currently generating 80% of pipeline from inbound/content. Need to add outbound + ABM layer to break into mid-market ($500K-$2M deal size)
- Disqualifier: Only managed MQL volume metrics, no pipeline ownership experience

**Output Sample — Behavioral Question:**

> **Competency:** Pipeline ownership under pressure
>
> **Question:** "Walk me through the last time your pipeline coverage dropped significantly below target mid-quarter. What was the situation, what did you do, and what was the outcome?"
>
> **Follow-up probes:**
> - "What was the exact pipeline coverage ratio when you noticed the problem, and what was your target?"
> - "What lever did you pull first, and why that one instead of others?"
> - "Looking back, what would you have done differently in the two weeks before the problem surfaced?"
>
> **Strong answer signals:**
> Candidate names specific numbers (coverage ratio, dollar gap, timeline). They demonstrate a mental model of what drives pipeline (not just MQL volume). They show they worked with sales, not around them. They describe experiments they ran — some of which failed — and what they learned. They use the word "pipeline" the way finance uses "revenue" — with precision.
>
> **Weak answer signals:**
> Candidate talks about MQLs, not pipeline. They describe what the team did without naming their specific role. They focus on process ("we had a meeting," "we built a dashboard") rather than decisions and outcomes. They can't name the number — "it was around... I think maybe 2x?" — suggests they weren't actually accountable.
>
> **Bias warning:**
> Candidates who worked at well-known companies (Salesforce, HubSpot, Drift, Gong) often get inflated scores here because interviewers assume brand = performance. Push for the specific numbers regardless of pedigree.

**Output Sample — Take-Home Assignment:**

> **Assignment: Mid-Market Demand Generation Strategy**
>
> *Brief:* Prismatic Data has historically sourced 80% of pipeline from inbound/content. Your mandate is to design a 90-day ABM + outbound demand gen program targeting mid-market accounts ($500K-$2M ACV) in the financial services vertical. You have a budget of $45K/quarter and access to 6sense, LinkedIn Sales Navigator, and HubSpot. The sales team has 4 AEs focused on this segment.
>
> *Deliverable:* A 3-5 page strategy document (slides or written, your choice) covering: target account list criteria and sizing, channel mix with rationale and expected CAC, 90-day campaign roadmap with milestones, how you'll measure success (leading and lagging indicators), and what you'd need from sales to make it work.
>
> *Time box:* 2.5 hours. Do not spend more. We want to see how you think under real constraints, not a polished agency deck.
>
> **Grading rubric — Strategic Thinking (weight: 30%):**
> - 5/5: Starts with ICP before channels. Acknowledges they don't have customer data and makes explicit, reasonable assumptions. Identifies the real constraint (sales bandwidth, not budget). Proposes phased approach that tests and learns before scaling.
> - 3/5: Selects reasonable channels but doesn't explain the tradeoffs. ICP criteria are generic ("VP Finance at 500+ employee companies"). Milestones are output-based, not outcome-based.
> - 1/5: Leads with tactics ("we'll run LinkedIn ads and do cold email sequences"). No account-level thinking. Budget allocation feels arbitrary.

## Success Metrics

- **Interview process health:** <10% of candidates drop out of process after stage 2 (dropout = process quality signal, not candidate quality)
- **Offer acceptance rate:** >85% of offers accepted — low rate signals mis-set expectations or slow process
- **Scorecard calibration:** Inter-rater reliability >0.70 across interviewers (scores for same candidate vary by <1.5 points on average) — requires training and calibration sessions
- **30-day new hire confidence:** >90% of new hires report they "clearly understood the role expectations before accepting" in onboarding survey
- **90-day ramp validation:** New hire 90-day performance correlates with hiring scorecard score (r > 0.5 after 10+ hires) — if it doesn't, your scorecard is measuring the wrong things
- **Time to hire:** Role filled within target window with no emergency compromises on must-haves

## Integration Tips

- **Notion:** Build a Hiring Hub database with one page per open role. Embed the structured question bank as a linked database candidates are evaluated against. Use Notion's toggle blocks for each question so interviewers can expand signal guidance during live interviews without overwhelming the page.
- **Greenhouse / Lever / Ashby:** Upload the candidate scorecard as a custom scorecard template in your ATS. Map each dimension to a specific interview stage so scores are collected stage-by-stage, not all at once at the end. Set automated reminders for debrief completion within 24 hours of interview.
- **Google Docs:** Share the take-home assignment brief as a View-Only Google Doc with a submission form (Google Form) so all submissions arrive in a structured spreadsheet with timestamps — prevents candidates from over-engineering after the time box.
- **Slack:** Create a private Slack channel per candidate (e.g., #hiring-[role]-[candidate-name]). Post the interview guide before each stage and collect async scorecards in-thread before the debrief. Set a channel archiving policy so institutional memory is preserved.
- **LinkedIn Talent Insights / Gem:** Use the ICP (ideal candidate profile) from Part 1 to build a talent pool search in LinkedIn or Gem. Pipe sourced candidates directly into your ATS with the structured scorecard pre-attached.
- **Zapier / Make:** Automate the reference check outreach: when a candidate reaches the reference stage in your ATS, trigger an automated email to their references with a structured questionnaire link (Typeform or Google Form). Pipe responses into a summary document automatically.

## Troubleshooting

- **Problem: Interviewers give inflated scores to everyone and the debrief is meaningless because every scorecard says "hire."**
  Solution: Implement mandatory score distribution training. Show interviewers real scorecards from past hires and calibrate: "This person we hired who struggled in month 3 — look at where the yellow flags were in their scorecard that we talked ourselves out of." Add a rule: if everyone gives a 4+/5 across all dimensions, require each interviewer to name one genuine concern before the debrief closes. No hire should be unanimous on everything — that signals group-think, not a perfect candidate.

- **Problem: The take-home assignment screens out great candidates who decline to complete it or submit generic AI-generated work.**
  Solution: Shorten the assignment to 90 minutes and provide more specific context (actual metrics, real constraints) that require genuine thinking, not just templating. Add a 30-minute "assignment debrief" conversation where candidates walk through their reasoning — this is where AI-generated work surfaces immediately. Great candidates who value their time will complete a well-scoped, respectful assignment; they're right to reject a 6-hour unpaid consulting engagement.

- **Problem: The structured process feels robotic and candidates report a cold, impersonal interview experience, hurting offer acceptance rate.**
  Solution: Structure and warmth are not mutually exclusive. Train interviewers to open each stage with 3 minutes of genuine conversation (not small talk theater — actual curiosity about the candidate's background and motivations). End every interview with: "What questions do you have for me — about the role, the team, or what it's really like to work here?" and answer honestly. Brief candidates on what to expect at each stage so they feel informed, not processed. The best candidates have options; the process is a two-way evaluation.
