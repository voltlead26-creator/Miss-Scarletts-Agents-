## Overview
Benchmarks your marketing team's compensation against current market data, identifies pay equity gaps and flight risks, designs competitive career ladders, and produces a complete talent retention strategy. Use this when you're losing top performers to competitors, building your first formal compensation structure, conducting annual comp reviews, or making counter-offer decisions.

- For companies without access to premium comp surveys, Levels.fyi (tech roles), LinkedIn Salary Insights, Glassdoor, and Payscale provide directional market data. Note these are self-reported and skew 5-8% above true market.

**HubSpot / Salesforce (Pipeline Impact Modeling):**
- Map each at-risk marketing employee to their pipeline contribution using your CRM attribution data. A demand gen manager who sourced $4M in pipeline last year has a specific, quantifiable replacement cost. Pull this number before your retention conversation — it makes the business case for a $20K salary adjustment obvious.

**Lattice / Leapsome / Rippling:**
- Input the career ladder framework directly into your performance management tool as a "growth framework." Link it to each employee's profile so career development conversations happen in the context of a documented, consistent standard — not vague "you need to do more" feedback.
- Set automated reminders in Lattice for compensation review cycles: every 10 months for top performers, every 12 months for solid performers.

**Notion / Confluence:**
- Build a living "Total Rewards Wiki" using the total compensation statement template. Update it quarterly with equity valuations as your company's valuation changes. A top performer who got options at a $50M valuation should see what those are worth at your current $200M valuation — that's a retention tool hiding in plain sight.

**Google Sheets (Flight Risk Tracker):**
- Build a private CMO-only spreadsheet with the flight risk scoring model. Score each team member quarterly. Use conditional formatting: red = 9+, yellow = 5-8, green = <5. Track interventions and outcomes over time to learn which retention actions work at your company.

**Carta / Pulley (Equity Management):**
- Use the equity vesting schedule data from your cap table tool to identify upcoming cliff events (1-year or 4-year) for each marketing employee. Any team member within 90 days of fully vesting their original grant is at peak flight risk. The counter-measure is a refresher grant — but it needs to be offered before they start looking, not after.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: You ran a pay equity audit and found significant gaps, but don't have budget to fix them all at once.**
Solution: Use the Priority Matrix from Module 1 to triage. Fix the top 2-3 critical-risk employees immediately — even a partial adjustment is better than nothing and shows good faith. For mid-tier gaps, create a documented remediation roadmap with specific timelines (e.g., "we'll address this in the January comp cycle") and put it in writing. Employees don't just leave because of pay gaps — they leave when they feel the gap is invisible to leadership. Acknowledging it and having a plan dramatically reduces flight risk even before you've fixed it.

**Problem: You did a stay interview and the top performer said they're leaving for a mission-driven company — not about money.**
Solution: This is actually the most recoverable scenario if you act fast. "Mission disconnect" is usually a symptom of feeling like their work doesn't matter, not a genuine desire to work for a nonprofit. Immediate action: assign them a high-visibility project with real strategic impact, give them a seat in a customer conversation or exec team meeting, and have them present their work at the next all-hands. If the work feels meaningful, the "mission" objection usually dissolves. If it doesn't — they've told you the truth and a counter-offer won't work.

**Problem: A counter-offer was accepted, but the employee leaves 4 months later anyway.**
Solution: Research consistently shows that 50-80% of employees who accept counter-offers leave within 12 months — because the underlying issue (growth, manager, culture) wasn't fixed, just temporarily masked with cash. When an employee brings an offer, always ask two questions before deciding to counter: (1) "What made you start looking?" and (2) "If we fix that, is there anything else that would make you leave?" If the answer to #2 reveals a structural problem you can't or won't fix (e.g., "I don't believe in the product direction"), counter-offering is expensive theater. Save the money, begin knowledge transfer, and hire their replacement with the counter-offer budget.
