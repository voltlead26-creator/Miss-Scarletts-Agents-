**Difficulty:** Advanced | **Time:** 20-30 min | **Tags:** b2b, saas, lead-velocity-rate, revenue-forecasting, pipeline-analytics, growth-metrics, revenue-operations, analytics

## Overview
Calculates Lead Velocity Rate (LVR) — the month-over-month growth rate of qualified leads — and transforms it into a forward-looking revenue prediction model. Unlike lagging indicators (pipeline, bookings), LVR tells you whether your revenue growth is accelerating or decelerating 6-12 months before it shows up in the numbers. Use this monthly to catch demand generation problems before they become missed quarters.

## Integration Tips

- **HubSpot**: Build a custom HubSpot report using "Contact Lifecycle Stage History" filtered to MQL stage entry date. Export monthly MQL counts on the 1st of each month and paste into a Google Sheet that auto-calculates LVR. The "Contacts by Lifecycle Stage" dashboard widget updates daily and can serve as your real-time LVR tracker.
- **Salesforce**: Create a "Leads Created by Month" report filtered to your qualified lead record type. Schedule it to deliver to your inbox on the 1st of each month. Combine with "Opportunities Created" report to track the lag between MQL and SQL creation for sales cycle validation.
- **Marketo**: Use the "Lead Performance" report with a date range filter and your MQL smart list. Export monthly and append to your LVR tracking spreadsheet. Marketo's Revenue Cycle Analytics (RCA) module, if licensed, provides native LVR-equivalent reporting.
- **Tableau / Looker**: Build a dedicated LVR dashboard with three panels: (1) monthly LVR bar chart with trailing 3-month average line, (2) LVR by source waterfall chart, (3) forecast cone showing bear/base/bull scenarios. Refresh weekly from your CRM data source.
- **Google Sheets**: Create a 13-column LVR tracker: Month, MQL Count, MoM Change, LVR%, 3-Mo Average, Source breakdown columns, Required LVR, Gap. Use SPARKLINE formulas for inline trend visualization. Share with CMO and RevOps as the single source of truth.
- **Salesforce Einstein / Clari / Gong Forecast**: If you use AI forecasting tools, compare their pipeline predictions to your LVR-based trajectory. When the two models diverge by >20%, investigate whether LVR is detecting a leading shift that lagging pipeline models haven't caught yet.
- **Zapier / Make**: Automate monthly MQL count extraction by building a Zap that runs on the 1st of each month, queries HubSpot or Salesforce for the prior month's MQL count, and appends it to your Google Sheet LVR tracker — triggering an email alert if LVR falls below your warning threshold.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: My MQL definition changed 3 months ago, making LVR comparisons meaningless — the numbers aren't apples-to-apples.**
Solution: Restate the LVR analysis from the date of the definition change forward and label the prior period as "pre-definition change (not comparable)." For the period before the change, manually re-score a sample of 50 historical leads using the new definition and extrapolate an adjusted count for those months. Flag this adjustment explicitly. Going forward, commit to a freeze on definition changes mid-year to preserve LVR integrity — any changes should take effect at the start of a new fiscal year.

**Problem: My LVR looks healthy but pipeline and revenue are still declining — the metric seems disconnected from reality.**
Solution: This is a quality drift signal. Check two things: (1) Has your MQL-to-SQL conversion rate declined over the same period? If yes, your MQL definition is producing leads that look qualified but aren't converting — tighten your scoring model. (2) Has your win rate declined? If yes, the problem may be downstream (sales execution, competitive, pricing) and LVR alone can't fix it. Run the LVR quality check section with particular focus on quality-adjusted LVR — if it's significantly lower than reported LVR, you've found your disconnect.

**Problem: LVR swings wildly month-to-month due to campaign launches and events — it's hard to read the trend.**
Solution: Use the trailing 3-month LVR as your primary indicator, not the single-month number. Normalize for known events by annotating the LVR dashboard with program launches, events, and seasonal effects. Consider calculating a "baseline LVR" that excludes event-sourced leads (which are inherently lumpy) and tracks organic + always-on program LVR separately — this gives a cleaner read on underlying demand generation momentum.
