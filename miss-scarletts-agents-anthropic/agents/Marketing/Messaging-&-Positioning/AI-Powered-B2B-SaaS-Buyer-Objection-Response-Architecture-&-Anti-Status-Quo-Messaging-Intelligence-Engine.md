## Troubleshooting

- **Problem:** Responses feel generic and don't resonate with buyers.
  **Solution:** The prompt requires specific proof points and customer stats. If you enter vague inputs ("customers are happier"), the output will be equally vague. Run customer interviews or pull G2 reviews first to extract specific language buyers use — then re-run with verbatim customer quotes as inputs.

- **Problem:** Sales reps don't use the cheat sheet after it's built.
  **Solution:** Adoption fails when the tool is handed over as a document, not embedded in workflow. Integrate the cheat sheet into your CRM as a deal-stage-triggered "playbook" or run a 30-minute live objection-handling drill where reps role-play using the exact scripts. Reps need to practice the language before they trust it.

- **Problem:** The cost-of-inaction statements feel manipulative rather than helpful.
  **Solution:** Every COI statement must be anchored in a real number specific to the buyer's situation (their team size, their deal volume, their current process). Generic urgency fails. Customize the COI math live on a call using the buyer's own numbers — "you told me your team does X — here's what that costs you" lands as insight, not pressure.
