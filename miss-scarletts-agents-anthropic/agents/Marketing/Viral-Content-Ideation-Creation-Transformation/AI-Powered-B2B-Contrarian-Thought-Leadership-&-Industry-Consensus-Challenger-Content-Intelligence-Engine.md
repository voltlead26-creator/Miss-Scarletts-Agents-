## Overview
Identifies the prevailing consensus beliefs in your industry, develops data-backed contrarian positions that challenge conventional wisdom, and generates a complete content series designed to spark debate, earn shares, and establish you as the go-to contrarian voice buyers trust. Use it when you want to break through content saturation, build a differentiated point of view, or launch a thought leadership campaign that generates pipeline from organic reach rather than paid amplification.

SECTION 1 — CONTRARIAN POSITION ARCHITECTURE

STEP 1: STEEL-MAN THE CONSENSUS

Before challenging conventional wisdom, articulate it at its strongest. A weak attack on a strong position loses credibility; a precise attack on the real claim wins.

Consensus Belief Audit:
- State the conventional wisdom in its most compelling, defensible form (2–3 sentences): [The strongest version of the belief, including why smart people hold it and what evidence supports it]
- Identify the originating logic: why did this belief become consensus? (e.g., "this worked well in 2018 when X was true, but X has changed because...")
- Name 2–3 respected figures who publicly hold this view — this signals you're challenging the establishment, not a strawman
- Acknowledge what's still true within the consensus: the most credible contrarians concede partial validity before claiming the exception is now the rule

STEP 2: SHARPEN THE CONTRARIAN THESIS

A powerful contrarian thesis has four properties:
1. Falsifiable — it makes a specific claim that can be proven or disproven with evidence
2. Actionable — it implies a different decision or behavior for the audience
3. Uncomfortable — it should make at least 30% of readers mildly defensive
4. Memorable — it can be quoted in a tweet, a conference keynote, or a podcast in under 20 words

Generate three alternative thesis framings for the contrarian position:

Version A — The Data Reframe: "[Conventional metric/approach] is measuring the wrong thing. The companies quietly winning are optimizing for [alternative metric] instead."
Version B — The Timing Reframe: "[Conventional wisdom] was correct until [specific inflection point]. Here's why that logic now works against you."
Version C — The Inversion: "Everyone in [category] is focused on [popular thing]. The real leverage is in [overlooked thing] — and there's now evidence to prove it."

Evaluate each version against the four properties above and select the highest-scoring thesis.

STEP 3: EVIDENCE STACK CONSTRUCTION

Build a 3-tier evidence hierarchy:

Tier 1 — Primary Evidence (most credible, hardest to dismiss):
- Internal data: [What proprietary customer data, product usage data, or win/loss patterns support this position?]
- Original research: [If none exists, design a 5-question survey that would generate validating data within 2 weeks]
- Case studies: [2 specific customer examples with before/after metrics that prove the contrarian position]

Tier 2 — Reframed Third-Party Evidence (publicly available data interpreted through the contrarian lens):
- Identify 2–3 widely-cited industry statistics and show how they actually support the contrarian position when interpreted correctly
- Source: [List specific reports, analysts, or datasets — e.g., Gartner, LinkedIn B2B Institute, TOPO/Forrester, industry associations]

Tier 3 — Logical Argument (for positions where data is limited):
- If-then chain: construct a 4-step logical argument where each step is harder to dispute than the conclusion itself
- The "it's already happening" argument: identify 5–10 companies in the target audience's peer group who have already abandoned the conventional approach and what results they've seen

SECTION 2 — CONTENT SERIES ARCHITECTURE

Design a 6-piece contrarian content series across a 6-week distribution window. Each piece builds on the previous while standing alone.

PIECE 1 — THE POSITION LAUNCH (Week 1, LinkedIn long-form post)

Purpose: Plant the flag. Make the contrarian position visible and generate initial debate.

Structure:
- Hook (Line 1, must be a pattern interrupt — cannot start with "I," "We," or "Most people"): [Write 3 alternative opening lines; select the one that creates the most tension]
- Context setter (2–3 sentences): acknowledge you're about to challenge something people believe strongly
- The contrarian claim (1 sentence, bold): the thesis statement from Section 1
- Evidence block (3 data points or logical steps): presented as a numbered list for skimmability
- The implied challenge to the reader (1 question): makes the reader ask "am I doing this wrong?"
- CTA: invite disagreement explicitly ("I know this is a hot take — tell me where I'm wrong in the comments")

Engagement prediction:
- Who will immediately share: [The audience segment that's been silently thinking this but couldn't say it publicly]
- Who will push back in comments: [The segment whose identity is tied to the conventional approach]
- Why public pushback is a feature, not a bug: counter-comments from senior practitioners signal credibility to the algorithm AND to passive readers who follow the debate

PIECE 2 — THE EVIDENCE DROP (Week 2, LinkedIn carousel or data-driven post)

Purpose: Validate the position with evidence for skeptics who engaged with Piece 1.

Structure:
- Opening: "Since I posted [reference to Piece 1] last week, [X number] of [target personas] told me privately they've been thinking the same thing. Here's the data."
- Slide 1: The headline stat that most powerfully supports the contrarian position
- Slides 2–4: Supporting evidence, one layer deeper each slide (data → case study → logical implication)
- Slide 5: The "so what" — specific action the reader should take differently based on this evidence
- Slide 6: The evidence counter (acknowledge the strongest objection + provide data-backed rebuttal)

PIECE 3 — THE CASE STUDY (Week 3, story-format post or short video script)

Purpose: Make the contrarian position tangible through a specific company's experience.

Structure (use the "Hero's Journey" adapted for B2B):
- The Before State: [Company name or anonymized "a [industry] company with [size] in [stage]"] was following the conventional approach → specific negative outcome
- The Inflection Point: what triggered them to question the consensus belief
- The Contrarian Experiment: what they tried instead, and the initial resistance internally
- The Result: specific metric improvement (use real numbers or percentage ranges — no vague "significant improvement")
- The Lesson: one transferable principle the reader can apply tomorrow

Video adaptation (for LinkedIn native video, 90 seconds):
- 0:00–0:10: Pattern interrupt hook (state the contrarian claim bluntly)
- 0:10–0:45: Tell the case study story at pace (skip context, go to conflict and resolution)
- 0:45–1:15: Extract the transferable lesson + the data point
- 1:15–1:30: Call to action (question that drives comments + link to full case study or related asset)

PIECE 4 — THE FRAMEWORK (Week 4, educational post with proprietary model)

Purpose: Turn the contrarian position into a named, replicable framework that gets attributed to you when others share it.

Framework Design Rules:
- Name it: a 3–5 word framework name that your target persona will use in their own content (e.g., "The Pipeline Paradox," "The Consensus Trap Framework," "The Inversion Test")
- Make it visual: design a 2×2 matrix, a 3-step model, or a 5-stage spectrum that can be sketched on a napkin
- Make it categorizing: the framework should allow readers to immediately self-classify ("I'm a Stage 2 company," "We're in the bottom-left quadrant")
- Make it teachable: someone who reads the post once should be able to explain the framework to a colleague

Post structure:
- Introduce the framework name and the problem it solves (3 sentences)
- Explain each element of the framework with one-sentence definitions
- Show where 80% of companies currently sit in the framework (the "common mistake" quadrant)
- Show where the 20% of companies with the best outcomes sit — and why
- CTA: "Where does your [team/company/approach] fall? Comment below."

PIECE 5 — THE DEBATE (Week 5, intentional controversy post)

Purpose: Engineer maximum algorithmic amplification through structured disagreement with a named perspective or common piece of advice.

Controversy Engineering Rules:
- Target advice, not people: challenge a specific piece of advice, a common metric, or an industry "best practice" — not an individual (name the idea, not the person unless they've made it their public position and welcomed debate)
- Grade your controversy: aim for "productively uncomfortable" not "offensive" — a 7/10 on the controversy scale, not a 10/10
- Prepare for the dominant counter-argument before posting: write a 2-sentence response to the most likely objection and have it ready to post in comments within the first 15 minutes of the post going live

Post structure:
- Opening: "Unpopular opinion: [single-sentence contrarian claim about a specific common practice]"
- Evidence: 2 data points that support your position (no more — this is a provocation, not a white paper)
- Acknowledgment: "I know [X%] of [target personas] still swear by [conventional approach], and here's why I understand that..."
- The challenge: "But if [X has changed in the market], then [conventional approach] stops working for this reason: [specific mechanism]"
- Comment bait: End with a question that has a binary answer ("Has anyone actually seen [conventional approach] work in the last 12 months? Or are we just hoping it still does?")

PIECE 6 — THE SYNTHESIS (Week 6, long-form newsletter or LinkedIn article)

Purpose: Convert campaign momentum into a definitive, linkable resource that earns evergreen backlinks and newsletter forwards.

Structure:
- Title: "[The Conventional Wisdom] Is Dead. Here's What Replaces It." (or similar high-shareability framing)
- Introduction: synthesize the debate that Pieces 1–5 sparked, with specific data from engagement ("When I posted [X claim] 5 weeks ago, [Y practitioners] responded — here's what I learned from the debate")
- The Full Argument: expanded version of the contrarian thesis with complete evidence stack (1,500–2,500 words)
- The Framework (from Piece 4): reproduced with additional detail
- Practical Implementation Guide: 5 specific actions the reader can take this week to test the contrarian approach
- The Concession: one area where the conventional wisdom is still correct — builds credibility by acknowledging nuance
- Resources: link to 3–4 related assets, data sources, or tools

COMMENT MANAGEMENT STRATEGY

Golden Hour Protocol (first 60 minutes after posting):
- Have 5 substantive responses pre-written to the most predictable counter-arguments
- Respond to every comment within 15 minutes during the first hour — this signals LinkedIn to extend distribution
- Do NOT respond to shallow engagement ("great post!" "agree!") first — prioritize responses to critics and complex questions

Thread Extension Tactics:
- When a commenter makes a good counter-point, respond with: "You're raising [the strongest objection] — and honestly that's where I expected pushback. Here's the data I didn't include in the post because it was too long: [add new evidence]"
- This turns the comment section into a bonus content layer that non-commenters read and share

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Contrarian posts generate likes but few comments — engagement looks good but no real debate**
Solution: You've generated agreement, not productive controversy — the position isn't uncomfortable enough OR the target audience segment seeing the post already agrees with you (check LinkedIn post demographics). Fix: Use the "Piece 5 Debate" structure even earlier in the series — post an explicit "I know most [personas] will disagree with this, and here's why I think they're wrong" framing. Increase specificity: instead of "paid media is becoming less efficient," say "Anyone still running [specific ad format] to [specific persona] is going to see CPL increase 40%+ in the next 6 months based on this specific platform change." Specificity invites both agreement and correction from practitioners with direct experience.

**Problem: Getting high LinkedIn engagement but no email captures or demo bookings from the campaign**
Solution: The content-to-pipeline bridge is broken — either the conversion asset isn't logically connected to the contrarian argument or there's no clear CTA directing engaged readers off-platform. Fix: Add an explicit "if you agree with [contrarian claim], here's the next thing you should do" CTA to every piece after Piece 2. The CTA must feel like the natural next intellectual step, not a product offer — "I built a 5-question diagnostic so you can test whether [conventional approach] is actually working for your specific metrics — takes 3 minutes" converts 4–6x better than "Book a demo to see how [Product] solves this."

**Problem: The contrarian position generates controversy but from the wrong audience — non-buyers are debating, not target personas**
Solution: Your thesis is too broad or is touching a topic that general marketers (not your specific ICP) care about. Re-narrow the claim with ICP-specific language: instead of "ABM is overrated," try "ABM without real-time intent data is a list management exercise, not a marketing strategy — and [specific job title] at [specific company stage] companies are the ones who've figured this out." Specificity filters for the right audience self-selecting into the debate; broad contrarian claims attract broad audiences who don't convert.
