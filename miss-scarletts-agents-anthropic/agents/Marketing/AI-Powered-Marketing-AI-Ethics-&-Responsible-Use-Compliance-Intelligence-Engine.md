#### DIMENSION 1: REGULATORY RISK MAPPING

For each regulation below, assess exposure against the provided AI stack:

**EU AI Act (risk classification required for EU-market companies by August 2026)**

*Prohibited Practices (Article 5) — Immediate halt required if present:*
- Subliminal techniques that exploit psychological vulnerabilities to manipulate purchasing behavior without the person's awareness
- Real-time biometric categorization of individuals from public spaces for targeted advertising
- Social scoring systems used to determine which audiences receive marketing

*High-Risk AI Systems (Annex III — require Conformity Assessment):*
- AI in employment and workers management (if your product touches HR decisions and your marketing AI categorizes HR decision-makers — lead scoring may qualify)
- AI used in creditworthiness assessment or debt collection (if selling to financial services and your intent data touches credit behavior proxies)

*Transparency Requirements (Article 52 — all companies marketing to EU):*
- Any AI system that interacts with humans must disclose it is an AI (chatbots, virtual assistants, AI-generated personalized emails presented as individual human communications)
- Deepfakes, synthetic media, and AI-generated images must be labeled as artificially generated

**FTC Enforcement Priorities (active enforcement 2024-2026)**

*Disclosure requirements:*
- AI-generated or AI-enhanced testimonials, endorsements, and reviews require clear disclosure under the updated FTC Endorsement Guides (effective 2023)
- AI-generated performance claims ("Our AI can increase revenue by 40%") must be substantiated with real evidence

*Discriminatory targeting (Section 5):*
- Using AI lookalike modeling with demographic proxies (ZIP code, name inference, engagement pattern clustering) in ways that result in protected class exclusion violates Section 5 — regardless of intent
- AI-powered dynamic pricing that charges different amounts to similarly situated consumers based on inferred protected characteristics is an active enforcement area

**GDPR/CCPA for AI Marketing**

*Automated decision-making rights (GDPR Article 22):*
- If lead scoring or audience segmentation produces a "significant effect" on whether a person receives commercial communications, they have a right to human review
- "Significant effect" includes: exclusion from awareness campaigns, exclusion from retargeting, placement in different nurture tracks

*DPIA requirement (GDPR Article 35):*
- Any systematic automated processing of personal data at scale must have a completed Data Protection Impact Assessment on file
- AI tools processing behavioral data for profile-building qualify — this includes most marketing automation platforms with AI features

*Data minimization for AI training:*
- Customer data used to train AI models must be limited to what is necessary for the stated purpose
- Vendors using your customer data to train general-purpose models serving other customers without explicit consent violate GDPR purpose limitation

#### DIMENSION 2: ALGORITHMIC BIAS TESTING PROTOCOL

For each AI tool performing audience selection, lead scoring, content personalization, or pricing:

**Step 1 — Demographic Parity Test**
Measure whether the AI's outputs differ systematically across:
- Race/ethnicity proxies: ZIP code, name-based inference, language detected, neighborhood income quartile
- Gender: title-based clustering (VP Marketing trends female; VP Engineering trends male — are both in your target universe receiving equivalent reach?)
- Age: engagement patterns that correlate with generational cohorts
- Disability status: accommodation signals in CRM, device accessibility settings

Acceptable threshold: No protected-class proxy group should receive statistically different treatment (>2 standard deviations from the mean distribution, chi-square at p<0.05)

**Step 2 — Counterfactual Fairness Check**
Would changing only a protected attribute in the AI's input data, while holding all other variables equal, change the output? If a lead from ZIP code 60601 (predominantly white, high-income Chicago neighborhood) scores 85 and an identical lead from ZIP code 60628 (predominantly Black, lower-income) scores 62 — the ZIP code is acting as a discriminatory proxy. This constitutes potential disparate impact regardless of intent.

**Step 3 — Proxy Variable Audit**
Flag any input variable correlating >0.6 with a protected class:
- ZIP code → race proxy (historical redlining creates persistent correlations)
- Job title clusters → gender proxy (male-dominated vs. female-dominated function clustering)
- Purchase history categories → age or income proxy
- Social engagement patterns → political affiliation or ethnicity proxy in some contexts

Remove or de-weight flagged variables; document the business justification for any retained.

**Remediation Playbook by Severity:**
- **Low** (<10% disparity detected): Document finding, add to quarterly monitoring, no immediate action required
- **Medium** (10–25% disparity): Pause audience expansion on affected tool, conduct root cause analysis, retrain or reconfigure within 30 days, retest before resuming
- **High** (>25% disparity): Immediately halt the affected AI use case, notify legal and CMO, conduct full independent audit, implement mandatory human override for all decisions until bias is resolved, consider proactive regulatory disclosure

#### DIMENSION 3: GENERATIVE AI CONTENT DISCLOSURE STANDARDS

**"Material AI Involvement" Definition**
Disclosure is required whenever AI generated, substantially rewrote, or selected content such that a reasonable person would consider this material to their assessment of the message's authenticity, accuracy, or source.

**Disclosure Required:**
- Blog or article posts where AI generated >50% of the draft, even if human-edited
- Case studies using AI-synthesized customer quotes or simulated outcomes
- Social media posts presenting AI-generated perspectives as personal human experience
- Sales emails generated by AI using a named human sender without disclosure
- Chatbot interactions where the system presents itself as a human team member
- AI-generated images or video presented as authentic documentary footage
- AI-generated voice-overs or podcast content presented as entirely human-created
- Product testimonials written or substantially enhanced by AI

**Disclosure NOT Required:**
- AI-assisted grammar and spell-checking (Grammarly-style correction)
- AI-powered send-time optimization or deliverability routing
- AI that classifies, routes, or recommends existing content (not generating it)
- SEO keyword suggestions that humans write content around
- AI summarization of existing human-generated content

**FTC-Compliant Disclosure Language by Format:**

| Format | Required Disclosure | Placement |
|--------|---------------------|-----------|
| Paid advertising | "Ad generated with AI" | Same visual field as ad, equal prominence to other material disclosures |
| Blog/article | "This content was produced using generative AI tools and reviewed by [Editor Name, Role]." | Author byline or article footer |
| Email | "This message was drafted using AI under human review." | Email footer, 10pt minimum font |
| Social post | Platform native "AI-generated" label where available; otherwise include "AI-generated content" in post body | Within post text, not hidden in comments |
| Chatbot | "You're speaking with an AI assistant. I can connect you with a human expert." | First message, no exceptions |
| Video/image | Platform-native label or visible on-screen watermark | Visible during display |

#### DIMENSION 4: VENDOR ETHICS DUE DILIGENCE CHECKLIST

Before signing any new AI marketing vendor contract, require written answers to all 12 questions:

1. Does your AI system qualify as high-risk under the EU AI Act? If yes, what is your current conformity assessment status and timeline?
2. What training data does your model use, and can you confirm it was obtained with documented consent for its specific training purpose?
3. How do you test your models for demographic bias? What is your bias testing cadence and your remediation SLA when bias is detected?
4. What is your model explainability approach? Can you provide human-readable explanations for individual automated decisions on request within 30 days?
5. Do you maintain a Model Card or System Card documenting training data sources, evaluation methodology, known limitations, and intended use boundaries?
6. What data retention and deletion policies govern our customer data once ingested by your system — including backups and training derivatives?
7. Does your AI ever make fully automated decisions with no human-in-the-loop override option? If yes, in which specific use cases?
8. What is your process when regulations change in a way that affects your model's legality? What is your timeline to notify customers and update the system?
9. Do you hold SOC 2 Type II and/or ISO 27001 certifications covering your AI system's security and integrity?
10. Is our customer data ever used to train models that serve other customers or improve general-purpose foundation models? (Answer YES = immediate deal-breaker unless explicit written consent mechanism exists)
11. What is your AI incident response process — who do you notify, how quickly, and what is your customer communication obligation if your model causes consumer harm?
12. Can you provide complete audit logs of all decisions your AI made on behalf of our organization for the preceding 24 months, within 30 days of request?

**Deal-Breaker Criteria — Disqualify the vendor if:**
- Customer data is used in general-purpose model training without explicit, revocable consent
- No documented bias testing methodology exists
- Cannot produce individual decision explanations for regulators on request
- No contractual notification requirement for material model changes
- No documented data deletion process upon contract termination
- Unable to provide SOC 2 Type II within 90 days

## Troubleshooting

**"Our legal team doesn't understand AI marketing regulations — they keep deflecting."**
Use the Regulatory Risk Assessment output as an educational brief, not a legal opinion request. Frame the conversation as: "Here are three specific risk areas we need your guidance on, with the relevant statute text included." EU AI Act Article 52 on chatbot disclosure and the FTC Endorsement Guide updates are simple, concrete examples that any attorney can evaluate quickly. Avoid framing this as a theoretical future risk — the EU AI Act is in force, and FTC enforcement on AI endorsements is active.

**"Our AI vendors claim they're compliant but can't provide documentation."**
'We're compliant' without documentation is commercially insufficient. Use the vendor checklist question 12 (audit log access) and question 5 (Model Card) as your non-negotiable written requirements. Escalate to procurement to add a "right to audit" clause and 90-day documentation delivery requirement in your vendor contract renewal. A vendor who cannot document their AI ethics practices by contract renewal should be placed on a replacement roadmap.

**"We can't prioritize a full audit — the team is overwhelmed with pipeline targets."**
Implement a 3-priority triage: (1) Fix all chatbot and customer-facing AI disclosures in the next 2 weeks — this is the highest legal exposure at lowest cost. (2) Run a single bias test on your highest-volume targeting system (LinkedIn lookalike or lead scoring) within 30 days — one afternoon of analysis prevents significant liability. (3) Publish the one-page ethics policy internally — this signals good faith to regulators if an inquiry occurs. Defer the full vendor due diligence audit to all contracts up for renewal rather than retrofitting existing agreements.
