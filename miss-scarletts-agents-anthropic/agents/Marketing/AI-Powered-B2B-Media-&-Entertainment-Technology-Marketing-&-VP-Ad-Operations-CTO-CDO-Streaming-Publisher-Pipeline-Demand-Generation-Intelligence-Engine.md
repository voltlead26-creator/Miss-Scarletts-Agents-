## Troubleshooting

**Problem: CDO and CTO are not engaging — all pipeline continues to come from VP Ad Operations who can't close deals without executive approval**
Solution: This is a structural "coach without champion" failure mode that afflicts virtually all media tech vendors. The fix requires a parallel executive engagement track that bypasses the ad ops layer entirely. Implement three tactics simultaneously: (1) Create a "CDO Revenue Briefing" content asset — a 12-slide, board-ready analysis of the publisher's specific monetization performance gap using public data (Similarweb traffic volume, industry CPM benchmarks for their publisher tier, estimated annual ad revenue vs. what top-quartile publishers with similar traffic earn). Deliver this directly to CDO via LinkedIn InMail from your own CDO — not a product pitch, a peer data exchange. This works because CDOs respond to peer outreach with relevant data; (2) Elevate your VP Ad Ops champion by giving them a business case builder — a pre-built revenue impact model they can present upward: "At our current impression volume, the fill rate and CPM gap vs. benchmark costs us $[X] annually. Here is the vendor evaluation, integration timeline, and projected ROI." This turns a technical champion into a business-case-armed executive sponsor; (3) Request a "Publisher Ad Revenue Diagnostic" meeting positioned as a strategic consultation, not a sales call — agenda is "we analyzed 140 publishers in your tier and identified 3 common monetization gaps; we want to understand if those apply to your stack." This gets CDO in the room without triggering procurement.

**Problem: Media company IT procurement is slowing down deals — legal, security reviews, and procurement cycles are adding 45–60 days to every deal**
Solution: Enterprise media companies — especially legacy broadcast-to-streaming transitions — have procurement processes built for on-premise software that do not accommodate modern API-first SaaS products. Proactively attack this delay at the beginning of the sales cycle, not when the deal is already stuck. Create a "Publisher Technical Trust Package" that preemptively delivers everything procurement will ask for: SOC 2 Type II report, penetration test summary, data processing addendum (DPA) with GDPR/CCPA language pre-populated, standard MSA redlines for media company terms, cloud infrastructure architecture diagram (AWS/GCP deployment), integration security questionnaire pre-answers. Give this package to your technical champion in Week 3 of the evaluation, before procurement gets involved. Add a "Technical Approval Checklist" to your SDR/AE discovery process — ask "Who in engineering, security, and procurement needs to approve a new ad tech vendor?" and map every stakeholder in Week 1 so you can engage them proactively rather than reactively. For CDO-level deals: consider a "master vendor agreement" pre-negotiation with 3–4 large media group legal teams — once you're on their approved vendor list, future deals within that group bypass the full procurement cycle.

**Problem: Competitor is undercutting on price and winning shorter-cycle deals by promising similar performance at 40% lower cost**
Solution: Price-based competition in media technology is almost always a proxy for "we haven't proven revenue impact clearly enough for the buyer to justify the premium." Do not match price — instead, make the value comparison unambiguous. Deploy three responses: (1) Immediately offer a free 30-day revenue comparison test — A/B test your platform against the competitor on a defined subset of the publisher's inventory. Real performance data on their actual inventory eliminates price as the primary decision variable; if your technology actually performs better, the data will show it; (2) Build a "True Cost of Cheap Ad Tech" analysis — model the annual revenue loss from 5% lower fill rate + 10% higher latency + 3% higher IVT rates across their actual impression volume. At scale (10M+ MAU), the difference in annual ad revenue between best-in-class and average SSAI can be $3–8M — a premium that dwarfs any licensing cost difference; (3) Shift the conversation from licensing cost to total economic value: present as "You're evaluating whether to pay $X more per year for an ad stack that generates $Y more in ad revenue. The question isn't whether we're cheaper — the question is whether the incremental revenue justifies the incremental investment." This reframes the negotiation entirely and prevents price from being the primary evaluation criterion.
