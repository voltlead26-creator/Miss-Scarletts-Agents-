1a. THE PROPRIETARY DATA ADVANTAGE FRAMEWORK

Before designing any report, map the full competitive moat your data creates. For each data dimension your platform captures, evaluate it against four criteria:

EXCLUSIVITY SCORE (1-5): Can competitors publish this same metric without having your platform's dataset?
- 5 = Unique to your platform (only possible with your customer data)
- 3 = Available from multiple sources but your dataset is larger/more representative
- 1 = Publicly available (anyone can publish this)

READER VALUE SCORE (1-5): How urgently does your ICP want a benchmark for this metric?
- 5 = This is a metric they track internally and have no external benchmark for
- 3 = Interesting but not a primary performance KPI for this role
- 1 = Low relevance to day-to-day decision-making

BUSINESS VALIDATION SCORE (1-5): Does benchmarking this metric help prospects realize they need your product?
- 5 = Seeing the benchmark immediately reveals that the prospect's performance is suboptimal, and your product is the solution
- 3 = Useful context but doesn't directly connect to product need
- 1 = Interesting but doesn't move the needle on purchase motivation

RESEARCH FRANCHISE SCORE (1-5): Will this metric still be relevant and trackable in 5 years, enabling year-over-year comparison?
- 5 = Perennial metric (email open rates, sales cycle length, churn rate) — year-over-year trend is itself newsworthy
- 3 = Relevant now but may be disrupted by technology or behavior change
- 1 = Trending topic with unclear longevity

For each data dimension: calculate the RESEARCH PRIORITY SCORE = Exclusivity (×2) + Reader Value (×2) + Business Validation (×2) + Franchise (×1). Maximum score: 35. Focus the flagship report on the 5-7 dimensions with scores above 20.

1b. RESEARCH OPPORTUNITY INVENTORY

For every data dimension scoring ≥20 on the Research Priority Score, complete this brief:

Data Dimension: [What you measure]
The Question It Answers: [The benchmarking question your ICP has been unable to answer — e.g., "What is a good email reply rate for outbound SDR prospecting at my company size?"]
The Insight Teaser: [One provocative finding from your dataset — e.g., "Top-performing SDR teams in our dataset reply in <11 minutes to inbound leads — 3× faster than the median. Every hour of delay reduces conversion by 17%."]

1c. THE RESEARCH THESIS

Every great proprietary research report is built around a single, provable, and somewhat counterintuitive thesis — a claim that your data proves true that challenges the conventional wisdom in your space.

THESIS STRUCTURE: "Contrary to [widely held belief in your category], [your data shows the opposite / a nuanced truth / a pattern that has never been publicly named]. Companies that [behavior associated with your product] achieve [X% better outcome] than those that [status quo behavior]."

Write 3 candidate thesis statements for the flagship report, ranked by:
- How counterintuitive or surprising the finding is (the more surprising, the more press-worthy)
- How directly connected to product use the behavioral differential is
- Whether the data is strong enough to support the claim (sample size, statistical significance)

Select one thesis as the report's editorial north star. Every section of the report must either prove the thesis, provide context for it, or show what happens when companies ignore it.

2a. REPORT IDENTITY & POSITIONING

REPORT NAMING FRAMEWORK:
Strong proprietary research reports use titles that combine:
- A clear category signal: "[State of / Benchmark Report / Annual Index / The [Year] [Category] Report]"
- A data credential: "[Based on analysis of X customer companies / X data points / X months of activity]"
- An aspirational reader identity: "[For revenue leaders / For CMOs / For teams building category-defining programs]"

Generate 5 candidate titles with subtitles for the flagship report. For each, write:
- The title and subtitle
- The tagline (one sentence that summarizes the report's value to the reader)
- The data credential statement (the specific numbers that prove exclusivity: "Based on analysis of [X] [data points] from [X] companies in [year]")
- The "impossibility statement" (one sentence proving this report could not have been created by anyone else — e.g., "This research is based on proprietary data from [X customer companies] using [Platform Name] in FY2025 — data that exists nowhere else.")

Select one report identity. This becomes the permanent franchise name, updated annually with the year.

2b. FULL REPORT TABLE OF CONTENTS

Design a complete 25-30 page research report structured in five acts:

ACT 1 — EXECUTIVE SUMMARY (pages 1-3)
Purpose: The shareable, self-contained condensation of the report's 5 most important findings. Designed to be forwarded internally by champions without requiring the recipient to read the full report. Must include: the research thesis stated in one sentence, the 5 headline findings as bullet points (each leading with a specific statistic), a 1-paragraph "what this means for your team" section targeting the economic buyer, and a visual "State of [Category] at a Glance" infographic summarizing all key benchmarks on one page.

ACT 2 — METHODOLOGY & DATASET (pages 4-5)
Purpose: Establish credibility by being transparent about how the research was conducted. Include: dataset size and composition (total companies, breakdown by company size / industry / geography / ARR segment), time period covered, data cleaning and normalization methodology (briefly — this builds trust without boring the reader), statistical significance statement, definitions of key terms (prevent misinterpretation), and a methodology FAQ anticipating the 3 most likely challenges to the data's validity.

ACT 3 — THE FINDINGS (pages 6-22, the core of the report)
Divide into 5-7 chapters, each structured as:
- Chapter title (framed as a question or insight — not neutral, provoke the reader)
- The finding: one headline statistic and one to two supporting statistics
- Visualization: specify the chart type (bar chart for comparisons, line chart for trends, scatter plot for correlations, heat map for segment comparisons) and what it should show
- Segment analysis: how the finding differs for different company sizes, industries, maturity levels, or use cases — the segment comparison is often the most actionable insight ("companies with >100 employees in financial services show 2× the benchmark improvement")
- The implication: "What this means for your team" — specific actions the reader should take based on this finding, written for the practitioner (champion), not the executive (economic buyer)
- The warning: "What happens if you ignore this" — the cost of inaction, expressed in quantified business terms where possible

ACT 4 — FRAMEWORK & RECOMMENDATIONS (pages 23-26)
Purpose: Give the reader a proprietary framework for acting on the research — a named methodology, maturity model, or scoring rubric that only your report provides and that positions your company's approach as the solution architecture.

Design the framework:
- Name it: A memorable 3-5 word framework name that becomes jargon in your category (e.g., "The Revenue Intelligence Stack," "The Activation Flywheel," "The Pipeline Signal Index")
- Structure it: 3-5 stages, levels, or dimensions — each with a name, description, benchmark characteristics, and the specific action to move from current stage to the next
- Self-assessment element: A 10-question scoring rubric readers can complete to place themselves on the maturity model — produces a personalized "score" that gives them a reason to share the report with their team and discuss it with a sales rep
- The maturity benchmark: What percentage of companies in your dataset fall into each stage — proves the framework is grounded in real data, not theory

ACT 5 — LOOKING AHEAD (pages 27-30)
Purpose: Position your company as a forward-looking authority, not just a data reporter.
- "What the data predicts": 3 specific predictions about how the benchmarks will shift in the next 12-18 months, with the leading indicators from this year's data that support those predictions
- "What the top 10% are doing today": A profile of the characteristics that define the highest-performing companies in your dataset — written as an aspirational state for the reader, not as a list of product features
- "The research we'll add next year": Tease 2-3 new data dimensions you'll add to next year's report — creates anticipation, drives return visits, and signals that the franchise is deepening

2c. STATISTICAL RIGOR CHECKLIST

Before publication, verify every claim in the report against this checklist. Any finding that fails these tests should be cut or reclassified from a "finding" to an "observation":

Minimum sample size: ≥500 companies for global/cross-industry benchmarks; ≥150 companies for segment-level benchmarks (industry-specific, company-size-specific); ≥50 companies for sub-segment insights (used only in sidebars, never as headline findings)

Statistical significance: All comparative claims ("companies using behavior X perform Y% better than those using behavior Z") require a p-value ≤0.05 and a Cohen's d (effect size) ≥0.3 to qualify as a meaningful difference. Report the sample sizes for both comparison groups.

Causality language discipline: Never use "causes," "leads to," or "results in" unless you have an experimental design (A/B test or pre/post study within your dataset). Use "is associated with," "correlates with," "companies that [behavior] tend to [outcome]," or "companies in the top quartile for [behavior] show [outcome]."

Survivorship bias audit: Identify any segment of your dataset where you only observe customers who stayed active long enough to generate the data point. If you are measuring 12-month metrics, your dataset excludes customers who churned before month 12. Disclose this explicitly in the methodology section — it is far better to acknowledge the limitation than to have a critic expose it post-publication.

Benchmark confidence intervals: For every headline benchmark (e.g., "the median reply rate is 8.3%"), include the 25th and 75th percentile. The range is more useful than the median alone and prevents readers from misapplying a single number to their specific context.

3a. THE 10-WEEK PRE-LAUNCH SEQUENCE

The period before report publication is when category authority is staked — not after launch. The pre-launch sequence builds anticipation, earns backlinks before the report exists, and ensures maximum distribution on launch day.

WEEKS 1-2: DATA DISCOVERY SOCIAL SERIES
Publish one "data finding teaser" per day on LinkedIn/X (Twitter) — each post reveals a single surprising statistic from the dataset without framing it as a preview of the report. Format: "[Surprising number]. [Context sentence that makes the statistic meaningful]. [Question to drive comments — "Is this consistent with what your team sees?"]." Tag 3-5 prominent practitioners in the space on selected posts to seed conversation. These posts should feel organic, not promotional. Collect the LinkedIn post data (engagement, comments, reshares) — this is your proof-of-concept audience intelligence, showing you which findings resonate before the report is written.

Goal: 3 posts go viral within your ICP (>500 engagements from target-role viewers). Posts that underperform (below 100 engagements from ICP) signal findings that need more context or stronger framing — revise before putting them in the report.

WEEKS 3-4: ANALYST & MEDIA BRIEFING WINDOW
Contact 8-12 industry analysts and trade journalists for embargoed previews of the report. Embargo date: 72 hours before public launch. Briefing packet: 1-page executive summary with the 5 headline findings (no full report), a quote from your CEO or Head of Research attributable under embargo, and 2-3 data visualization images they can use in their coverage.

Analyst target list: Identify analysts at Forrester, IDC, Gartner, or relevant boutique firms who cover your category. For each, write a 3-sentence personalized pitch connecting the report's thesis to research they have published previously — analysts respond to pitches that reference their existing work and position the report as adding data to a topic they already cover.

Journalist target list: Identify 5-7 journalists at publications your ICP reads (e.g., VentureBeat, TechCrunch, Fast Company, Forbes, industry trade publications). For each, write a 2-sentence pitch that leads with the most provocative finding and immediately anchors it to a business trend they have been covering.

WEEKS 5-6: RESEARCH PARTNER RECRUITMENT
Identify 2-4 non-competitive partners to co-brand the report or publish complementary commentary. Ideal partners: industry trade associations, complementary technology vendors whose product data would add depth to yours (e.g., a CRM vendor for a RevTech report, an HR platform for a WFM report), and well-known practitioners whose endorsement adds credibility.

For each partner: write a 4-sentence partnership pitch explaining (1) what their audience gains, (2) what data or commentary they contribute, (3) what they receive in return (logo placement, joint webinar, lead share for gated downloads), and (4) why this partnership elevates both brands. Co-branded research commands 2-3× the download volume of single-company research and generates backlinks from the partner's domain — accelerating SEO authority for the report page.

WEEKS 7-8: EARLY ACCESS PROGRAM
Gate the report for a pre-launch early access list. Early access targets: your existing customer base, active trial users, newsletter subscribers, and prospects currently in the pipeline. Early access email sequence (3 emails, sent over 10 days):

Email 1 — Invitation: "You're getting early access to [Report Name] before it goes public. We analyzed [data credential]. Here's the finding that surprised us most: [teaser stat]. Early access download link opens in 72 hours."

Email 2 — Reminder with a second teaser: Send 48 hours after Email 1 with a different finding. Subject line: "The data shows [finding] — and you might disagree." Include the download link.

Email 3 — Final reminder (12 hours before public launch): "Early access ends at [time]. [X] people have already downloaded [Report Name]. The finding that generated the most discussion: [third teaser stat]."

Track who downloads during early access. Contacts who download and do not have an active sales opportunity should trigger an SDR notification within 24 hours.

WEEKS 9-10: PRODUCTION LOCKDOWN & LAUNCH PREP
Finalize report design, legal review, and all launch assets. Complete checklist:
- Report PDF (gated version) ready for distribution platform (Unbounce, Marketo landing page, or website CMS)
- Report teaser page (ungated) with the 5 headline findings — for SEO and for sharing without requiring a form fill
- Press release drafted and embargoed with 8 journalists
- LinkedIn sponsored content creative (5 variations) approved and loaded in Campaign Manager
- Email launch sequence (5 emails over launch week) drafted and QA'd in MAP
- SDR outreach sequence loaded in Outreach/Salesloft: 4-step sequence using the report as the first-touch asset
- Internal sales team briefing deck: 1-page "how to use this report in your deals" guide with 3 conversation starters, 3 objection-handling uses, and 3 share templates for champions

3b. LAUNCH DAY EXECUTION PROTOCOL

LAUNCH DAY is the single highest-value day in the research program calendar. Execute this protocol to maximize first-day momentum:

6:00 AM: Press release publishes via PR wire service (Newswire, PRWeb, or a major wire). Embargo lifts for all journalists and analysts briefed in weeks 3-4.

7:00 AM: CEO or CMO publishes LinkedIn post (first mover, before company page). Format: The most provocative single finding, 3 lines of context, one question, and a native document upload of the executive summary (not a link — native uploads receive 3× the algorithmic reach). Tag 5 industry influencers directly: "Curious how this compares to what you're seeing."

8:00 AM: Company LinkedIn page post with the report infographic image (not a link to a report page — the image itself, with the landing page link in the first comment). This format maximizes organic reach before paid amplification begins.

9:00 AM: Email blast to full list. Subject line should lead with the most counterintuitive finding, not "introducing our research." A/B test subject lines: Version A = "[Surprising statistic] — is your team performing above or below benchmark?" Version B = "We analyzed [X companies]. Here's what separates the top 10%."

10:00 AM: LinkedIn Sponsored Content campaigns activate. Run three audiences simultaneously: (1) Retargeting — people who visited your website in the last 90 days; (2) ICP Lookalike — LinkedIn matched audience based on your ideal customer profile; (3) Direct ICP Targeting — job titles + seniority + industry + company size targeting.

11:00 AM: SDR team activates launch-week outbound sequence. Every prospect in Stage 1 (Qualified) receives a personalized email sharing the one report finding most relevant to their business context. AE team notified to reference the report in any same-day calls.

3:00 PM: Real-time performance check. Review download-to-registration conversion rate on the landing page. If conversion rate is below 40%, the value proposition on the landing page is weak — immediately A/B test the headline copy.

End of launch day debrief: Record total downloads, press mentions (Google Alerts set up in week 6), social shares, website traffic volume, and pipeline touched (deals where at least one contact engaged with the report on launch day).

- Data-driven email newsletter series: A 6-week email series that sends one chapter summary per week to subscribers who downloaded the report — deepening engagement and keeping the brand top-of-mind throughout the quarter following launch.

TIER 3 DERIVATIVES (months 3-12, evergreen amplification):

Conference organizers prioritize original data over opinion-based submissions — your research is your competitive advantage in the speaker selection process.
- Year-over-year comparison (for year 2): In Q4 of year 1, begin collecting additional data points specifically designed to show trend movement from year 1 to year 2. 5a. RESEARCH PROGRAM REVENUE KPIs

Track the following metrics to measure the full commercial impact of the research program:

DEMAND GENERATION METRICS:
- Total report downloads (gated + ungated)
- Net-new contacts generated (contacts who downloaded the report and were not previously in your CRM — these are the program's direct demand generation output)
- Download-to-demo conversion rate: Of all contacts who downloaded the report in the last 90 days, what percentage have requested a demo? Benchmark target: 4-8% for enterprise ICP, 8-15% for SMB/mid-market ICP
- Research-sourced pipeline: Opportunities where the report was the documented first touch (contact downloaded the report before any other known marketing or sales interaction). Tag in CRM as "Research-Sourced" campaign.

PIPELINE INFLUENCE METRICS:
- Report-influenced pipeline: Opportunities where a buying group contact downloaded the report at any point before the opportunity reached Stage 3 evaluation. Track as a CRM Opportunity field.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem:** The data team says there is not enough proprietary data to publish statistically credible benchmarks — the dataset is too small (fewer than 500 companies) or too skewed toward one customer segment.

**Solution:** There are three paths forward, in order of preference. Path 1 — Reframe the report scope: Instead of publishing top-line industry benchmarks, publish benchmarks for your specific customer segment (e.g., "B2B SaaS companies with $5M-$25M ARR using outbound as their primary acquisition channel"). A narrower scope with a valid sample is more credible than a broad claim with an insufficient one. Path 2 — Add a survey layer: Run a 15-question survey of your ICP (not just your customers — your entire addressable market) to supplement your proprietary data. Publish the report as "Based on proprietary platform data from [X companies] and a survey of [X additional respondents]" — this is the hybrid model used by HubSpot, Salesforce, and most enterprise software research programs. The platform data provides the unique angle; the survey provides the scale. Path 3 — Partner for data scale: Identify 2-3 complementary technology vendors whose aggregate dataset, combined with yours, meets the sample threshold. Co-author the report, share the data, and launch jointly. Co-authored research from two complementary platforms reaches both companies' audiences and generates 2-3× the distribution of a single-company report.

**Problem:** The report launches, generates strong downloads, but produces zero pipeline — contacts are downloading and disappearing, with no progression to demo or SDR conversation.

**Solution:** A high download/low conversion pattern almost always means one of three things: (1) Wrong audience — the report is being downloaded by practitioners who use it professionally (researchers, consultants, students, curious competitors) rather than active buyers. Check the company-size and job-title breakdown of downloaders in your MAP. If >30% of downloads come from companies outside your ICP or from individual contributors without purchase authority, your targeting and distribution channels need recalibration — shift budget from broad content syndication to LinkedIn Sponsored Content with strict ICP job title targeting. (2) No follow-up architecture — without an immediate follow-up sequence (the 4-step SDR sequence loaded before launch), downloads are inert leads that cool within 72 hours. Check whether the SDR sequence fired for each download. If automation failed, manually identify the 50 highest-quality downloaders and have SDRs reach out within 24 hours with a personalized reference to the one finding most relevant to the prospect's situation. (3) Wrong gating strategy — if the full report is freely available without a form fill (common when reports are linked from press coverage without UTMs), you are generating anonymous readers instead of known contacts. Audit all inbound report traffic sources in GA4. Any high-traffic source that is not routing through the gated landing page is leaking leads.

**Problem:** Year 2 launch falls flat — lower downloads, no press coverage, and the team loses confidence in the program.

**Solution:** Year 2 underperformance is almost always caused by one of three failures: (1) Insufficient year-over-year narrative — if Year 2 is positioned as "this year's version" rather than "what changed and why it matters," it will not generate press coverage or social engagement. Every Year 2 report must lead with a trend finding: "[Key metric] changed by [X]% since last year — and here's what drove it." The trend story, not the benchmark update, is what earns Year 2 media attention. (2) Stale thesis — if the research thesis from Year 1 has become conventional wisdom (competitors adopted the framework, the industry now agrees with the finding), Year 2 needs a new counterintuitive claim. Revisit the thesis development process from Module 1c and find the finding in the new dataset that would make your ICP say "really? that surprises me." (3) List decay — if your Year 1 download list was not re-engaged throughout the year (via quarterly pulse reports, year-preview teasers, etc.), you are relaunching to a cold audience in Year 2. In the 8 weeks before Year 2 launch, run a re-engagement campaign to the Year 1 download list offering "Year 1 vs. Year 2: a preview of what changed" — this surfaces the active segment of your list before the full re-engagement email and gives you a warm audience on launch day.
