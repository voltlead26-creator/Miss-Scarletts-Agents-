## Troubleshooting

**Problem: Introducers agree but never actually send the introduction**
Solution: Make the action as easy as possible — never ask someone to write an intro from scratch. Always provide a pre-written draft they can forward with one click or a brief edit. Follow up with the introducer 48 hours later: "Hey Avery — just wanted to check if you got a chance to forward that note to Sarah? Happy to tweak the message if needed." Introducers who don't follow through often just forgot — a gentle nudge converts >70% of stalled intros.

**Problem: Prospects treat the warm intro like spam and don't reply**
Solution: The introduction message is often too "sales-y" — even when warm. Audit the intro kit your customer sends. It should read like a genuine peer recommendation, not a vendor referral. Remove phrases like "cutting-edge solution" or "helping companies like yours." Replace with specific, concrete outcomes the introducer achieved. Also ensure your Day 0 follow-up email is short (<80 words) and immediately references the shared connection by name with a specific mutual context.

**Problem: Running out of warm paths after the first 90 days**
Solution: Warm outbound is a renewable resource only if you invest in relationship capital consistently. Implement a 90-day relationship investment cycle: identify 20 net-new customer advocates each quarter through high NPS scores, add technology partner introduction SLAs to your partner agreements (e.g., "2 warm introductions per quarter"), and build your advisor/investor network specifically for pipeline coverage of your next target vertical. Never treat warm outbound as a one-time campaign — build it as a recurring system.
