## Overview
This engine produces a rigorous, defensible market sizing analysis — using both top-down and bottom-up methodologies — to quantify your total addressable market (TAM), serviceable addressable market (SAM), and serviceable obtainable market (SOM). Use it when preparing investor decks, justifying budget to the board, prioritizing market segments, or deciding whether a new market entry is worth pursuing.

## Troubleshooting

**Issue: My market is so new that no analyst coverage exists — I can't find a top-down number.**
Fix: Use the "adjacent market anchor" approach. Find the closest established category and estimate your category as a percentage of it using logic: "Our AI compliance monitoring product lives inside the $14B GRC software market. AI-native compliance automation is emerging as a distinct sub-category; based on adoption curves from similar AI-native sub-categories (e.g., AI coding tools within developer tools), it represents 8-12% of the parent category today, growing to 25-30% by 2028." This gives investors a credible anchor even without an analyst report. Add: "No analyst firm has published a standalone TAM for this category yet — which is itself a signal that we're early." Investors respect intellectual honesty more than a fake number.

**Issue: My bottom-up and top-down estimates diverge by 3x or more.**
Fix: This usually means one of three things: (1) your category definition doesn't match the analyst's definition — clarify exactly what the analyst's report includes vs. excludes; (2) your ACV is significantly different from the market average — your premium pricing creates a smaller SAM by count but equal SAM by dollars; or (3) you've defined your ICP too narrowly. Run the prompt again with this instruction: "The top-down and bottom-up estimates diverge by [X]x. Diagnose the most likely reasons and propose a reconciled methodology that explains the gap without adjusting either estimate arbitrarily."

**Issue: Sophisticated investors are pushing back on my SOM assumptions as too aggressive.**
Fix: Add an explicit market share velocity benchmark: "Show me the Year 1-5 revenue trajectory of [Competitor X] from their founding year, and map how that trajectory compares to my SOM assumptions." If your SOM implies growing faster than every comparable company in the category, that's a red flag. Scale back your SOM, then compensate with a clear articulation of your unfair advantage that explains why you can grow faster than historical comps. Grounded humility paired with a specific differentiation argument is more compelling than an aggressive number with weak support.
