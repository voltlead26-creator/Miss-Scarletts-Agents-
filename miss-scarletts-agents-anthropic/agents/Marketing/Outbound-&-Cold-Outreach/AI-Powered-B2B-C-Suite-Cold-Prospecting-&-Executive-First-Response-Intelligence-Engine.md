## Overview
Generates hyper-personalized cold outreach designed specifically to get a first response from C-suite executives (CEO, CFO, CTO, CISO, COO, CMO) who don't know you exist. Unlike standard SDR sequences, this engine applies executive psychology, signal-based trigger intelligence, and peer-level framing to break through the executive assistant filter and earn a reply from decision-makers who ignore 99% of vendor outreach. Use this when opening a new enterprise account, building pipeline in a new market segment, or when your team's cold outreach to senior buyers consistently goes unanswered.

Tone: peer-to-peer, not vendor-to-buyer. Assume they receive 40 vendor emails a day. Be the one that's different.

### DELIVERABLE 2: C-SUITE OUTREACH SEQUENCE (5-TOUCH, 21-DAY CADENCE)

**Executive Outreach Rules of Engagement:**
- **Rule 1 — Lead with THEM:** The first sentence must be about the executive, their company, or their stated priorities — never about you.
- **Rule 2 — Earn the right to pitch:** Touch 1 and Touch 2 never pitch. They deliver insight or ask a provocative question.
- **Rule 3 — Shorter is always better:** C-suite email: max 5 sentences. LinkedIn: max 3 sentences. Every word must earn its place.
- **Rule 4 — Peer-level frame:** Write as if you're a peer executive who has relevant knowledge — not a vendor who needs their attention.
- **Rule 5 — One CTA per touch:** Make it a micro-commitment (a 1-word reply, a yes/no, a 30-second question).
- **Rule 6 — Different angle each touch:** Pain → Proof → Insight → Urgency → Breakup.

**TOUCH 1: The Trigger Hook (Day 1 — Email)**

Subject line formula options (choose the most relevant trigger):
- Insight hook: "[Exec's company] + [specific observation from trigger research]"
- Question hook: "[Provocative question about their stated priority]"
- Data hook: "[Benchmark stat that reframes their problem]"
- Peer hook: "[Name of similar executive] told us [insight] — curious if you're seeing the same"

Email structure:
Line 1 (THEM): Reference their specific trigger signal — a board initiative, a recent post, a regulatory challenge, a company milestone. Show you did your homework.

Line 2 (INSIGHT): Share one counter-intuitive data point or perspective related to that trigger. This is NOT a pitch — it's intellectual provocation. Example: "Most CFOs we talk to are cutting SaaS spend by 18-22% — but the ones growing fastest are actually increasing spend in [specific category] because the ROI compounds. Counterintuitive."

Line 3 (BRIDGE): One sentence connecting the insight to a question or observation about their situation. "Given [their initiative], I imagine [challenge] is on your radar."

Line 4 (MICRO-CTA): A question that takes 10 seconds to answer. "Is this something your team is navigating right now — yes/no?" Or: "Worth a 15-minute exchange to share what [peer title] at [similar company] did about this?"

Line 5 (Signature): Name, title, company. No social proof logos. No bullet points. No attachments.

**TOUCH 2: The LinkedIn Angle (Day 4 — LinkedIn DM or InMail)**

Approach: Different angle from the email. If email led with company data, LinkedIn leads with their personal professional interest or a recent post they wrote.

Structure:
Sentence 1: Reference something they posted, commented on, or a topic they've spoken about publicly. "Saw your post on [topic] — you made a point about [specific idea] that I keep thinking about."

Sentence 2: Add one sentence of specific, non-obvious value. A data point. A contrarian take. A pattern you've seen.

Sentence 3: Soft door-open. "Happy to share the full picture if it's useful — or not if the timing's wrong." 

[No explicit CTA to book a meeting]

**TOUCH 3: The Value Drop (Day 8 — Email)**

Objective: Deliver standalone value with no pitch. Make them feel like they received something worth their time even if they never buy.

Format options:
- **The Benchmark Gift:** "I put together a 1-pager on how 47 [their title]s at companies like [their company] are approaching [their priority]. Happy to send it — takes 3 minutes to read."
- **The Insight Memo:** A 3-bullet summary of a pattern you've observed that's directly relevant to their challenge. Not your product — just the insight.
- **The Peer Story:** "A [same title] at [similar company] solved [specific version of their problem] by [approach]. I wrote up what worked and what didn't — it's 1 page. Want it?"

**TOUCH 4: The Soft Urgency (Day 14 — Email or LinkedIn)**

Objective: Create gentle urgency without false scarcity. Reference a real external event or deadline.

Examples:
- "We're running a [peer benchmark roundtable / executive cohort / research study] with [titles like theirs] in [timeframe] — [their peer executive] from [company] is participating. If you'd want to be included, I need to know by [date]."
- "We're finalizing our 2026 State of [their function] report and have room for [X] more [titles] to share their perspective. Takes 12 minutes. The report comes back to all participants first."

**TOUCH 5: The Breakup (Day 21 — Email)**

The breakup email often generates more replies than any previous touch because it removes the obligation to respond and signals respect for their time.

Template:

I've reached out a few times and haven't heard back — which tells me either the timing isn't right, this isn't relevant to you, or my message hasn't landed the way I intended.

## Troubleshooting

**Problem:** Touch 1 email consistently gets no replies despite good personalization.
**Solution:** The issue is almost always one of three things: (1) your trigger signal isn't strong enough — it feels like you could have sent this to anyone; (2) your CTA asks for too much — replace "book a 30-minute call" with "worth a 1-paragraph reply?"; or (3) your insight isn't actually insightful — if the executive already knows it, there's no pattern interrupt. Test a new first sentence with a different trigger source, and replace your data point with a more counter-intuitive benchmark.

**Problem:** Executives reply to the breakup email but not the earlier touches.
**Solution:** This tells you your earlier messaging was too salesy. The breakup email works because it removes pressure — replicate that tone across the entire sequence. Rewrite Touches 1–3 to remove all urgency language, all product references, and all CTAs except questions. Make every touch feel like you're giving, not asking.

**Problem:** Multi-threading creates awkward situations where multiple executives mention your outreach to each other.
**Solution:** This is actually a positive signal — you're getting attention. Brief your AE immediately. Send a response to the executive who raised it: "Good — [colleague's name] and I connected briefly as well. This tells me [their challenge] might be more of a priority than I realized. When's a good time to align everyone together?" Turn the coordination into a champion-building moment.
