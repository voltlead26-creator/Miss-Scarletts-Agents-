**Part 4: Share of Voice → Share of Market Model (Ehrenberg-Bass)**

The fundamental brand economics equation for B2B:
- Share of Voice (SOV) = your media spend / total category media spend
- Share of Market (SOM) = your revenue / total addressable market revenue
- Rule: Companies with SOV > SOM grow; companies with SOV < SOM decline (Excess Share of Voice = SOV – SOM)

For B2B SaaS, calculate:
1. Your estimated SOV: (Your brand + performance spend) / (Your spend + top 3 competitor spend estimates)
   - Use SimilarWeb for traffic estimates, SEMrush for keyword spend estimates, LinkedIn Ad Library for volume proxies
   - This is approximate — a directional signal, not an audit
2. Your SOM: (Your ARR / analyst-estimated category TAM)
3. Excess Share of Voice (ESOV): SOV% – SOM%
   - ESOV > 0: You are investing ahead of your market position → expect share growth
   - ESOV = 0: Spending to defend current position → expect flat share
   - ESOV < 0: Underspending vs. market position → at risk of share decline

4. ESOV-to-Growth Equation (IPA research-derived): Each +10 points of ESOV = approximately +0.5% market share gain per year for B2B
   - Apply to your numbers: If your ESOV is +15%, project +0.75% market share gain this year
   - At your current ARR: +0.75% SOM × TAM = $[X] in incremental ARR opportunity from brand investment

## Troubleshooting

**Problem: "We only have 12 months of data — the lag-correlation analysis isn't statistically significant"**
Solution: With only 12 months (12 data points), you have insufficient statistical power for meaningful cross-lagged analysis — any correlation is likely noise. Two approaches: (1) Source historical data — pull 2–3 years of brand spend from accounting/finance, and CRM pipeline from HubSpot/Salesforce going back further; even rough monthly buckets are sufficient for correlation analysis. (2) Use a proxy leading indicator instead — run a Share of Search analysis (always available historically via Google Trends) and show the directional correlation between brand investment periods and subsequent SOV increases. A rising SOV trend, even without the full lag model, is a defensible CFO argument that brand investment is working. Flag the data limitation clearly and commit to a full 24-month dataset by next quarter.

**Problem: "Our CFO says the lag-correlation doesn't prove causation — 'maybe pipeline growth caused brand spend increases, not the other way around'"**
Solution: This is the reverse-causality objection and it is valid — correlation alone cannot prove direction. Counter with three arguments: (1) Run a formal Granger Causality test (available in Python's statsmodels library with 5 lines of code) — this tests whether lagged brand spend values predict future pipeline values AFTER controlling for past pipeline autocorrelation. If brand Granger-causes pipeline (p < 0.05) but pipeline does NOT Granger-cause brand, you have a defensible statistical argument for direction. (2) Point to the mechanism: Show specific brand campaigns and their timing relative to subsequent pipeline spikes — qualitative case studies alongside the quantitative model. (3) Reference IPA's 1,400-company database showing that SOV > SOM companies consistently outgrow SOV < SOM companies — the causal direction has been established across thousands of companies; your data is consistent with this established finding.

**Problem: "Our Share of Search is flat even though we've been investing in brand for 18 months — is brand spend not working?"**
Solution: Flat Share of Search despite investment usually means one of three things: (1) Competitors are increasing spend at the same rate — you're running to stay still. Pull competitor branded search trends in Google Trends alongside yours to diagnose. If all category players are growing at similar rates, SOV is stable by construction. (2) Your brand investment is in channels that don't generate search behavior — CISO roundtables and PR don't directly produce Google searches in the short term; they build mental availability that shows up as direct traffic and word-of-mouth referral first. Check your direct traffic trend and unattributed pipeline — these are the more appropriate metrics for offline brand channels. (3) 18 months may not be enough — IPA research shows brand effects typically take 2–3 years to fully compound for B2B enterprise. Reframe with leadership: you are 60% of the way through the minimum effective investment horizon; cutting now destroys the compounding that is about to pay off.
